BIDEN'S 2020 VICTORY AND THE MULTILATERAL REVERSAL: RESTORING AMERICA'S ALLIANCES

ELECTORAL CONTEXT AND THE MEANING OF 2020

Joe Biden's defeat of Donald Trump in the November 2020 US presidential election carried explicit foreign policy significance from the moment he declared his candidacy. Biden framed his entire campaign around the proposition that America's global standing had been damaged and needed restoration. His slogan "America is back" was as much a foreign policy manifesto as a campaign tagline. Biden won 306 electoral votes against Trump's 232, and secured over 81 million popular votes, the highest total in US history at the time.

The election was watched with unusual intensity by allied governments, particularly in Europe and East Asia, which had spent four years navigating an erratic American partner. The prospect of Biden returning US foreign policy to a multilateral, alliance-centric orientation was greeted with relief in Brussels, Berlin, Paris, Seoul, and Tokyo.

IMMEDIATE FOREIGN POLICY REVERSALS

Biden moved with deliberate speed to signal the multilateral turn. Within hours of taking office on January 20, 2021, his administration rejoined the Paris Climate Agreement, reversed the US withdrawal from the World Health Organization, and began the process of reaffirming NATO commitments. Secretary of State Antony Blinken visited Brussels within weeks, delivering an unequivocal reaffirmation of Article Five mutual defense obligations that Trump had repeatedly questioned.

The contrast with Trump's "America First" posture was stark and intentional. Biden restored funding to international organizations, re-engaged with the UN Human Rights Council, and relaunched the Iran nuclear diplomacy through the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) framework, though negotiations proved protracted and ultimately inconclusive.

NATO AND THE RUSSIA CHALLENGE

Biden's multilateralism was tested almost immediately by Russian assertiveness. In April 2021, Russia massed tens of thousands of troops on Ukraine's borders in what appeared to be a rehearsal for potential military action. Biden responded by coordinating closely with European allies, imposing sanctions on Russia, and conducting high-level diplomacy with Putin. The Biden team's approach deliberately embedded US responses within a multilateral framework, seeking allied consensus rather than acting unilaterally.

When Russia launched its full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, the Biden administration's alliance management was put to its most significant test. Biden successfully organized an unprecedented Western response: coordinated weapons deliveries to Ukraine, sweeping financial sanctions against Russia including the freezing of Russian central bank assets, and sustained diplomatic cohesion across NATO's 30-plus members. The response demonstrated the practical utility of the multilateral approach Biden had promised. NATO unity held through multiple rounds of escalation, and the alliance expanded to include Finland and Sweden, a dramatic geopolitical shift triggered by Russian aggression.

CHINA POLICY: COMPETITION WITH GUARDRAILS

Biden's China policy represented a more nuanced shift. Rather than abandoning Trump's competitive posture, Biden sought to embed rivalry within a multilateral framework. The administration maintained most Trump-era tariffs, expanded export controls on advanced semiconductors, and organized the Quad grouping of the US, Japan, Australia, and India as an informal balancing coalition. However, Biden also pursued selective engagement, maintaining lines of communication with Beijing and seeking cooperation on climate change and pandemic preparedness.

LIMITATIONS AND LEGACY

Biden's multilateral reversal had genuine limits. The chaotic August 2021 withdrawal from Afghanistan damaged US credibility with allies despite Biden's multilateral commitments in other areas. The AUKUS submarine partnership with Australia and the UK, announced in 2021, antagonized France which lost a major submarine contract with Canberra, creating an unexpected allied friction. Domestic political constraints also limited Biden's ability to ratify new multilateral treaties or rejoin international institutions that required Senate approval.

Despite these complications, Biden's 2020 election and the foreign policy that followed represented a genuine restoration of the alliance-centric model of American statecraft. The 2024 election would test whether that model had durable domestic political support.
