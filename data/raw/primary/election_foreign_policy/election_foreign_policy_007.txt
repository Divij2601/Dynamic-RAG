GERMANY 2025: ELECTION, MIGRATION CRISIS, AND FOREIGN POLICY RECALIBRATION

ELECTORAL CONTEXT: THE COLLAPSE OF THE TRAFFIC LIGHT

Germany's snap federal election in February 2025 was triggered by the collapse of the Olaf Scholz "traffic light" coalition in November 2024, when the Free Democrats withdrew over irreconcilable differences on fiscal and economic policy. The election produced a result that reshaped German domestic and foreign policy. The center-right CDU/CSU under Friedrich Merz won approximately 28.5 percent of the vote, positioning Merz to lead a coalition government. The Alternative for Germany (AfD) achieved its best-ever national result at around 20 percent, becoming the second-largest party and forcing mainstream parties to negotiate coalition arithmetic that excluded far-right participation.

Merz formed a CDU/CSU-SPD grand coalition, returning to the familiar but often cumbersome German governing formula. The new government took office amid Europe's most significant migration debate since 2015-2016 and with the Ukraine war entering its fourth year.

MIGRATION AS FOREIGN POLICY DRIVER

Migration became the defining domestic issue of the 2025 campaign, and its foreign policy dimensions were substantial. Merz ran on a platform of sharply restricting irregular migration, which translated into several foreign policy imperatives. First, Germany pushed forcefully within the EU for a much harder external border regime and the expansion of processing centers in third countries, taking positions closer to Hungary and Italy than to traditional German preferences. Second, Berlin pursued intensified partnerships with transit countries including Turkey, Tunisia, Morocco, Libya, and Western Balkan states, offering trade, visa, and development incentives in exchange for migration cooperation agreements.

Third, and most controversially, Germany under Merz accelerated discussions about "safe country" designations that would allow expedited returns to countries like Syria as the Assad regime's collapse in late 2024 created new questions about conditions on the ground, and to Afghanistan under Taliban governance, which human rights organizations vigorously contested.

UKRAINE POLICY UNDER MERZ

Friedrich Merz had established himself during the Scholz years as one of the most outspoken critics of insufficient German support for Ukraine. His election signaled a hardening of Germany's posture. The Merz government increased weapons deliveries, supported extending EU financial assistance to Kyiv, and maintained a firm stance against any peace settlement that would require Ukraine to cede territory. Merz also took a notably firmer line on Russia sanctions than his predecessor, endorsing proposals to use frozen Russian assets more aggressively to fund Ukrainian reconstruction.

This more assertive Ukrainian support created an important dynamic within NATO and the EU as the Trump administration in Washington pulled back from its commitments, making German leadership on European security more central than at any point since World War II.

RELATIONS WITH THE UNITED STATES AND CHINA

Merz navigated the Trump return with pragmatic realism. Germany accelerated its own defense spending commitments to demonstrate reliability within NATO and reduce US leverage. Merz also signaled a harder line on China trade dependencies than Scholz, supporting tighter semiconductor export controls and pushing for more aggressive EU reciprocal tariffs in response to Chinese industrial subsidies.

Germany's relationship with China remained complicated by industrial interests. The automotive sector, facing existential competition from Chinese electric vehicle manufacturers, simultaneously wanted protection from Chinese imports and access to the Chinese market. The Merz government attempted to hold these tensions in balance through a differentiated sectoral approach that pleased no one entirely but avoided the stark choices of outright decoupling.

FAR-RIGHT PRESSURE AND DEMOCRATIC RESILIENCE

The AfD's strong showing created persistent pressure on the Merz government from the right on migration and Russia policy. Merz had explicitly ruled out governing with the AfD, maintaining the democratic firewall, but the AfD's legislative strength meant it could complicate passage of legislation and dominate public discourse on the issues it prioritized. Germany's ability to maintain its Zeitenwende commitments while managing the far-right pressure became a key test of democratic resilience in Europe's largest economy.
