MEXICO 2024: SHEINBAUM'S ELECTION AND THE CONTINUITY OF AMLO'S FOREIGN POLICY

THE HISTORIC 2024 ELECTION

Mexico's June 2024 general election was historic on multiple fronts. Claudia Sheinbaum, the former mayor of Mexico City and climate scientist, became the first woman elected to the Mexican presidency, winning approximately 59 percent of the vote against the center-right National Action Party (PAN) candidate Xochitl Galvez's 28 percent. The landslide consolidated the Morena party's dominance of Mexican politics and gave Sheinbaum a clear mandate to continue, while also modestly updating, the foreign policy framework established by her predecessor and mentor, Andres Manuel Lopez Obrador (AMLO).

Sheinbaum's victory also gave Morena and its allies a supermajority in both chambers of the Mexican Congress for most of the legislative year, creating political conditions for substantial policy implementation, though AMLO's signature judicial reform — which proposed electing Supreme Court justices by popular vote — was already advancing before she took office in October 2024 and created significant constitutional controversy.

FOREIGN POLICY CONTINUITY: THE AMLO LEGACY

AMLO's foreign policy had been organized around several principles that Sheinbaum inherited: non-intervention in the internal affairs of other states, sovereignty, and a rejection of sanctions-based approaches to regional governance. This meant Mexico under AMLO had maintained diplomatic relations with Venezuela under Maduro, Cuba, and Nicaragua despite the authoritarian character of those governments, and had refused to join regional multilateral condemnations. AMLO had also navigated a deliberately balanced relationship with China and the United States, seeking economic benefits from nearshoring driven by US-China decoupling without formally choosing sides in the geopolitical competition.

Sheinbaum maintained these foundational principles while seeking to avoid some of AMLO's most abrasive personal confrontations with US officials and business interests. She presented a technocratic, less combative face of the same nationalist sovereigntist foreign policy.

US-MEXICO RELATIONS: THE PARAMOUNT CHALLENGE

The bilateral relationship with the United States dominated Sheinbaum's foreign policy from the outset. The Trump administration's return in January 2025 brought a dramatically more confrontational American posture. Trump imposed 25 percent tariffs on Mexican goods, citing migration and fentanyl trafficking concerns, creating an economic crisis for Mexico given its deep integration into the North American manufacturing supply chain. The tariff dispute was eventually partially resolved through negotiations in which Mexico committed to enhanced border enforcement measures, military-style operations against cartel-controlled migration routes, and accelerated extraditions of drug trafficking suspects.

The US-Mexico security relationship remained the most consequential and sensitive dimension of the bilateral dynamic. Trump's designation of Mexican cartels as foreign terrorist organizations, combined with talk of unilateral US military operations on Mexican soil, created a constitutional crisis in Mexican politics and threatened to inflame nationalist sentiment. Sheinbaum walked a difficult line between meeting enough US demands to avoid economic punishment while preserving Mexican sovereignty and domestic political legitimacy.

NEARSHORING AND ECONOMIC DIPLOMACY

Mexico's economic foreign policy was shaped by the extraordinary nearshoring investment wave driven by US-China decoupling. Multinational manufacturers, particularly in semiconductors, electronics, and automotive sectors, were relocating production from China to Mexico to serve the US market while avoiding tariff exposure. Sheinbaum's government worked to develop industrial corridors, upgrade energy and infrastructure, and negotiate investment agreements that could capture this manufacturing relocation opportunity.

The nearshoring boom created diplomatic complexity: China was simultaneously investing in Mexico to use it as a platform for US market access, while the Trump administration sought to close this potential tariff circumvention channel, creating pressure on Mexico to restrict Chinese investment flows.

REGIONAL STANDING

Within Latin America, Mexico maintained its traditional leadership role in Central America and the Caribbean while managing sometimes tense relationships with Brazil, Argentina, and the Venezuelan situation. Sheinbaum maintained AMLO's non-interventionism while engaging more actively in multilateral forums than her predecessor, signaling a subtle shift toward institutional multilateralism even as core sovereigntist principles remained intact.
