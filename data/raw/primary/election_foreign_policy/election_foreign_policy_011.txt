LULA'S RETURN: BRAZIL 2022 AND THE RESTORATION OF MULTILATERAL FOREIGN POLICY

THE 2022 ELECTION AND POLARIZED POLITICS

Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva's narrow victory over incumbent President Jair Bolsonaro in Brazil's October 2022 presidential runoff — 50.9 to 49.1 percent — was one of the tightest elections in Brazilian history, reflecting a deeply polarized society. Lula's victory ended four years of Bolsonaro's ideologically charged populist nationalism, which had aligned Brazil with a cluster of right-wing governments internationally and systematically undermined Brazil's traditional role as a multilateral bridge-builder and environmental leader.

The transition was turbulent. Bolsonaro refused to explicitly concede and his supporters stormed government buildings in Brasilia on January 8, 2023, in scenes reminiscent of the January 6 events in Washington, though the Brazilian military and courts did not support the insurrection and Lula was inaugurated successfully.

RETURN TO MULTILATERALISM

Lula's foreign policy represented a sharp reversal from Bolsonaro's posture on multiple dimensions. Lula immediately re-engaged with international environmental commitments, signaling a return to the Paris Agreement targets and relaunching the Amazon Fund, which Norway and Germany had suspended over Bolsonaro's deforestation policies. The declaration that "Brazil is back" in international climate forums echoed Biden's language and represented a genuine restoration of Brazil's historic identity as an environmental steward of the Amazon.

Lula rebuilt relations with left-leaning governments in Latin America that Bolsonaro had antagonized, including Venezuela, Cuba, Nicaragua, Argentina under Peronist leadership, and Chile under Gabriel Boric. The Lula government pursued reactivation of UNASUR (Union of South American Nations) and broader regional integration frameworks that Bolsonaro had abandoned. Brazil under Lula also rejoined Community of Latin American and Caribbean States mechanisms and worked to reestablish MERCOSUR's diplomatic credibility.

STRATEGIC POSITIONING: NON-ALIGNMENT AND BRICS

Lula adopted a conspicuously balanced international posture that defied easy categorization. On Ukraine, Brazil consistently refused to condemn Russia's invasion explicitly, abstaining in key UN votes while calling for dialogue and positioning Brazil as a potential peace mediator. Lula maintained relationships with Moscow and Beijing while also participating in G7 outreach sessions and managing a complex relationship with Washington.

Brazil's BRICS membership became a more central element of its foreign policy under Lula than under Bolsonaro, who had largely sidelined BRICS engagement. Brazil participated actively in the BRICS expansion discussions of 2023, which admitted Saudi Arabia, Iran, UAE, Ethiopia, Egypt, and Argentina (though Argentina under Milei later declined membership). Brazil held the BRICS chairmanship in 2025, using it to push a development-finance and South-South cooperation agenda that positioned Lula as a voice for the Global South.

CHINA AND THE UNITED STATES

Lula managed a delicate balance between Brazil's two most important economic partners. China was Brazil's largest trade partner, absorbing vast quantities of agricultural commodities and raw materials. Lula visited Beijing in April 2023 in a high-profile trip that emphasized economic cooperation and included controversial statements questioning why Ukraine had not yet sought a peace settlement, which drew sharp criticism from Western governments.

Relations with the United States under Biden were constructive, with the two governments sharing environmental priorities and democratic values messaging. The return of Trump complicated this, as the Trump administration's trade tariffs threatened Brazilian exports and its skepticism of multilateral environmental commitments conflicted with Lula's core agenda.

DOMESTIC CONSTRAINTS

Lula governed with a fragile congressional coalition in a politically divided country. His foreign policy ambitions were partly constrained by domestic economic challenges, including high debt levels, structural fiscal difficulties, and the political need to deliver tangible economic results. Nevertheless, Lula's third term established Brazil's return to a pragmatic, non-aligned, multilateralist foreign policy that contrasted sharply with Bolsonaro's ideological nationalism.
