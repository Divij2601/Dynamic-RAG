TRUMP'S RETURN: THE 2024 US PRESIDENTIAL ELECTION AND THE RESHAPING OF AMERICAN FOREIGN POLICY

BACKGROUND AND ELECTORAL CONTEXT

Donald Trump's victory in the November 2024 US presidential election marked a seismic return to power for a leader whose first term fundamentally disrupted the post-World War II liberal international order. Trump defeated Democratic incumbent Vice President Kamala Harris in a contest shaped by voter anxieties over inflation, immigration, and a general sense of national decline. The Republican nominee secured 312 electoral votes, winning key battleground states including Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Georgia, and Arizona. Trump also won the popular vote, becoming the first Republican to do so since 2004, underscoring a genuine mandate for his foreign policy agenda.

The election result was interpreted globally as a clear signal that the United States was prepared to reassert a transactional, sovereignty-first approach to international relations. Allied governments in Europe, East Asia, and beyond scrambled to recalibrate their diplomatic strategies before Trump's January 2025 inauguration.

AMERICA FIRST 2.0: CORE FOREIGN POLICY SHIFTS

Trump's second term foreign policy, articulated by advisers including Marco Rubio as Secretary of State and Pete Hegseth as Defense Secretary, centered on several pillars. First, a demand that NATO allies meet and exceed the two percent GDP defense spending threshold, with Trump openly questioning whether the United States would defend members failing to pay their "fair share." Second, an immediate push to end the war in Ukraine through negotiations that critics argued would reward Russian aggression, with Trump claiming he could end the conflict "in 24 hours." Third, a hardening of trade postures toward China, the European Union, Canada, and Mexico through sweeping tariff proposals.

In the first weeks of the administration, Trump signed executive orders pausing US military aid to Ukraine pending a diplomatic review, sending shockwaves through European capitals and prompting emergency meetings of European Union and NATO foreign ministers. Trump also signaled a potential drawdown of US forward-deployed forces in Europe and the Pacific unless host nations increased burden-sharing payments.

IMPACT ON ALLIANCES AND MULTILATERAL INSTITUTIONS

The Trump return accelerated Europe's reckoning with strategic autonomy. Germany, France, Poland, and the Baltic states dramatically increased defense spending pledges. The EU moved to activate its own defense industrial mechanisms, expediting the ReArm Europe initiative announced in early 2025 and committing hundreds of billions in European defense investment. NATO held emergency consultations in Brussels, with Secretary-General Mark Rutte carefully managing the alliance's public posture while privately urging Washington to maintain article five commitments.

Relations with China entered an accelerated phase of competition. Trump imposed new tariffs ranging from 60 to 100 percent on Chinese goods, triggering retaliatory measures from Beijing and prompting WTO dispute proceedings. The Taiwan Strait remained a flashpoint, with Trump's team signaling greater arms sales to Taipei while simultaneously entertaining back-channel communications with Beijing on trade.

In the Middle East, Trump's team moved quickly to rehabilitate relationships with Gulf Arab states and Israel, signaling renewed support for the Abraham Accords framework and a harder line on Iran's nuclear program.

DOMESTIC POLITICS AND FOREIGN POLICY NEXUS

Trump's foreign policy was inseparable from domestic political imperatives. Immigration enforcement, framed partly as a national security issue, drove a muscular posture toward Mexico and Central American governments, with the administration deploying military assets to the southern border and invoking wartime statutes to facilitate mass deportations. This created friction with Mexico City, whose government under President Claudia Sheinbaum protested what it termed violations of Mexican sovereignty.

The return of Trump also energized populist nationalist movements globally. Far-right parties in Europe, Latin America, and elsewhere cited Trump's victory as validation, while liberal democratic governments faced intensified pressure from domestic electorates skeptical of multilateral commitments.

CONCLUSION

Trump's 2024 election victory confirmed that the 2016 result was not an aberration but the expression of a durable strand of American political sentiment. The foreign policy consequences are profound: a weakened multilateral order, accelerated European defense autonomy, a US-China rivalry stripped of diplomatic pretense, and an international system increasingly organized around bilateral bargains rather than rules-based institutions. How durable these shifts prove beyond Trump's second term remains the central question for global order in the late 2020s.
