FRANCE 2027: THE LOOMING FAR-RIGHT CHALLENGE AND POTENTIAL FOREIGN POLICY TRANSFORMATION

POLITICAL LANDSCAPE HEADING INTO 2027

France's 2027 presidential election is shaping up as one of the most consequential votes in the Fifth Republic's history. With Emmanuel Macron constitutionally barred from seeking a third term, the political center faces its most serious challenge from the far-right Rassemblement National (RN) since the party's founding. Polling in 2025 and early 2026 consistently placed RN candidates, particularly party leader Jordan Bardella and veteran Marine Le Pen (pending her eligibility following a 2024 embezzlement conviction), near the top of first-round preference surveys, with the traditional center-right Les Republicains fragmented and the left divided among multiple contenders.

The question of whether a far-right government would actually assume power hinges on France's two-round electoral system, which has historically produced a "republican front" coalition in the second round to block RN candidates. However, analysts noted in 2025-2026 that this firewall showed signs of erosion as voter fatigue with mainstream parties deepened.

WHAT A FAR-RIGHT VICTORY WOULD MEAN FOR EU POLICY

A Rassemblement National presidency would represent a fundamental challenge to the EU project from within its second most powerful member state. The RN has substantially moderated its official position on EU membership, abandoning its earlier "Frexit" ambitions, but its program retains elements deeply incompatible with EU rules: proposals to restore primacy of French law over EU law in certain domains, demands to renegotiate EU treaties to reduce the scope of free movement, and resistance to further integration of EU defense and fiscal policy.

In practical terms, a RN presidency would likely pursue a blocking strategy within EU institutions similar to Hungary's Viktor Orban, using France's considerable institutional weight to veto deepening integration, obstruct EU sanctions on Russia, and challenge the European Commission's authority over migration and border policy. Unlike Hungary, France holds a permanent seat on the UN Security Council and is a nuclear power, giving such obstruction vastly greater strategic consequences.

NATO AND THE RUSSIA QUESTION

The most alarming foreign policy implications of a potential far-right French government concern NATO and the Russia relationship. Le Pen and Bardella have historically maintained warm relationships with Moscow, with the RN's 2014 loan from a Russian-linked bank remaining a point of sustained scrutiny. While the RN formally endorsed Ukraine's right to sovereignty after the 2022 invasion, it consistently opposed weapons deliveries that might "escalate" the conflict and called for early negotiations on terms critics argued would favor Russia.

A French president pursuing RN foreign policy would likely seek an early end to Western sanctions on Russia, reduce or eliminate French military support to Ukraine, and push for a normalized relationship with Moscow. Within NATO, France could not unilaterally block Article Five commitments, but it could significantly undermine operational planning, intelligence sharing, and the political cohesion that sustains allied deterrence.

TRANSATLANTIC AND GLOBAL IMPLICATIONS

A far-right French government would profoundly alter the transatlantic political landscape. Combined with a Trump administration in Washington, it could effectively suspend the Western alliance's collective commitment to the rules-based international order, creating a vacuum that China, Russia, and other revisionist powers would move to fill. For smaller EU members, particularly in Central and Eastern Europe, the prospect of France abandoning its role as a guarantor of European security would be deeply alarming.

Conversely, a far-right French government might paradoxically accelerate integration among the remaining committed EU members. Germany, Poland, the Nordics, and the Benelux states might forge a tighter inner core of European security cooperation, effectively creating a multi-speed EU with France temporarily sidelined from its traditional role as co-architect of European integration.

ELECTORAL UNCERTAINTIES

As of 2026, the 2027 outcome remains genuinely uncertain. The republican front dynamic, the specifics of the candidate lineup, and events between now and the election could all alter the trajectory significantly. What is certain is that France's 2027 election will be among the most consequential votes for the future of European order.
