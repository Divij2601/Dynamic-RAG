STARMER'S LABOUR VICTORY 2024: CONTINUITY, RECALIBRATION, AND UK FOREIGN POLICY

THE 2024 UK GENERAL ELECTION

The United Kingdom's July 2024 general election delivered a landslide victory for Keir Starmer's Labour Party, ending fourteen years of Conservative government. Labour won 412 seats in the House of Commons on approximately 34 percent of the vote, a commanding parliamentary majority produced by a collapse in Conservative support and the fragmentation of the right-wing vote between the Tories and Nigel Farage's Reform UK party. The result was a political earthquake in domestic terms but, unusually, was accompanied by significant continuity rather than discontinuity in UK foreign policy.

Starmer had carefully shaped Labour's foreign policy platform to reassure voters that a Labour government would not repeat the perceived weaknesses of Jeremy Corbyn's leadership on national security, defense, and Britain's alliances. The manifesto committed to maintaining the Trident nuclear deterrent, meeting NATO's two percent spending commitment, and sustaining support for Ukraine.

FOREIGN POLICY CONTINUITY: UKRAINE, NATO, AND THE SPECIAL RELATIONSHIP

In the crucial area of Ukraine support, the Starmer government maintained and deepened the commitment established by its Conservative predecessors. The UK had been one of the most active supporters of Ukraine under both Johnson and Sunak, providing significant military assistance including Storm Shadow cruise missiles, armored vehicles, and air defense systems. Starmer continued and expanded this support, working closely with European partners to sustain a coordinated Western response.

On NATO, Starmer reaffirmed British commitments with particular emphasis on the Alliance's eastern flank, maintaining the UK's Enhanced Forward Presence contributions in Estonia and Poland. The new government navigated the Trump return to Washington with careful pragmatism, maintaining the "special relationship" framework while emphasizing the UK's European partnerships more explicitly than its Conservative predecessors, though this balance required constant management given the sensitivities on both sides.

EUROPEAN RESET: POST-BREXIT RECALIBRATION

The most significant foreign policy shift under Starmer was a deliberate warming of UK-EU relations within the constraints of Brexit's red lines. Starmer's government pursued what it termed a "reset" of the UK-EU relationship, seeking to reduce frictions in trade, security cooperation, and practical governance without reopening the fundamental questions of single market or customs union membership. A UK-EU Summit in May 2025 produced agreements on defense cooperation frameworks, youth mobility schemes, and closer coordination on migration and border security.

The reset was welcomed in Brussels as a pragmatic step and in key European capitals, particularly Paris and Berlin, which had found post-Brexit UK-EU friction strategically costly. The Conservatives criticized it as "Brexit in reverse," though the red lines on free movement and EU court jurisdiction remained formally intact.

DEFENSE INVESTMENT AND INDUSTRIAL STRATEGY

Starmer's government faced acute pressure to increase defense spending beyond two percent of GDP in response to the deteriorating European security environment. Chancellor Rachel Reeves' fiscal constraints made this difficult, but the government committed to a path toward 2.5 percent with a planned defense spending review. The UK announced significant investments in its defense industrial base, including shipbuilding, munitions manufacturing, and next-generation weapons programs under the AUKUS framework with the US and Australia.

GLOBAL BRITAIN IN PRACTICE

Labour inherited and adapted the Conservative "Global Britain" concept with more multilateral and development-focused content. The government restored UK foreign aid spending toward the UN target of 0.7 percent of gross national income, reversing Conservative cuts, and re-engaged with multilateral institutions on climate, health, and global governance. The Commonwealth remained an institutional focus though with more realistic expectations about its strategic utility.

Starmer's foreign policy represented disciplined continuity on core security commitments with selective modernization of the UK's international posture, a pragmatic recalibration rather than a transformative break.
