MODI'S THIRD TERM: INDIA 2024 ELECTION AND CONTINUING STRATEGIC AUTONOMY

THE 2024 GENERAL ELECTION OUTCOME

India's April-June 2024 general election, the world's largest democratic exercise with nearly a billion eligible voters, produced a result that was simultaneously a victory and a setback for Narendra Modi and the Bharatiya Janata Party. The BJP won approximately 240 seats in the 543-seat Lok Sabha, falling short of the 272 needed for an outright majority and significantly below the 303 seats won in 2019. Modi was able to form a government only with coalition partners in the National Democratic Alliance, principally the Telugu Desam Party and Janata Dal (United), giving regional parties leverage they had not possessed in the previous two terms.

Modi was sworn in for a third consecutive term as Prime Minister in June 2024, but the coalition arithmetic constrained his domestic agenda more than his foreign policy one. India's foreign policy, pursued under Modi's direct personal supervision, continued along established lines with relatively little disruption from the coalition dynamics.

STRATEGIC AUTONOMY: THE ORGANIZING PRINCIPLE

India's foreign policy under Modi's third term maintained the defining principle of strategic autonomy — the pursuit of India's national interest through multiple partnerships without exclusive alignment with any single power bloc. This manifested in India's sustained refusal to condemn Russia's invasion of Ukraine by name in UN votes, continued purchase of discounted Russian oil despite Western pressure, participation in the Quad security dialogue with the US, Japan, and Australia, and maintenance of border tension management with China following the 2020 Galwan clashes.

India deepened its defense partnership with the United States through agreements on industrial cooperation, technology transfer, and co-production of military equipment, including a landmark GE jet engine manufacturing deal for Indian combat aircraft. At the same time, India maintained approximately 60 percent reliance on Russian defense equipment and refused to abandon this relationship at Western urging.

CHINA POLICY: DISENGAGEMENT AND COMPETITION

The China relationship remained India's most complex foreign policy challenge. Following the June 2020 Galwan Valley clash in which twenty Indian soldiers died, the two sides undertook a prolonged military disengagement process along the Line of Actual Control in Ladakh. Disengagement at the most contested patrol points was completed by late 2024, and foreign ministers from both sides exchanged visits to manage the relationship's stabilization. However, the underlying strategic competition remained intense, with India viewing Chinese infrastructure and investment expansion in South Asia, the Indian Ocean, and Southeast Asia as a systematic encirclement effort requiring active diplomatic counter-pressure.

GLOBAL SOUTH LEADERSHIP

Modi's third term coincided with India's growing claim to leadership of the Global South, a diplomatic project that received enormous boost from India's successful G20 presidency in 2023. India leveraged its standing to position itself as a voice for developing nations on debt restructuring, climate finance, and multilateral reform, giving New Delhi influence with African, Latin American, and Southeast Asian governments that complemented its great power partnerships.

India's application to join BRICS Plus, its leadership of the G20 development agenda, and its active diplomacy in the Gulf and Africa reflected an ambitious foreign policy punching increasingly at its growing economic weight. India became the world's fifth-largest economy during this period, providing material foundation for its diplomatic ambitions.

NEIGHBORHOOD CHALLENGES

The third-term Modi government faced significant challenges in India's immediate neighborhood. Bangladesh experienced a political upheaval in August 2024 when Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina resigned amid mass protests and fled to India, complicating India-Bangladesh relations. Relations with Pakistan remained frozen, with no diplomatic breakthrough in sight. Sri Lanka and Nepal required delicate management of competing Chinese and Indian influence. The Maldives elected a government in 2023 that initially took a more pro-China posture, though practical realities constrained full reorientation.

India's third-term foreign policy reflected the ambitions of a rising power determined to shape its environment rather than simply react to it, while managing the complexities of neighborhood relations and great power triangles with considerable strategic sophistication.
