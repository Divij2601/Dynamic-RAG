ERDOGAN RE-ELECTED 2023: TURKEY'S STRATEGIC BALANCING ACT

THE 2023 ELECTION AND ITS SIGNIFICANCE

Turkey's May 2023 presidential and parliamentary elections were among the most closely watched in the country's history. President Recep Tayyip Erdogan of the Justice and Development Party (AKP), governing since 2003, faced his most serious electoral challenge from a broad opposition coalition led by Republican People's Party (CHP) candidate Kemal Kilicdaroglu. The first round on May 14 produced no outright winner, with Erdogan taking 49.5 percent and Kilicdaroglu 44.9 percent. The runoff on May 28 saw Erdogan prevail with 52.2 percent, securing a new five-year term and preserving the AKP-dominated parliamentary majority needed to govern.

The election occurred under exceptionally difficult circumstances: the February 2023 earthquake in southeastern Turkey killed over 50,000 people and devastated the region, initially threatening Erdogan politically before the government's reconstruction response partly reversed the damage. The economic context was also challenging, with inflation running at over 80 percent in late 2022 before a policy pivot toward more orthodox monetary policy in mid-2023.

FOREIGN POLICY OF STRATEGIC BALANCING

Erdogan's re-election confirmed the continuation of Turkey's signature foreign policy of strategic balancing between competing great powers. Turkey is a NATO member with a complex and sometimes contradictory set of relationships: simultaneously a US treaty ally, a purchaser of Russian S-400 air defense systems (which led to Turkey's exclusion from the F-35 program), a mediator between Russia and Ukraine, a participant in the Astana process on Syria alongside Russia and Iran, and a major regional power with significant influence in the Balkans, Central Asia, the Middle East, and Africa.

On Ukraine, Erdogan had maintained a uniquely valuable position. Turkey refused to join Western sanctions on Russia, maintaining economic relationships that provided Russia with some measure of relief from isolation, while simultaneously selling Bayraktar TB2 drones to Ukraine that proved highly effective in the early phases of the conflict. Turkey's Bosphorus control under the Montreux Convention gave Ankara leverage to close the straits to warships of belligerent nations, limiting Russian naval expansion in the Black Sea. Turkey also hosted and co-facilitated the Black Sea Grain Initiative in 2022, allowing Ukrainian grain exports to resume for a period.

NATO TENSIONS AND THE SWEDEN/FINLAND ACCESSION

Erdogan used Sweden and Finland's NATO membership applications as leverage to extract concessions from NATO members. Turkey initially blocked both applications, demanding that Sweden and Finland extradite individuals Turkey designated as terrorists, including Kurdish PKK-affiliated activists and individuals involved in the 2016 coup attempt. After extended negotiations, Sweden made legislative changes to its counterterrorism law and both countries were eventually admitted: Finland in April 2023 and Sweden in March 2024. The episode demonstrated Erdogan's skill at extracting maximum value from Turkey's strategic position.

REGIONAL ACTIVISM: AFRICA, CAUCASUS, AND THE MIDDLE EAST

Turkey under Erdogan's fifth election cycle continued its activist regional foreign policy. Turkish military deployments in Libya and Somalia were maintained. The mediation and military support role in Azerbaijan's 2020 victory over Armenia in Nagorno-Karabakh solidified Turkey's influence in the South Caucasus. Turkish-backed Syrian opposition forces continued to occupy areas of northern Syria. Turkey expanded its African diplomatic and military presence through embassy openings and defense cooperation agreements.

On Israel-Gaza following the October 7, 2023 Hamas attack, Turkey took one of the most vocally pro-Palestinian positions among NATO members, with Erdogan condemning Israeli military operations in terms that led to a temporary suspension of Turkish-Israeli trade relations.

ECONOMIC DIPLOMACY AND THE PIVOT TO ORTHODOX POLICY

Erdogan's post-election economic policy pivot — appointing Mehmet Simsek as finance minister and Hafize Gaye Erkan as central bank governor, implementing interest rate increases — had foreign policy implications, signaling Turkey's desire to restore credibility with international financial markets and reduce its vulnerability to currency crises that had constrained its foreign policy capacity.
