GERMANY'S ZEITENWENDE: THE 2021 SCHOLZ COALITION AND THE TRANSFORMATION OF GERMAN DEFENSE POLICY

THE 2021 ELECTION AND COALITION FORMATION

Germany's September 2021 federal election produced a fractured result that ultimately brought Olaf Scholz of the Social Democrats (SPD) to the chancellorship in a three-party "traffic light" coalition with the Greens and the Free Democrats (FDP). The SPD won 25.7 percent of the vote, a narrow plurality over the CDU/CSU at 24.1 percent, ending sixteen years of Angela Merkel's chancellorship. Coalition negotiations produced a government that combined SPD foreign policy caution, Green human rights commitments, and FDP economic liberalism — a combination that critics initially argued would produce foreign policy paralysis.

The new government's early foreign policy was indeed cautious. Germany's reluctance to supply lethal weapons to Ukraine and its insistence on keeping open the option of dialogue with Russia drew sharp criticism from Eastern European allies and US officials in the weeks before the February 2022 Russian invasion.

ZEITENWENDE: THE TURNING POINT

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022 shattered the premises of German foreign policy with a suddenness that shocked even veteran observers. Three days later, on February 27, Scholz delivered his landmark Bundestag speech announcing the Zeitenwende, or historic turning point, in German foreign and security policy. The speech was one of the most consequential statements by a German leader since reunification.

Scholz announced a one-time special fund of 100 billion euros for the Bundeswehr to modernize Germany's chronically underfunded military. He committed Germany to meeting and sustaining the NATO two percent GDP defense spending target. He announced a complete reversal on weapons deliveries, with Germany ultimately supplying Ukraine with Leopard 2 tanks, Gepard air defense systems, Patriot batteries, and other heavy weapons after overcoming significant internal coalition resistance. He announced the end of the Nord Stream 2 pipeline project, Germany's flagship energy relationship with Russia.

STRUCTURAL FOREIGN POLICY SHIFTS

The Zeitenwende represented more than a policy adjustment; it signaled a fundamental rethinking of Germany's postwar strategic culture. Germany had built its postwar identity around "Wandel durch Handel" — change through trade — the proposition that economic engagement would moderate authoritarian behavior. The invasion of Ukraine demonstrated the failure of that proposition in the Russian case.

Germany moved to diversify its energy sources away from Russian gas with extraordinary speed, building floating LNG terminals in record time, signing long-term LNG supply agreements with the United States, Qatar, and Norway, and dramatically accelerating its renewable energy buildout. By late 2023, Germany had essentially eliminated its dependence on Russian gas, a transformation that analysts had previously estimated would take a decade.

On China, Germany moved more cautiously. German industry's deep integration with the Chinese market, particularly in the automotive sector, made a rapid decoupling politically and economically painful. The Scholz government adopted a nuanced "de-risking" language borrowed from European Commission framing, seeking to reduce dependencies in critical sectors while maintaining trade relationships. Scholz visited Beijing in late 2022, drawing criticism for appearing to normalize relations prematurely, though German policy gradually hardened on Huawei exclusion and investment screening.

COALITION TENSIONS AND LIMITS

The traffic light coalition's foreign policy was marked by sustained internal tension. The Greens, with Foreign Minister Annalena Baerbock, pushed for a more values-based, human rights-centered approach. The SPD's old guard maintained reservations about confrontational postures toward Russia and China. The FDP prioritized trade and economic interests. These tensions produced sometimes contradictory signals and delayed decisions on weapons deliveries to Ukraine.

The coalition collapsed in November 2024 over budget disputes, triggering early elections in early 2025. Despite its difficulties, the Scholz era's most durable legacy was the Zeitenwende itself — the structural reorientation of German strategic culture toward greater defense investment, harder security commitments, and a more skeptical approach to engagement with authoritarian powers.
