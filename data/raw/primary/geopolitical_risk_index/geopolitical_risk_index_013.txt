SOMALIA FAILED STATE GOVERNANCE RISK

OVERVIEW

Somalia has been a reference case for state failure since the collapse of the Siad Barre government in 1991, triggering decades of civil war, famine, international intervention, and persistent armed non-state actor governance. In 2025, Somalia's federal government under President Hassan Sheikh Mohamud continues to exercise limited authority, concentrated primarily in Mogadishu and select urban centers, while Al-Shabaab controls or contests approximately 20-30 percent of Somali territory and extracts taxation and governance functions across a much wider area. The African Union Transition Mission in Somalia (ATMIS), scheduled to transfer security responsibility to Somali forces by end of 2024, has faced challenges with Somali National Army capacity. Somalia ranks near the bottom of global governance indices and near the top of fragile state risk indices.

AL-SHABAAB: INSURGENCY AND GOVERNANCE

Al-Shabaab (Harakat al-Shabaab al-Mujahideen), an Al-Qaeda affiliate, represents the defining security threat in Somalia. Originating from the Islamic Courts Union defeated by Ethiopian intervention in 2006, Al-Shabaab has demonstrated extraordinary resilience, surviving major military offensives by AMISOM, U.S. drone strikes (including the killing of multiple leaders), and clan militia pressure. Al-Shabaab controls significant rural territory in south-central Somalia, maintains a shadow governance system collecting "zakat" taxation from businesses and villages, and conducts regular high-casualty attacks including car bombings in Mogadishu, attacks on African Union and Somali National Army bases, and operations in Kenya and Ethiopia. The 2022-2023 offensive against Al-Shabaab by clan militias (Macawisley) and Somali forces achieved significant initial gains but has faced Al-Shabaab counterattacks that partially reversed these gains.

HUMANITARIAN CRISIS AND CLIMATE VULNERABILITY

Somalia is among the most climate-vulnerable countries in the world. Prolonged drought from 2021-2023 — the worst in 40 years according to the UN — pushed over 6 million Somalis into acute food insecurity and triggered famine conditions in multiple regions. The subsequent El Nino-associated flooding in late 2023-2024 displaced hundreds of thousands more. Climate-conflict nexus dynamics are well-documented in Somalia: drought reduces pastoralist and agricultural livelihoods, driving recruitment into Al-Shabaab and inter-clan conflict over scarce resources. The UN Humanitarian Response Plan for Somalia remains chronically underfunded, with needs consistently exceeding pledged international support.

FEDERAL GOVERNANCE STRUCTURE AND POLITICAL RISK

Somalia's provisional federal constitution (2012) established a federal structure with five member states (Puntland, Hirshabelle, Jubaland, South West State, and Galmadug) alongside the Benadir regional administration for Mogadishu. The federal structure has produced persistent jurisdictional conflicts between the Federal Government of Somalia and member states over security, resource revenues (particularly offshore oil and gas), and electoral administration. Puntland declared itself an "independent" federal state in 2023, deepening the political fragmentation. Somaliland, in the northwest, has maintained a separate and relatively stable self-governing administration since 1991 and has pursued (unsuccessfully) international recognition.

PIRACY AND MARITIME SECURITY

While Somali piracy was dramatically reduced by international naval patrolling and private security escorts from its 2010-2012 peak, there has been concern about resurgence. Renewed piracy incidents in the western Indian Ocean from late 2023 raised alarms about whether deteriorating onshore conditions would drive a return to maritime crime. The correlation between onshore state weakness, youth unemployment, and maritime predation remains a structural risk factor. Somalia's exclusive economic zone contains potentially significant hydrocarbon resources, making offshore governance capacity a long-term development issue.

COMPOSITE RISK SCORE

Somalia maintains an EXTREME risk classification as a prototypical failed state with Al-Shabaab insurgency, absent central governance, chronic famine risk, and minimal prospects for meaningful stabilization in the short-to-medium term without sustained and coordinated international engagement that has historically proved difficult to maintain.
