VENEZUELA POLITICAL ECONOMIC RISK COMPOSITE

OVERVIEW

Venezuela's geopolitical risk profile in 2025 reflects a state that has undergone one of the most dramatic economic collapses in modern history while maintaining authoritarian political control under Nicolas Maduro. The combination of hyperinflation, oil industry destruction, mass emigration, political repression, and international isolation places Venezuela among the highest-risk countries in Latin America. The July 2024 presidential election, which Maduro claimed to have won despite widespread evidence of fraud and the opposition's credible documentation of a Edmundo González Urrutia victory, triggered fresh international isolation and domestic repression while Maduro consolidated power for another six-year term.

POLITICAL REPRESSION AND AUTHORITARIAN CONSOLIDATION

Nicolas Maduro has maintained power through a combination of security apparatus coercion, patronage networks, and foreign support (principally from Cuba, Russia, China, and Iran). The Bolivarian National Intelligence Service (SEBIN) and pro-government paramilitary groups (colectivos) suppress political opposition through arbitrary detention, torture, and targeted killing. The UN Fact-Finding Mission on Venezuela documented crimes against humanity including extrajudicial executions, arbitrary detention, and systematic torture, attributing these acts directly to state security forces and pro-government groups. Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International maintain extensive documentation of Venezuelan political prisoner cases. The political opposition remains fragmented and externally displaced, with key figures in exile or imprisoned.

ECONOMIC COLLAPSE AND PARTIAL RECOVERY

Venezuela experienced the most severe peacetime economic collapse recorded in modern history between 2013 and 2021. GDP contracted by approximately 75 percent cumulatively. Hyperinflation peaked at over one million percent in 2018. The bolivar underwent multiple currency reforms and remains chronically unstable. However, partial dollarization (informal and then semi-official acceptance of U.S. dollars and Colombian pesos) stabilized transactions from 2020 onward. The oil industry, nationalized under Hugo Chavez and then mismanaged and under-invested, collapsed from producing 3 million barrels per day at peak to under 700,000 barrels per day by 2020, before recovering partially to approximately 900,000 barrels per day in 2024. U.S. sanctions on PDVSA (Venezuela's state oil company) and Chevron's limited license to operate in Venezuela create a complex sanctions architecture. The economy has shown marginal GDP growth from 2022-2024, but the baseline is devastated.

MASS EMIGRATION AND REGIONAL DESTABILIZATION

Venezuela's migration crisis is the largest in the Western Hemisphere's history. Over 7-8 million Venezuelans have emigrated since 2015, primarily to Colombia (which hosts approximately 2.9 million), Peru, Chile, Ecuador, Brazil, and the United States. The scale of Venezuelan migration has strained receiving countries' social services, labor markets, and political systems, generating anti-immigrant sentiment and political backlash. Venezuela's diaspora maintains an active opposition political community but has been unable to translate external pressure into regime change. The migration crisis has geopolitical implications for U.S.-Latin America relations, with Venezuelan migration flows through the Darien Gap and toward the U.S. southern border becoming a major U.S. domestic political issue.

CRIMINAL NETWORKS AND TERRITORIAL CONTROL

The Venezuelan state has effectively ceded territorial control of significant areas to criminal organizations and guerrilla groups. The TREN DE ARAGUA prison gang has expanded from Venezuelan prisons into a transnational criminal network operating in Colombia, Peru, Chile, Ecuador, and the United States — designated a Tier 1 national security threat by the U.S. government in 2024. The ELN (Colombian National Liberation Army) guerrilla group operates freely in Venezuelan border regions under an informal accommodation with the Maduro government. Gold mining in the Orinoco Mining Arc involves criminal networks, illegal mining, and documented massacres of indigenous communities.

COMPOSITE RISK SCORE

Venezuela is classified as HIGH RISK, with particular emphasis on political repression, economic fragility, criminal governance spillovers, and regional migration destabilization. The regime's durability through international pressure and its external support network reduce near-term collapse risk but sustain chronic governance failure.
