LIBYA FRAGILE STATE RISK: FACTIONS AND GOVERNANCE FAILURE

OVERVIEW

Libya's geopolitical risk profile in 2025 reflects a state that has not recovered functional unified governance since the NATO-backed overthrow of Muammar Gaddafi in 2011. The country remains divided between two primary governmental authorities: the internationally recognized Government of National Unity (GNU) based in Tripoli, led by Abdul Hamid Dbeibeh, and the Government of National Stability (GNS) in Benghazi, aligned with the Libyan National Army (LNA) under Field Marshal Khalifa Haftar. Haftar controls eastern Libya and significant portions of the south, while Tripoli-based governments have cycled through UN-facilitated agreements without consolidating authority. The Fragile States Index places Libya in the high-fragility category, reflecting ongoing armed conflict, governance bifurcation, and resource revenue mismanagement.

FACTIONAL LANDSCAPE AND ARMED GROUPS

Libya's conflict topology involves numerous armed factions beyond the primary GNU-LNA division. Tripolitania (western Libya) hosts competing militias — including the Deterrence Apparatus for Combating Organized Crime and Terrorism (DACOT), the Nawasi Brigade, and the 444 Brigade — that nominally align with the GNU but operate with significant autonomy and have clashed violently. The 2023 Tripoli clashes, which killed dozens of civilians, exemplified the volatility of western militia politics. In eastern Libya, Haftar's LNA functions as a disciplined command structure supported by Egypt, the UAE, and Russia (through Wagner/Africa Corps presence). Southern Libya's Fezzan region is contested between tribal armed groups, Tuareg and Tebu militias, and criminal smuggling networks that profit from migration flows to Europe.

OIL REVENUES AND RESOURCE CONTESTATION

Libya possesses Africa's largest proven oil reserves (approximately 48 billion barrels) and has used oil revenue — and control over the National Oil Corporation — as a primary tool of factional competition. Oil production has been repeatedly disrupted by blockades, infrastructure attacks, and political standoffs over revenue distribution. The Petroleum Facilities Guard, which protects oil infrastructure, is divided along factional lines. In 2022-2024, oil output was repeatedly reduced from its potential capacity of approximately 1.2-1.3 million barrels per day by politically motivated blockades. The Central Bank of Libya's governance has been a flashpoint, with rival factions appointing competing governors and fighting over budgetary authority for oil revenues.

MIGRATION AND EUROPEAN SECURITY LINK

Libya's position as the primary transit route for migrants and asylum seekers crossing the Mediterranean to Europe creates a direct linkage between Libyan stability and European security. The International Organization for Migration (IOM) documents hundreds of migrant deaths in Libyan detention centers and at sea annually. European governments, particularly Italy, have funded and trained Libyan Coast Guard units — which have been documented returning migrants to Libya where they face abuse and detention — as a migration containment strategy. The EU's engagement with Libya on migration has been criticized by human rights organizations as complicit in the abuse of migrants. Libya's migration management function gives Libyan factions leverage over European policy, creating perverse incentives.

FOREIGN INTERFERENCE AND PROXY DYNAMICS

Libya exemplifies the geopolitics of foreign interference in fragile states. Turkey intervened militarily in 2019-2020 to save the GNU from Haftar's LNA siege of Tripoli, deploying Syrian mercenaries and advanced air defense systems. Turkey's intervention secured a maritime boundary agreement with Libya and economic concessions. Russia deployed Wagner Group mercenaries (now operating as Africa Corps) to support Haftar's LNA, with continued presence in al-Jufra airbase and Brak al-Shati. Egypt and the UAE have provided weapons and financial support to Haftar. The internationalization of Libya's conflict has created entrenched factional incentives against the negotiated settlement that UN Special Envoy processes have repeatedly attempted to broker.

COMPOSITE RISK SCORE

Libya is classified as HIGH RISK, with particular emphasis on governance bifurcation, armed faction proliferation, oil revenue weaponization, and foreign interference as drivers of chronic instability that periodically erupts into violent conflict.
