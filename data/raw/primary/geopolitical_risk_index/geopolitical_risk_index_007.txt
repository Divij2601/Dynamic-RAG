SUDAN FAILED STATE RISK INDEX

OVERVIEW

Sudan's geopolitical risk profile reached crisis levels in April 2023 when the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) — two armed factions that had jointly conducted the 2021 coup — turned on each other in a catastrophic civil war. What began as a power struggle over the integration of the RSF into the national military has become one of the world's worst humanitarian disasters, with fighting spreading from Khartoum to Darfur, Kordofan, and Gezira. Sudan's ranking on the Fragile States Index places it consistently among the top five most fragile states globally, a position reinforced by the 2023-2025 conflict that has destroyed much of the country's remaining institutional capacity.

CONFLICT DYNAMICS AND FACTIONAL ANALYSIS

The Sudanese Armed Forces, led by General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan, represent the remnants of the formal military establishment and claim governmental legitimacy. The Rapid Support Forces, commanded by General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo ("Hemedti"), evolved from the Janjaweed militias responsible for the Darfur genocide of the 2000s and were formalized and expanded under Omar al-Bashir. Both factions have extensive external support networks: the RSF receives backing from the United Arab Emirates and has historical ties to Russian mercenary networks, while the SAF has sought support from Egypt and other regional actors. Neither faction has demonstrated meaningful commitment to civilian protection, democratic transition, or negotiated settlement. The fractured opposition civilian coalition (Forces of Freedom and Change) has been unable to translate political legitimacy into military or governance capacity.

HUMANITARIAN CATASTROPHE

The scale of Sudan's humanitarian crisis by 2025 is staggering. The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) reports Sudan as the world's largest displacement crisis, with over 8 million internally displaced persons and approximately 2 million refugees who have fled to Egypt, Chad, South Sudan, and Ethiopia. The UN declared famine conditions in multiple regions of Sudan in 2024 — the first official famine declaration anywhere in the world in years. Attacks on humanitarian aid convoys and workers by both SAF and RSF forces have systematically obstructed relief delivery. Sexual violence, including mass rape as a weapon of war, has been extensively documented in Darfur and Khartoum, mirroring atrocity patterns from the Darfur genocide of two decades prior.

GOVERNANCE COLLAPSE AND STATE FRAGILITY

Sudan's state institutions have been functionally destroyed across large parts of the country. The SAF controls portions of northern Sudan and Port Sudan (the provisional governmental seat), while the RSF controls significant parts of Khartoum, Darfur, and Kordofan. Banking, healthcare, education, and civil administration have collapsed in conflict-affected areas. Sudan's currency has hyperinflated, external debt is in default, and oil revenues (derived from fields largely in South Sudan transit through Sudan) provide insufficient fiscal support. The World Bank has suspended most operations. The Sudanese pound lost over 90 percent of its value in 2023-2024.

REGIONAL SPILLOVER RISK

Sudan's collapse generates significant regional risk spillovers. Chad hosts nearly one million Sudanese refugees, straining an already fragile state that itself experienced a coup in 2021. The Central African Republic and South Sudan face destabilization from weapons flows and refugee pressures. The RSF's external support networks, particularly from the UAE via Libya and Chad, reflect the conflict's embeddedness in regional power competition. Egypt views a fragmented Sudan as a strategic threat given the Nile water-sharing dynamics and security concerns on its southern border. Ethiopia, itself managing the aftermath of the Tigray conflict, faces additional pressure from Sudanese displacement.

PROSPECTS AND RISK TRAJECTORY

Peace negotiations facilitated by the African Union, IGAD, and the United States have repeatedly failed to produce ceasefires that either party honored. The structural incentives for both SAF and RSF to continue fighting — control of gold mines, agricultural land, and smuggling networks — undermine negotiated settlement prospects. Sudan's trajectory through 2025-2026 remains toward continued fragmentation, with the risk of de facto partition between SAF-controlled north and RSF-controlled west becoming a medium-term reality.

COMPOSITE RISK SCORE

Sudan is classified as EXTREME RISK under failed state metrics, with active famine, mass atrocity, and state collapse constituting simultaneous crisis dimensions.
