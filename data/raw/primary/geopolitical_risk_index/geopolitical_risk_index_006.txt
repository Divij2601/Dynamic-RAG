PAKISTAN GEOPOLITICAL RISK: NUCLEAR INSTABILITY

OVERVIEW

Pakistan presents a geopolitical risk profile of exceptional complexity, combining nuclear weapons capability with persistent governance fragility, civil-military political dysfunction, economic crisis, active jihadist insurgency, and a high-tension rivalry with India. Pakistan is the only nuclear-armed state that simultaneously faces significant internal fragility risks, making it arguably the most dangerous country from a nuclear security perspective in the world according to analysts at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace and the Stimson Center. In 2024-2025, Pakistan's political crisis deepened with the imprisonment of former Prime Minister Imran Khan and the military's continued role as the ultimate arbiter of political power.

NUCLEAR SECURITY AND WEAPONS PROGRAM

Pakistan possesses an estimated 160-170 nuclear warheads, making it the world's fifth-largest nuclear arsenal. Pakistan's nuclear doctrine is based on "full spectrum deterrence" against India, explicitly rejecting no-first-use pledges. Pakistan has developed battlefield tactical nuclear weapons (TNWs) with very low yield thresholds, designed to deter India's Cold Start conventional military doctrine. The combination of tactical nuclear weapons, delegated authority concerns, and the presence of Islamist extremist networks that have penetrated elements of the military establishment creates the highest nuclear terrorism acquisition risk of any nuclear-armed state. The A.Q. Khan network, dismantled in 2004, demonstrated that Pakistani nuclear scientists were willing to proliferate nuclear technology to Libya, Iran, and North Korea — a historical precedent that remains a reference point for worst-case nuclear security analyses.

CIVIL-MILITARY DYSFUNCTION AND POLITICAL CRISIS

Pakistan's political system has never established firm civilian supremacy over the military. The army (and its Inter-Services Intelligence directorate) functions as a parallel state that selects, supports, and removes civilian governments. Imran Khan's Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI) party won the February 2024 elections by most assessments but was denied power through manipulation, with Shehbaz Sharif installed as Prime Minister. Khan was imprisoned on multiple charges widely viewed as politically motivated. Mass PTI protests, including the November 2024 march on Islamabad that was violently dispersed, demonstrated deep societal polarization. The political crisis consumes governmental bandwidth and deepens institutional distrust.

ECONOMIC FRAGILITY

Pakistan's economy has been in near-continuous crisis since 2022, requiring multiple IMF bailout programs. Foreign exchange reserves fell to critically low levels, threatening import capacity for fuel and food. Inflation exceeded 30 percent in 2023 before moderating. Pakistan's debt service obligations consume over 50 percent of government revenues. Structural economic problems include low tax compliance (tax-to-GDP ratio around 9 percent), energy sector circular debt, and an export base dominated by low-value textiles. Climate shocks — the catastrophic 2022 floods that submerged one-third of Pakistan's territory — reveal extreme climate vulnerability that exacerbates economic fragility.

JIHADIST INSURGENCY AND REGIONAL TERRORISM

The Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP), emboldened by the Afghan Taliban's return to power in Kabul, has escalated attacks across Pakistan's Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province and FATA regions. TTP attacks on military personnel, police, and civilians increased sharply in 2023-2024. The Afghan Taliban government has provided safe haven to TTP despite Pakistani pressure, creating a strategic dilemma: Pakistan's historical support for Afghan Taliban proxies has now produced a blowback insurgency it cannot easily suppress. Balochistan separatist violence (Balochistan Liberation Army attacks on Chinese CPEC projects) adds a second insurgency track.

INDIA-PAKISTAN RISK

The India-Pakistan rivalry remains one of the most dangerous bilateral relationships in the world, involving two nuclear-armed states with unresolved territorial disputes (Kashmir), a history of four wars, and recurring crisis escalation dynamics. The 2019 Pulwama-Balakot crisis, in which India conducted an airstrike inside Pakistani territory following a terrorist attack, demonstrated how quickly crises can escalate toward nuclear thresholds. India's revocation of Kashmir's special status in 2019 and ongoing military standoffs along the Line of Control sustain chronic tension.

COMPOSITE RISK ASSESSMENT

Pakistan's composite risk score is VERY HIGH. The combination of nuclear weapons, governance fragility, economic crisis, jihadist insurgency, and India rivalry creates a uniquely dangerous risk matrix that places Pakistan at the apex of nuclear security concern globally.
