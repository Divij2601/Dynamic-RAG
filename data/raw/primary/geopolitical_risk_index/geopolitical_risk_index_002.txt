CHINA GEOPOLITICAL RISK ASSESSMENT 2025

OVERVIEW AND STRATEGIC SIGNIFICANCE

China's geopolitical risk profile in 2025 is defined by its unique position as the world's second-largest economy, the primary strategic competitor to the United States, and a revisionist power seeking to reshape the rules-based international order. Unlike fragile or failed states, China's risk derives not from state weakness but from state strength deployed in pursuit of contested territorial and hegemonic objectives. The U.S. National Security Strategy designates China as the "pacing challenge," while NATO's 2023 Strategic Concept identified Chinese coercion as a systemic challenge. Composite risk indices must distinguish between China's internal stability (high) and its external aggression potential (rising), which together produce a complex risk topology.

TAIWAN STRAIT AND MILITARY ESCALATION RISK

The Taiwan Strait represents the single most consequential potential flashpoint in the global system. The People's Liberation Army (PLA) has conducted increasingly provocative military exercises around Taiwan, including the August 2022 exercises following Nancy Pelosi's visit and expanded exercises in 2024 following Taiwan's presidential election of Lai Ching-te, whom Beijing regards as a separatist. The PLA's military modernization, including anti-access/area-denial (A2/AD) capabilities, carrier strike groups, and hypersonic missile systems, is specifically designed to deter or defeat U.S. intervention in a Taiwan contingency. Intelligence assessments differ on timelines, but Director of National Intelligence reports indicate China is developing the capability to conduct a Taiwan invasion operation by 2027, though intent remains uncertain. A Taiwan conflict would trigger global economic catastrophe through supply chain disruption, financial market contagion, and potential U.S.-China military confrontation.

SOUTH CHINA SEA AND REGIONAL COERCION

China claims approximately 90 percent of the South China Sea via the "nine-dash line," a claim rejected by the 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration ruling. Despite this, China has continued island-building on contested reefs, militarized artificial islands, and conducted gray-zone harassment of Philippine, Vietnamese, and Malaysian vessels. Incidents at Second Thomas Shoal between Chinese Coast Guard vessels and Philippine resupply missions escalated through 2024 and into 2025. ASEAN's inability to produce a unified response reflects China's successful divide-and-influence strategy across the region. The risk of a miscalculation triggering broader conflict remains elevated.

ECONOMIC INTERDEPENDENCE AND DECOUPLING RISK

China's deep integration into the global economy creates a paradox: it is simultaneously the largest trading partner of over 120 countries and a source of systemic economic risk through trade weaponization, technology theft, and supply chain chokepoints. The U.S.-China trade war, semiconductor export controls, and the broader "de-risking" agenda pursued by the G7 are reshaping global supply chains. China dominates critical mineral processing (over 60 percent of rare earth processing) and solar panel manufacturing (over 80 percent), creating strategic leverage points. China's own economic headwinds in 2025, including property sector deleveraging (Evergrande collapse), youth unemployment above 20 percent, and deflation risk, introduce domestic instability considerations that could prompt external assertiveness as a political distraction.

INTERNAL GOVERNANCE AND SOCIETAL RISK

Xi Jinping's consolidation of power following the 20th Party Congress in 2022 and his unprecedented third term have eliminated intra-party checks. The suppression of Hong Kong's democracy movement, ongoing repression in Xinjiang (documented by UN OHCHR as potentially constituting crimes against humanity), and surveillance state expansion reduce internal political risk of regime change but increase external reputational and sanction risk. Taiwan, Tibet, and Xinjiang remain active human rights flashpoints that generate international pressure and constrain diplomatic options.

COMPOSITE RISK SUMMARY

China presents a HIGH and RISING geopolitical risk profile, with the Taiwan contingency representing the highest-consequence risk scenario globally. Economic interdependence makes risk management complex, requiring differentiated treatment of economic exposure risk versus military confrontation risk. Xi Jinping's centralized decision-making reduces the probability of course correction through elite signaling, increasing the importance of direct bilateral communication channels.
