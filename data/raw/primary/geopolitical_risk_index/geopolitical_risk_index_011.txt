ETHIOPIA CONFLICT FRAGILITY RISK PROFILE

OVERVIEW

Ethiopia, Africa's second-most populous country with approximately 125 million people, experienced a catastrophic civil war from November 2020 to November 2022 in the Tigray region that claimed an estimated 300,000-500,000 lives and left millions in famine-like conditions. Although the Pretoria Agreement of November 2022 ended major hostilities in Tigray, Ethiopia faces multiple concurrent armed conflicts in 2025 — in Amhara region, Oromia region, and along the Somali and Afar peripheries — that collectively suggest a state facing deep structural fragility despite its developmental ambitions and federal institutional framework. The Fragile States Index has elevated Ethiopia's score significantly since 2020.

TIGRAY WAR AND AFTERMATH

The Tigray war, which began when Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's federal government sent military forces against the Tigrayan People's Liberation Front (TPLF) following the TPLF's preemptive attack on federal military bases, became one of Africa's deadliest conflicts in decades. Both sides committed documented atrocities. The Ethiopian National Defence Force and allied Amhara regional forces conducted massacres, systematic sexual violence, and imposed siege conditions causing famine deaths. TPLF forces committed atrocities during their brief occupation of Amhara and Afar regions in 2021. The Pretoria Agreement provides for TPLF disarmament and Tigrayan reintegration but implementation has been slow. Hundreds of thousands of displaced Tigrayans remained in camps in 2024-2025 with limited return conditions, and accountability for war crimes has been minimal.

AMHARA AND OROMIA CONFLICTS

Following Tigray, conflict spread to Amhara region, where the Fano militia — an Amhara nationalist armed group — turned against the federal government in 2023. Amhara Fano fighters, who had previously cooperated with federal forces against Tigray, objected to Abiy Ahmed's post-war peace process and perceived threats to Amhara territorial interests. By 2024-2025, significant fighting continues in Amhara region with reports of heavy civilian casualties. In Oromia, the Oromo Liberation Army (OLA/Shane), which once held a ceasefire with the government, has continued armed operations against federal and regional forces. The Oromia conflict involves complex dynamics including ethnic targeting of Amhara communities within Oromia, mass displacement, and internet shutdowns that obscure conflict reporting.

FAMINE AND HUMANITARIAN RISK

Ethiopia has experienced recurrent acute food insecurity driven by conflict, drought (La Nina-related rainfall deficits in the south and east), and economic deterioration. The UN estimated approximately 20 million Ethiopians in need of humanitarian assistance in 2024. The Tigray famine of 2021-2022, in which UN officials documented conditions that may have caused hundreds of thousands of deaths, represents one of the worst famines of the 21st century to that point. Humanitarian access remains restricted in conflict zones. The Ethiopian government has periodically expelled UN agencies and restricted aid delivery, accusing organizations of political bias.

NILE POLITICS AND REGIONAL TENSIONS

Ethiopia's construction of the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) on the Blue Nile has created a long-running diplomatic dispute with Egypt and Sudan. Egypt regards the Nile as an existential water security issue and has repeatedly threatened unspecified action to protect its water rights. GERD reached full reservoir capacity in 2023, and Ethiopia continues adding turbines to expand power generation capacity. Egypt-Ethiopia relations remain acrimonious, with Ethiopia's internal instability adding uncertainty to an already tense regional water diplomacy context. The GERD dispute intersects with broader Nile Basin geopolitics involving ten riparian states.

COMPOSITE RISK ASSESSMENT

Ethiopia is classified as VERY HIGH RISK under fragile state metrics. The convergence of multiple simultaneous armed conflicts, famine risk, weak accountability institutions, and regional water disputes creates a compounding fragility profile. Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's Nobel Peace Prize-winning trajectory has been reversed by the Tigray war; his political survival and policy coherence remain uncertain amid domestic military and political pressures.
