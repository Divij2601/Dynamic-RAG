MYANMAR COUP MILITARY GOVERNANCE RISK

OVERVIEW

Myanmar's geopolitical risk profile underwent a fundamental transformation on February 1, 2021, when the Tatmadaw (Myanmar military) staged a coup overthrowing the democratically elected National League for Democracy government of Aung San Suu Kyi and detaining her along with President Win Myint. The coup reversed a decade of democratic transition and triggered a mass civil disobedience movement and eventually armed resistance that has evolved into a multi-front civil war. By 2025, the military junta (State Administration Council, SAC) has lost effective control of large swaths of Myanmar's territory to the People's Defence Force (PDF), ethnic armed organizations (EAOs), and resistance coalitions. Myanmar is classified among the most fragile states in Southeast Asia.

COUP ORIGINS AND POLITICAL CONTEXT

The February 2021 coup was triggered by the Tatmadaw's rejection of the November 2020 election results, in which the NLD won a supermajority. Military commander Senior General Min Aung Hlaing alleged widespread electoral fraud, a claim rejected by independent election monitors. The underlying driver was the military's determination to preserve its constitutionally embedded political and economic privileges against a democratic consolidation that threatened both. The 2008 constitution, drafted under military supervision, reserved 25 percent of parliamentary seats for the military and granted it autonomous control over defense, home affairs, and border affairs ministries. The coup represented the military's refusal to allow the civilian-led constitutional reform process that would have ended these privileges.

CIVIL WAR AND MILITARY FRAGMENTATION

The SAC's attempt to suppress post-coup resistance through mass violence — security forces killed over 3,000 protesters and bystanders between February 2021 and end of 2022 — instead galvanized armed resistance. The People's Defence Force, formed by the National Unity Government (NUG, the parallel civilian government-in-exile), conducted guerrilla operations across central Myanmar. A major shift occurred in late 2023 with "Operation 1027," launched by the Three Brotherhood Alliance (Three Brothers Alliance) of ethnic armed organizations — the Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army, Taang National Liberation Army, and Arakan Army — which seized large swaths of Shan State and northern Myanmar from SAC forces. By 2024-2025, SAC forces have suffered unprecedented territorial losses, with some analysts describing the regime as at risk of military collapse in specific regions.

HUMANITARIAN CRISIS

The UN estimates over 2 million internally displaced persons in Myanmar as of 2025, with displacement accelerating following the 2023-2024 military offensives and counter-offensives. The junta has used airstrikes against civilian populations, including attacks on IDP camps, churches, and humanitarian convoys, documented by the UN Special Rapporteur on Myanmar. The healthcare and education systems have been severely disrupted by coup-related brain drain (doctors, teachers, and civil servants participating in the Civil Disobedience Movement) and targeted attacks on facilities serving resistance communities. Coup-related economic contraction has pushed over one million additional people into poverty.

REGIONAL IMPLICATIONS AND ASEAN FAILURE

Myanmar's crisis has exposed the limits of ASEAN's non-interference doctrine. The ASEAN Five-Point Consensus peace plan, agreed in April 2021, has been systematically violated by the SAC. ASEAN has excluded Myanmar's SAC representative from summits, an unprecedented step, but has been unable to produce meaningful pressure. China, which shares a long border with Myanmar, has adopted a pragmatic hedging strategy, maintaining relations with both the SAC and several ethnic armed organizations it has historically supported. The drug trade, particularly methamphetamine produced in SAC-controlled and ethnic-controlled areas, represents a major regional security spillover. Scam compound operations in border areas have emerged as a major transnational crime issue, victimizing tens of thousands of people from across Asia.

COMPOSITE RISK SCORE

Myanmar is classified as VERY HIGH RISK, with ongoing civil war, mass atrocities, state fragmentation, and regional spillover effects defining the risk landscape. The SAC's declining military control suggests a trajectory toward further fragmentation rather than stabilization.
