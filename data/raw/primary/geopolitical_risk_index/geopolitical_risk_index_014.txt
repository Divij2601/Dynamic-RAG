YEMEN HUMANITARIAN AND MILITARY RISK ASSESSMENT

OVERVIEW

Yemen's geopolitical risk profile in 2025 is shaped by a decade of civil war that has produced what the UN has described at various points as the world's worst humanitarian crisis. The conflict pits the internationally recognized government (supported by a Saudi-led coalition) against the Houthi movement (Ansar Allah), which controls Sanaa, the Red Sea coast, and much of northern Yemen. A fragile UN-brokered truce has reduced but not ended major military operations in the main theater, but the Houthis' entry into the Gaza conflict paradigm through Red Sea shipping attacks from late 2023 transformed Yemen from a contained regional crisis into a global geopolitical flashpoint with implications for international trade routes.

HOUTHI RED SEA ATTACKS AND GLOBAL IMPLICATIONS

From November 2023, following the outbreak of the Gaza war, Ansar Allah declared itself in solidarity with Palestinian civilians and began targeting commercial shipping in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden. Over 100 commercial vessels were attacked using anti-ship missiles, naval drones, and in some cases Iranian-supplied weapons by mid-2024. The attacks forced major shipping companies to divert vessels around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-14 days and significant cost to Asia-Europe trade routes. The Suez Canal's transit volumes declined by approximately 50 percent. The U.S. and UK conducted airstrikes against Houthi military infrastructure in Yemen, while also deploying naval assets for interception. The Houthis demonstrated considerable resilience, continuing attacks despite significant infrastructure destruction, showcasing the asymmetric capacity of a non-state actor to impose global economic costs.

HUMANITARIAN CATASTROPHE

Yemen's humanitarian crisis is among the most severe in the world. The UN estimates over 150,000 people have been killed in the conflict, including both direct violence and indirect deaths from disease and malnutrition. Over 21 million Yemenis — roughly two-thirds of the population — require humanitarian assistance. Famine conditions have periodically affected northern Houthi-controlled areas and southern regions. The destruction of medical infrastructure, water systems, and ports has created a public health catastrophe compounded by cholera outbreaks, COVID-19, and malnutrition. The Safer oil tanker, moored off the Hodeida coast and laden with decaying crude oil, posed a catastrophic environmental and shipping risk for years before an emergency UN-led operation transferred the oil in 2023.

POLITICAL FRAGMENTATION

Yemen's political landscape is fragmented beyond the primary Houthi-government divide. The Southern Transitional Council (STC), backed by the UAE, seeks independence for southern Yemen and has clashed militarily with the internationally recognized government it nominally supports against the Houthis. Tribal politics, regional grievances, and competing external patrons (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iran) have created a multi-party conflict in which alliances are fluid and peace agreements are difficult to sustain. The Presidential Leadership Council established in 2022 represents an attempt to consolidate anti-Houthi political forces but has struggled with internal coherence.

SAUDI ARABIA-IRAN DIPLOMACY AND CONFLICT DYNAMICS

The March 2023 Saudi Arabia-Iran diplomatic normalization, brokered by China, raised hopes for a Yemen conflict resolution pathway. Saudi-Houthi negotiations gained momentum through 2023-2024, and a formal ceasefire process was underway. However, the Gaza conflict and the Houthi decision to enter the war against Israeli and Western targets complicated this trajectory, with Saudi Arabia caught between its diplomatic normalization with Iran and pressure from the U.S. and Israel to contain Houthi operations. The Yemen conflict's resolution trajectory remains deeply uncertain given its embeddedness in regional geopolitics.

COMPOSITE RISK SCORE

Yemen is classified as EXTREME humanitarian risk and VERY HIGH military-geopolitical risk. The Houthi Red Sea operation has elevated Yemen from a contained regional crisis to a direct actor in the global geopolitical competition, with implications for international trade security and Middle East regional dynamics that extend far beyond Yemen's territory.
