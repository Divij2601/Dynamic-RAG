HAITI STATE COLLAPSE RISK COMPOSITE

OVERVIEW

Haiti's geopolitical risk profile in 2025 represents a textbook case of compounding state collapse, in which each crisis layer has weakened institutional capacity for the next. The July 2021 assassination of President Jovenel Moïse removed the last semblance of elected executive authority. Gang federations, principally the G9 alliance led by Jimmy Chérizier ("Barbecue") and the Viv Ansanm coalition, now control an estimated 80 percent of Port-au-Prince. In early 2024, coordinated gang attacks seized control of the main international airport, the port, the central bank building, and the national penitentiary (releasing approximately 4,000 prisoners), effectively demonstrating that the Haitian National Police could not secure the capital. The Fragile States Index ranks Haiti among the most fragile states in the Western Hemisphere.

POLITICAL VACUUM AND GOVERNANCE COLLAPSE

Haiti has been without a functional elected government since 2021. The Transitional Presidential Council (TPC) established in April 2024, under the auspices of a U.S.-Caribbean Community (CARICOM) framework, represents an attempt to create an interim governance structure bridging to elections. However, the TPC has been plagued by internal disputes, corruption allegations, and the fundamental problem that it lacks coercive capacity to enforce authority against armed gangs. Prime Ministers have resigned in rapid succession under threat and pressure. Local government structures have largely ceased functioning. Courts, hospitals, schools, and utilities operate sporadically or not at all in many areas.

GANG CONTROL AND CRIMINAL GOVERNANCE

The gang ecosystem in Haiti has evolved beyond simple criminal enterprise into a form of predatory governance. Viv Ansanm, a coalition formed in late 2023 between previously rival gang federations, represents a strategic consolidation that overwhelmed state security forces. Gangs control access to food, fuel, and humanitarian aid in Port-au-Prince, extracting rents from the population. Kidnapping for ransom became Haiti's fastest-growing industry by some measures, with rates skyrocketing between 2021 and 2024. Humanitarian organizations have faced systematic extortion and staff kidnappings. The structural drivers of gang power include the dismantling of the Haitian military in 1995 (and its only partial reconstitution), decades of political instrumentalization of armed groups, and the economic marginalization of urban youth.

HUMANITARIAN CRISIS AND NATURAL DISASTER VULNERABILITY

Haiti's humanitarian profile reflects severe multi-dimensional poverty. Over half the population was food insecure as of 2024 UN assessments. Cholera, nearly eliminated by 2022, re-emerged in 2022 and has persisted in conflict-affected areas lacking clean water infrastructure. The 2010 earthquake killed an estimated 230,000 people and displaced 1.5 million, with reconstruction incomplete when the 2021 earthquake struck, killing over 2,200 more. Hurricane-force storms regularly devastate Haiti's deforested hillsides, triggering landslides and flooding. The combination of poverty, governance failure, and natural disaster exposure creates a humanitarian risk profile that is essentially permanent absent transformative structural change.

INTERNATIONAL INTERVENTION AND MSS MISSION

A Multinational Security Support Mission (MSS) authorized by UN Security Council Resolution 2699 in October 2023 and led by Kenya began deploying in 2024. The mission of approximately 1,000 Kenyan and Caribbean police officers faced immediate challenges: insufficient personnel, limited equipment, and a security environment in which gang forces are well-armed and organized. The mission has achieved limited operational impact in restoring Port-au-Prince security. The fundamental challenge — that external security assistance cannot substitute for indigenous political will and institutional rebuilding — remains unresolved. Previous UN missions (MINUSTAH, 2004-2017) failed to produce durable improvements and were associated with the introduction of cholera into Haiti.

COMPOSITE RISK SCORE

Haiti receives an EXTREME risk classification under state collapse metrics. The convergence of absent elected government, gang territorial control, humanitarian catastrophe, and natural disaster vulnerability makes Haiti the Western Hemisphere's most acute failed state risk scenario, with no credible pathway to stability in the near term.
