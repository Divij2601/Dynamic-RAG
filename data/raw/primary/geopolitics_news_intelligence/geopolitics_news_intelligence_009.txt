SECURITY INTELLIGENCE BRIEF: NORTH AFRICA AND THE SAHEL

CLASSIFICATION: REGIONAL SECURITY INTELLIGENCE BRIEF
DATE: 2025
REGION: SAHEL — MALI, BURKINA FASO, NIGER, CHAD; NORTH AFRICA — LIBYA, TUNISIA, ALGERIA

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Sahel region in 2025 is experiencing the convergence of multiple intersecting crises: jihadist insurgency by Islamic State Sahel Province and al-Qaeda-affiliated Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin, a wave of military coups that has swept through Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger, mass displacement of civilian populations, and the systematic expulsion of French and Western security forces. Russia's Africa Corps, the successor structure to the Wagner Group, has moved into the security vacuum created by the departure of French Operation Barkhane forces. The resulting governance collapse across the Sahel is creating conditions amenable to jihadist territorial expansion and threatens to spill over into coastal West African states including Benin, Ghana, Ivory Coast, and Togo.

JIHADIST THREAT LANDSCAPE

JNIM, affiliated with al-Qaeda, remains the dominant jihadist actor in Mali and Burkina Faso and has extended operations into Niger, Benin, and Togo. The group conducts complex ambushes against military convoys, assassinations of local officials, imposition of informal governance structures in rural areas, and attacks on civilian populations perceived as supportive of state security forces. The Islamic State Sahel Province, competing with JNIM for territory and recruits, has been particularly active in the Tri-Border Area of Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso and in the Lake Chad Basin shared by Niger, Nigeria, Chad, and Cameroon. Boko Haram offshoots in the Lake Chad Basin continue to terrorize civilian populations, though their territorial control has been significantly reduced by multinational military operations.

POLITICAL DYNAMICS — COUP BELT AND ANTI-WESTERN SENTIMENT

The military governments of Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger have formed the Alliance of Sahel States, declaring their departure from ECOWAS and their intention to pursue security partnerships outside Western frameworks. The withdrawal of French troops from all three countries, followed by the departure of American forces from Niger in 2024, represents a dramatic shift in the Western security architecture that has shaped the region for decades. Russia's Africa Corps has assumed security advisory and combat support roles in Mali and Burkina Faso, with reported deployments in Niger as well. Anti-French and anti-Western sentiment has been deliberately amplified through social media campaigns traced to Russian information operations, resonating with populations who have experienced decades of French military presence with limited improvement in security outcomes.

NORTH AFRICA DYNAMICS

Libya remains a divided state with the internationally recognized Government of National Unity in Tripoli and the Libyan National Army under General Khalifa Haftar controlling the eastern regions. The UAE, Egypt, and Russia support Haftar while Turkey backs Tripoli factions. The political division has prevented economic development from Libya's significant oil reserves and maintained the country as a transit hub for migrants attempting to reach Europe. Tunisia under President Kais Saied has consolidated authoritarian rule, triggering an economic crisis as Western financial institutions have suspended assistance and investment. Algeria maintains its strategic posture of non-intervention while quietly influencing Sahel dynamics through intelligence operations and diplomatic mediation.

INTELLIGENCE ASSESSMENT AND OUTLOOK

The Sahel security situation is assessed as continuing to deteriorate without a comprehensive reversal of governance failures, economic underdevelopment, and jihadist recruitment drivers. Russia's Africa Corps lacks the capability, legitimacy, or motivation to address these root causes and is primarily focused on protecting mining interests and junta security while harvesting political influence. The expansion of jihadist insurgency into coastal West Africa represents the most significant near-term escalation risk. European states face difficult choices between engagement with coup governments — which may aid counterterrorism but legitimizes authoritarian rule — and principled disengagement that cedes ground to Russia. Migration flows toward Europe driven by insecurity and climate shocks will remain politically consequential for European domestic politics.
