NEWS ANALYSIS: BRICS EXPANSION IMPLICATIONS 2025

CLASSIFICATION: OPEN SOURCE GEOPOLITICAL ANALYSIS
DATE: 2025
REGION: GLOBAL — BRICS MEMBER STATES

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The BRICS grouping, which expanded dramatically with the admission of Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates in January 2024 and Indonesia in January 2025, now represents a coalition encompassing nearly half the world's population and a significant share of global GDP and energy production. The expansion reflects dissatisfaction with Western-dominated international institutions among a broad coalition of large developing and middle-income economies. However, BRICS remains characterized by deep internal divisions of interest, competing visions for the grouping's purpose, and structural barriers to acting as a coherent geopolitical bloc. The most consequential debates within the expanded BRICS concern the development of alternative financial infrastructure, the role of the New Development Bank, and the prospects for a BRICS currency or payment system to reduce dollar dependence.

EXPANSION DYNAMICS AND INTERNAL TENSIONS

The 2024 expansion nearly doubled BRICS membership and significantly altered the grouping's internal political geometry. The addition of Gulf states Saudi Arabia and the UAE brought large hydrocarbon exporters with deep financial ties to the Western financial system and the United States security umbrella, creating inherent contradictions within the bloc. Both states maintain dollar-pegged currencies and significant dollar-denominated sovereign wealth funds. Saudi Arabia's participation reflects a hedging strategy rather than alignment with Chinese or Russian geopolitical objectives. Ethiopia and Egypt bring significant African representation, while Iran's inclusion represents the most overtly anti-Western addition. Indonesia's admission adds the world's largest Muslim-majority democracy and a country with historically strong non-alignment traditions.

DE-DOLLARIZATION AMBITIONS AND FINANCIAL INFRASTRUCTURE

The de-dollarization agenda remains the most discussed but least advanced element of BRICS ambitions. Proposals for a common BRICS currency or a basket-based settlement mechanism face fundamental economic and political obstacles. China's yuan is the only BRICS currency with significant international usage, but Chinese capital controls limit its role as a true reserve currency. Russia has the most immediate political motivation for de-dollarization given sanctions, but its economy lacks the scale to anchor an alternative. Brazil and India, while rhetorically supportive of reducing dollar dependence, maintain deep integration with dollar-based trade and finance. The New Development Bank has grown its loan portfolio but remains a modest complement to, rather than replacement for, the IMF and World Bank.

GEOPOLITICAL SIGNIFICANCE

Despite internal incoherence, BRICS expansion carries real geopolitical significance. It signals that the era of US-led unipolarity is contested by a diverse coalition of states unwilling to simply align with Washington's preferences. It provides a platform for coordination on multilateral reform demands including representation changes at the IMF and World Bank, reform of the UN Security Council, and climate finance obligations of wealthy nations. The grouping amplifies narratives about Global South solidarity that resonate with populations in Africa, Asia, and Latin America who view Western foreign policy through the lens of historical imperialism. China and Russia use BRICS summits and related processes to contest Western narratives about democracy, rule of law, and international order.

INTELLIGENCE ASSESSMENT AND OUTLOOK

BRICS is assessed as unlikely to cohere into a unified geopolitical bloc capable of systematically challenging Western-led institutions in the near term. Internal divisions of interest, particularly between democratic members like India, Brazil, and South Africa and authoritarian members like China, Russia, and Iran, create structural limits on coordination. The most realistic assessment of BRICS impact is as a signal of multipolarity and a venue for marginal but cumulatively significant shifts in international economic governance. The key variable to watch is whether the expansion drives meaningful progress on alternative payment systems. Even partial progress on this front could have long-term consequences for US financial sanctions effectiveness and dollar hegemony, which are genuine security interests for Washington.
