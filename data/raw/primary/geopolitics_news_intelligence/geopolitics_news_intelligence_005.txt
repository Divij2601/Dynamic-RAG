INTELLIGENCE BRIEF: SUDAN CIVIL WAR AND FAMINE RISK ASSESSMENT

CLASSIFICATION: HUMANITARIAN AND SECURITY INTELLIGENCE BRIEF
DATE: ONGOING — 2025 ASSESSMENT
REGION: SUDAN, HORN OF AFRICA

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Sudan's civil war, which erupted in April 2023 between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces, has produced one of the world's most severe humanitarian catastrophes. The conflict has displaced over 10 million people internally and driven more than 2 million refugees into neighboring Chad, Egypt, South Sudan, Ethiopia, and the Central African Republic. The UN has declared famine in multiple regions of Sudan, making it the first officially declared famine globally in years. Agricultural systems have collapsed across the Darfur, Kordofan, and Khartoum regions. International humanitarian access remains severely constrained, and the war shows no signs of abating as both sides receive external military support.

MILITARY SITUATION

The Rapid Support Forces, commanded by General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo known as Hemeti, controls large swaths of Darfur, Kordofan, and much of greater Khartoum following its initial seizure of the capital in 2023. The Sudanese Armed Forces, led by General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan, has been attempting to recapture lost territory from its strongholds in the north and east, including Port Sudan, which now serves as the de facto government seat. Both sides have been accused of severe human rights abuses; the RSF in particular has been credibly linked to mass atrocities against non-Arab civilian populations in Darfur that the United States has officially designated as genocide. Air power used by the SAF has caused significant civilian casualties in RSF-held urban areas.

FAMINE AND HUMANITARIAN CRISIS

The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification declared famine in the Zamzam displacement camp in North Darfur, affecting over 500,000 people in the camp alone. Conditions across Sudan's conflict zones are deteriorating rapidly. An estimated 26 million people — more than half the population — face acute food insecurity. Agricultural seasons have been missed as farmers are displaced or killed, livestock have been looted, and markets have ceased to function. Humanitarian organizations including the World Food Programme, UNICEF, and Doctors Without Borders have repeatedly reported obstruction of aid deliveries at both SAF and RSF checkpoints. Sudan's healthcare system, already fragile before the conflict, has effectively collapsed in war-affected areas.

EXTERNAL ACTORS AND GEOPOLITICAL DIMENSIONS

The conflict has significant external dimensions that complicate resolution. The UAE has been widely reported to be supplying weapons and logistical support to the RSF through transit routes in Chad and Libya, motivated by interests in Sudan's gold reserves and regional influence. Egypt supports the SAF, reflecting historical ties and concerns about Nile security. Russia has maintained connections with both military factions and seeks to preserve its access to a planned Red Sea naval base agreed under earlier Sudanese governments. The United States and European Union have imposed targeted sanctions on key figures and pressed for humanitarian access but lack significant leverage. The Intergovernmental Authority on Development and African Union mediation efforts have produced no durable ceasefire.

INTELLIGENCE ASSESSMENT AND OUTLOOK

Sudan's trajectory is assessed as continuing to deteriorate through 2025. Neither the SAF nor RSF appears capable of achieving decisive military victory in the near term, ensuring continued warfare and humanitarian suffering. The famine risk extends beyond Darfur and will likely reach catastrophic proportions in additional regions absent a significant increase in humanitarian access and a cessation of hostilities. The regional spillover effects, including refugee pressure on Chad, South Sudan, and Egypt, will stress already fragile states. International attention has been insufficient relative to the scale of the crisis, partly overshadowed by Gaza and Ukraine. The risk of ethnic cleansing and genocide in Darfur requires urgent international priority designation.
