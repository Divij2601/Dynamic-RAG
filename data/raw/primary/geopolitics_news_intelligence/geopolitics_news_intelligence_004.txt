WEEKLY GEOPOLITICAL DIGEST: ASIA-PACIFIC — MARCH 2025

CLASSIFICATION: OPEN SOURCE WEEKLY INTELLIGENCE DIGEST
DATE: MARCH 2025
REGION: ASIA-PACIFIC

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Asia-Pacific region in March 2025 is defined by escalating great-power competition between the United States and China, ongoing instability on the Korean Peninsula, and deepening security cooperation among US-aligned states through frameworks including the Quad and AUKUS. China's military modernization and assertive posture in the South China Sea and around Taiwan continue to drive the region's strategic calculus. Simultaneously, Southeast Asian states are navigating intensified pressure to align with either Washington or Beijing, often seeking strategic ambiguity. Economic interdependence continues to complicate security alignments, with most regional economies maintaining significant trade ties with China while deepening security partnerships with the United States.

CHINA STRATEGIC POSTURE

China's People's Liberation Army conducted large-scale exercises in the Western Pacific in late February 2025, including simulated air and naval blockade scenarios around Taiwan. The exercises involved carrier strike groups, amphibious landing forces, and strategic rocket force assets conducting mock missile strikes on critical infrastructure. Beijing characterized the exercises as a response to what it termed "provocative separatist activities" following recent Taiwan-US arms sale announcements. The Philippines has formally protested PLA Navy incursions into its exclusive economic zone in the South China Sea, with Chinese Coast Guard vessels using water cannons against Philippine resupply missions to the BRP Sierra Madre grounded vessel at Second Thomas Shoal. The US-Philippines Mutual Defense Treaty continues to be tested as both sides calibrate red lines.

KOREAN PENINSULA DYNAMICS

North Korea conducted its twelfth ballistic missile test of 2025 in early March, launching what analysts identified as a new intermediate-range ballistic missile variant with a maneuvering reentry vehicle. Kim Jong-un has consolidated personal authority through a series of party purges and promoted younger technocratic loyalists to key positions. The Russia-North Korea defense partnership has deepened following the deployment of North Korean troops to Russia's Kursk region, with Pyongyang receiving advanced missile technology and satellite assistance in return. South Korea has responded by increasing defense spending to 3 percent of GDP and accelerating its own ballistic missile program. Diplomatic engagement between Seoul and Pyongyang remains frozen.

SOUTHEAST ASIA AND ASEAN

The March 2025 ASEAN summit in Kuala Lumpur produced a joint communique notable for its more direct language on South China Sea disputes, reflecting growing assertiveness among claimant states. Vietnam, the Philippines, and Indonesia have been particularly vocal in challenging Chinese encroachment. Myanmar remains a key destabilizer, with the military junta's counterinsurgency campaign creating massive refugee flows into Thailand, Bangladesh, and India. ASEAN's inability to address the Myanmar crisis effectively has further strained the bloc's credibility. Meanwhile, the Mekong region faces escalating resource competition linked to Chinese upstream dam management affecting downstream water flows to Vietnam, Thailand, Cambodia, and Laos.

ECONOMIC AND TRADE DYNAMICS

Regional economic patterns reflect the accelerating decoupling of technology supply chains from China. Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and the United States have deepened the Chip 4 Alliance coordination, restricting sales of advanced semiconductors and manufacturing equipment to China. Beijing has responded with export controls on gallium, germanium, and rare earth elements critical to Western defense industries. The Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership continues to function as the world's largest free trade area by GDP but is increasingly strained by geopolitical tensions. Australia's trade relationship with China has partially recovered following the lifting of Chinese tariffs on Australian barley, beef, and wine, but security-oriented economic screening has become normalized across the region.

INTELLIGENCE ASSESSMENT AND OUTLOOK

The Asia-Pacific threat landscape is projected to remain highly volatile through 2025 with the primary risk of armed conflict centered on Taiwan and the South China Sea. North Korea's continued missile development and nuclear posture represent a persistent threat. US-China competition is increasingly institutionalized across military, economic, and technological domains. The most significant near-term flashpoint risk involves incidents at sea between PLA Navy and Coast Guard vessels and those of the Philippines or other claimant states, with potential for rapid escalation.
