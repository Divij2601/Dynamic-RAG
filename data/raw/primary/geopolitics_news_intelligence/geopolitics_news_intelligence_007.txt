NEWS INTELLIGENCE: RED SEA SHIPPING DISRUPTION — ONGOING ASSESSMENT

CLASSIFICATION: MARITIME SECURITY AND ECONOMIC INTELLIGENCE
DATE: 2025
REGION: RED SEA, BABS-EL-MANDEB, SUEZ CANAL CORRIDOR

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Houthi campaign of attacks against commercial shipping in the Red Sea, initiated in November 2023 in solidarity with Palestinians in Gaza, has produced the most significant disruption to global maritime trade routes since the Suez Crisis of 1956. Major shipping companies have rerouted vessels around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10 to 14 days and significant fuel costs to Asia-Europe voyages. The disruption has contributed to freight rate increases of over 200 percent on key routes at peak intensity, created supply chain backlogs, and generated inflationary pressure globally. The US-led Operation Prosperity Guardian has conducted sustained strikes against Houthi launch sites and air defense systems in Yemen but has failed to suppress the threat, which continues to evolve and expand.

ATTACK PATTERNS AND HOUTHI CAPABILITIES

The Houthis have demonstrated an evolving and increasingly sophisticated attack capability. Initial attacks relied primarily on anti-ship missiles and explosive-laden boat drones. The campaign subsequently incorporated ballistic missiles, cruise missiles, and one-way attack aerial drones capable of range exceeding 1,500 kilometers. The Houthis have claimed targeting of vessels linked to Israeli, American, and British interests, though in practice many attacks have targeted vessels with no discernible connection to those countries. The use of commercial satellite imagery and intelligence support from Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps has enabled the Houthis to identify and track vessel targets. Anti-ship ballistic missiles have proven particularly difficult to intercept and have struck several vessels causing significant damage.

ECONOMIC AND SHIPPING INDUSTRY IMPACT

The Suez Canal Authority reported a 60 percent drop in canal transits during the peak period of disruption in early 2024, translating to approximately 7 billion dollars in lost revenue annually for Egypt. The rerouting around Cape of Good Hope has added significant operational costs for shipping companies and created congestion at alternative ports. European automakers have faced parts shortages due to delayed shipments from Asian suppliers. Energy tankers have faced elevated insurance premiums classified under the war risk category. The Baltic Dry Index and container freight indices have shown extreme volatility correlated with attack frequency and effectiveness. Overall, the disruption is estimated to have cost the global economy tens of billions of dollars through higher trade costs and supply chain inefficiencies.

MILITARY RESPONSE AND STRATEGIC DYNAMICS

Operation Prosperity Guardian, led by the United States with British participation and contributions from other allied navies, has conducted sustained air strikes against Houthi missile and drone launchers, radar installations, and command infrastructure in Yemen. Despite significant damage to Houthi military infrastructure, attacks on shipping have continued, demonstrating the group's ability to reconstitute capabilities, potentially with Iranian resupply. The strategic logic of the Houthi campaign serves multiple purposes: solidarity with Gaza, which generates domestic and regional political support; testing of Western military response capabilities; and advancing Iran's broader pressure campaign against Israel and its partners. The Yemen civil war context means there is no ground force option available to Western navies.

INTELLIGENCE ASSESSMENT AND OUTLOOK

The Red Sea shipping disruption is assessed as a persistent threat contingent primarily on the status of the Gaza conflict. Houthi leaders have stated that attacks will cease only upon an end to Israeli military operations in Gaza. Absent a durable Gaza ceasefire, attacks are expected to continue at reduced but still significant frequency. The long-term impact on Suez Canal usage patterns and global trade routing may persist beyond any ceasefire as shipping companies reassess baseline risk. Insurance market changes, including elevated war risk premiums in the region, will create lasting cost increases. The broader lesson for global maritime security is that non-state actors with Iranian support can pose sustained threats to critical maritime chokepoints, a paradigm relevant to the Strait of Hormuz and other key maritime passages.
