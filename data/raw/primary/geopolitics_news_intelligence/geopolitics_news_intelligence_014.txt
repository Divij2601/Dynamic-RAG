POLICY ANALYSIS: EU STRATEGIC AUTONOMY AND DEFENSE

CLASSIFICATION: POLICY AND STRATEGIC INTELLIGENCE ANALYSIS
DATE: 2025
REGION: EUROPEAN UNION, TRANSATLANTIC RELATIONSHIP

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The European Union's pursuit of strategic autonomy in defense and security has accelerated dramatically in response to two converging pressures: Russia's invasion of Ukraine demonstrating the existential nature of the security threat on Europe's eastern border, and the Trump administration's repeated questioning of the US commitment to NATO and European security. The EU has launched a series of initiatives to increase defense industrial capacity, common procurement, and military capability. The European Defence Industrial Strategy, the European Defence Industry Reinforcement through Common Procurement Act, and ongoing discussions about an EU defense bond program represent a qualitative shift in European ambitions. However, significant obstacles remain, including member state sovereignty over defense, divergent threat perceptions, and the challenge of integrating EU defense initiatives with NATO structures.

EU DEFENSE INITIATIVES AND MECHANISMS

The European Defence Agency has seen its budget and scope expanded significantly. The European Defence Fund, providing grants for collaborative defense research and development, has been allocated billions of euros. The EDIRPA mechanism and its successor frameworks have facilitated common procurement of ammunition and air defense systems to replenish stocks drawn down from EU member state inventories to support Ukraine. The Permanent Structured Cooperation framework has enabled a subset of EU members to pursue enhanced defense cooperation in specific capability areas. Commission President Ursula von der Leyen has made defense industrial integration a flagship priority, proposing a dedicated EU Defense Commissioner and a new multi-year defense spending program. The debate over Eurobonds or defense bonds to collectively fund these investments remains politically contentious, particularly with fiscally conservative member states.

TRANSATLANTIC TENSIONS AND BURDEN SHARING

The relationship between EU strategic autonomy and the NATO alliance is a source of ongoing strategic debate. The United States has historically been skeptical of EU defense structures that might duplicate NATO capabilities or exclude non-EU NATO members such as the United Kingdom, Norway, and Turkey. However, the Trump administration's explicit pressure on European allies to spend more and do more for their own defense has paradoxically accelerated European integration ambitions. European leaders including French President Macron and German Chancellor Merz have made explicit arguments for European defense capabilities independent of guaranteed American backing. The UK-EU security relationship is also evolving, with post-Brexit cooperation frameworks under development that could allow UK participation in EU defense programs while maintaining NATO primacy.

INDUSTRIAL AND CAPABILITY CHALLENGES

Europe's defense industrial base has been chronically underfunded relative to US, Russian, and increasingly Chinese defense industries. The surge in demand for ammunition, air defense systems, and ground equipment generated by Ukraine's war has exposed severe capacity limitations. European artillery shell production fell dramatically short of commitments to supply Ukraine. The lead times for increasing production of key systems are measured in years, not months. Several EU member states have concluded that national defense industrial policies must become core elements of industrial strategy, reversing decades of post-Cold War defense budget cuts. France and Germany are attempting to drive convergence on major common procurement programs including tanks, aircraft, and naval systems, but bilateral Franco-German differences have complicated several flagship programs.

INTELLIGENCE ASSESSMENT AND OUTLOOK

EU strategic autonomy is transitioning from a political aspiration to an operational priority, driven by genuine security imperatives rather than merely political preferences for European independence. Progress will be uneven given divergent member state security perceptions, with eastern flank members focused on conventional defense against Russia and Mediterranean members more focused on migration and southern neighborhood instability. The most consequential variable is the trajectory of the US security commitment to Europe under successive administrations. If the United States maintains robust NATO engagement, EU autonomy will complement rather than replace the transatlantic relationship. If the US commitment becomes unreliable, European strategic autonomy will need to become genuine and comprehensive, requiring investments and political decisions that are currently only being partially made.
