GEOPOLITICAL RISK FLASH: MYANMAR COUP ANNIVERSARY ASSESSMENT

CLASSIFICATION: OPEN SOURCE GEOPOLITICAL RISK FLASH
DATE: FEBRUARY 2025 — FOURTH ANNIVERSARY OF FEBRUARY 2021 COUP
REGION: MYANMAR, SOUTHEAST ASIA

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The fourth anniversary of Myanmar's military coup of February 1, 2021 arrives with the Tatmadaw military junta under unprecedented pressure from coordinated resistance forces that have made significant territorial gains across the country. Operation 1027, launched by an alliance of ethnic armed organizations in October 2023, and subsequent resistance operations have resulted in the largest territorial losses by the Tatmadaw since independence, with multiple cities, key border crossings, and significant military installations captured by opposition forces. The National Unity Government, the parallel civilian administration formed in opposition to the junta, has expanded its international diplomatic footprint and financial relationships. Despite these setbacks, the Tatmadaw retains control of Naypyidaw, Yangon, and key Ayeyarwady delta territories, and continues to conduct airstrikes against civilian populations.

MILITARY SITUATION AND RESISTANCE ADVANCES

The Three Brotherhood Alliance — comprising the Ta'ang National Liberation Army, Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army, and Arakan Army — achieved dramatic gains in Shan State and Rakhine State following the launch of Operation 1027. The Arakan Army has consolidated control over most of Rakhine State, including strategically important border areas adjacent to China's Belt and Road projects. The Kachin Independence Army has intensified operations in Kachin State. The People's Defence Force, the military wing of the National Unity Government, has expanded operations nationwide and has demonstrated growing tactical capability. The Tatmadaw's capacity for large ground operations is significantly degraded, forcing reliance on airstrikes that cause heavy civilian casualties and have been condemned internationally.

HUMANITARIAN CRISIS

Myanmar has experienced one of the most severe humanitarian deteriorations in Southeast Asian history since the coup. Over 3 million people are internally displaced, with additional hundreds of thousands as refugees in Thailand, India, China, and Bangladesh. Humanitarian access is severely restricted; the junta has systematically blocked aid deliveries to opposition-held territories. Forced conscription orders issued by the junta have accelerated youth flight from junta-controlled areas. Healthcare and education systems in many regions have functionally collapsed. The banking sector has contracted sharply, with widespread economic deterioration affecting civilians across political divides.

EXTERNAL ACTOR DYNAMICS

China occupies a complex and pivotal position in Myanmar's conflict. Beijing has historically supported the Tatmadaw as a stable partner for its economic interests, including the China-Myanmar Economic Corridor and Kyaukphyu deep-sea port. However, China has shifted toward pragmatic engagement with ethnic armed organizations controlling territory adjacent to its border, particularly the Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army in the Kokang region and the United Wa State Army. Beijing brokered a fragile ceasefire in parts of northern Shan State in January 2024. Russia has continued to supply arms to the junta, including aircraft and artillery. ASEAN's Five-Point Consensus framework has been widely assessed as ineffective, with the junta ignoring its requirements.

INTELLIGENCE ASSESSMENT AND OUTLOOK

The Tatmadaw is assessed as increasingly unable to reverse territorial losses and restore control over major population centers outside core junta-held areas. A junta collapse scenario, while not imminent, has moved from low-probability to plausible within a three to five year horizon. The more likely near-term scenario is continued fragmentation with the country effectively partitioned into junta-controlled, resistance-controlled, and ethnic armed organization-controlled zones. The humanitarian situation will continue to deteriorate. Regional stability implications are significant: a failed state scenario in Myanmar would create massive refugee flows, undermine China's infrastructure investments, and potentially create ungoverned spaces for transnational criminal networks already deeply embedded in border areas.
