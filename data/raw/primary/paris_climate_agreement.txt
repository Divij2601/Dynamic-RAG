THE PARIS CLIMATE AGREEMENT: OVERVIEW

Overview

The Paris Agreement is an international treaty on climate change adopted in 2015. Its central aim is to limit global warming and strengthen the global response to the threat of climate change. It is a landmark agreement because it was the first time that nearly all nations of the world committed to a common framework to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and address the impacts of a changing climate.

Adoption

The Paris Agreement was adopted on December 12, 2015, at the twenty-first Conference of the Parties, known as COP21, of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, held in Paris, France. It entered into force on November 4, 2016, after the required number of countries representing a sufficient share of global emissions had ratified it. The agreement was negotiated under the auspices of the United Nations and has been joined by the large majority of the world's countries.

Goals

The central goal of the Paris Agreement is to hold the increase in the global average temperature to well below two degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, while pursuing efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 degrees Celsius. Scientists have warned that exceeding these thresholds would substantially increase the risks of severe climate impacts, including extreme weather, sea-level rise, and ecosystem disruption.

How It Works

Unlike earlier climate agreements, the Paris Agreement relies on a system of nationally determined contributions. Each country sets its own targets for reducing emissions and adapting to climate change and is expected to update and strengthen these targets over time. The agreement establishes a process for countries to report on their progress and a global stocktake to assess collective progress toward the long-term goals. The approach is sometimes described as bottom-up, because it relies on voluntary national commitments rather than legally binding international emission limits.

Key Elements

The agreement addresses not only the reduction of emissions, known as mitigation, but also adaptation to the effects of climate change and the provision of finance to help developing countries respond. Developed countries committed to mobilizing financial resources to assist developing nations. The agreement also recognizes the importance of addressing loss and damage associated with the adverse effects of climate change.

United States Participation

The participation of the United States, one of the world's largest emitters, has fluctuated with changes in administration. The United States joined the agreement under one administration, announced its withdrawal under another, rejoined, and again moved to withdraw, reflecting deep domestic political divisions over climate policy. These shifts have raised questions about the long-term consistency of major-power commitments to the agreement.

Significance and Challenges

The Paris Agreement is widely regarded as a historic achievement in international cooperation, establishing a near-universal framework for climate action. However, it faces significant challenges. Critics note that national commitments to date are collectively insufficient to meet the temperature goals, and that the agreement lacks strong enforcement mechanisms. Supporters argue that its flexible, inclusive design is the most realistic way to secure broad participation and to ratchet up ambition over time.
