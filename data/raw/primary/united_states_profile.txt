UNITED STATES OF AMERICA: COUNTRY AND GEOPOLITICAL PROFILE

Overview

The United States of America is a federal republic of fifty states located primarily in North America. With a population of roughly 335 million people and the largest economy in the world by nominal gross domestic product, the United States has been the dominant global power since the middle of the twentieth century. Its capital is Washington, D.C., and its largest city is New York City. As of 2026, the president is Donald Trump, who returned to office for a second non-consecutive term following the 2024 election.

Government and Political System

The United States is a constitutional federal republic with a presidential system. Power is divided among three branches: the executive, led by the president; the legislative, consisting of a bicameral Congress made up of the Senate and the House of Representatives; and the judicial, headed by the Supreme Court. The Constitution, ratified in 1788, is the supreme law of the land and is the oldest written national constitution still in use. The system of federalism reserves significant powers to the individual states, while a system of checks and balances limits the authority of any single branch.

The two dominant political parties are the Democratic Party and the Republican Party. Federal elections are held every two years, with presidential elections every four years. The president is limited to two elected terms under the Twenty-Second Amendment.

Economy

The United States has a highly developed, diversified, market-based economy. It is a global leader in technology, finance, aerospace, pharmaceuticals, entertainment, and higher education. The U.S. dollar is the world's primary reserve currency, giving the country substantial influence over global finance. Major economic hubs include New York for finance, Silicon Valley in California for technology, and Houston for energy. The country is among the world's largest producers of oil and natural gas, having become a net energy exporter in recent years.

Foreign Policy and Military

The United States maintains the most powerful and best-funded military in the world, with armed forces organized under the Department of Defense and headquartered at the Pentagon. It operates a global network of military bases and alliances. The United States is a founding member of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) and maintains security alliances in the Indo-Pacific, including treaties with Japan, South Korea, Australia, and the Philippines.

American foreign policy in the twenty-first century has focused increasingly on strategic competition with China, support for Ukraine against Russian invasion, tensions in the Middle East, and the management of global trade relationships. The country is a permanent member of the United Nations Security Council with veto power, one of five such members.

Historical Background

The United States declared independence from Great Britain on July 4, 1776, and secured it through the American Revolutionary War. The nation expanded westward across the nineteenth century, endured a civil war from 1861 to 1865 that ended slavery, and emerged as an industrial power by 1900. Its decisive participation in both World War I and World War II established it as a superpower. During the Cold War from 1947 to 1991, the United States led the Western bloc against the Soviet Union. After the collapse of the Soviet Union, the United States entered a period of unrivaled global dominance often described as a unipolar moment.

Contemporary Role

In the 2020s, the United States remains the central node of the Western alliance system and the world's foremost economic and military power, though it increasingly operates in a multipolar environment shaped by the rise of China, the assertiveness of Russia, and the growing weight of emerging economies. Debates over trade policy, tariffs, immigration, and the appropriate scope of American commitments abroad are central features of its contemporary politics.
