ANTI-IMMIGRATION SENTIMENT AND EUROPEAN ELECTIONS 2019-2025

OVERVIEW

Anti-immigration sentiment has been among the most powerful electoral forces shaping European politics in the 2019-2025 period. Far-right and hard-right parties that made immigration their central issue performed exceptionally well in national elections across Sweden, Italy, France, Germany, the Netherlands, Finland, Austria, and Slovakia, as well as in the 2024 European Parliament elections. This electoral pattern reflected both genuine public concern about migration levels and skilled political exploitation of economic and cultural anxieties. Disentangling the specific role of immigration concern from broader societal anxieties requires examination of polling data, voting behavior research, and the policy responses these dynamics generated.

THE SCALE OF PUBLIC CONCERN

Polling data consistently showed immigration as one of the top issues for European voters throughout this period. Eurobarometer surveys found that "immigration" ranked among the top two concerns cited by EU citizens in 2019-2022, competing with economic concerns and climate change. The 2022 survey, conducted amid the arrival of over 4 million Ukrainian refugees, showed a complicated pattern: sympathy for Ukrainian refugees was very high across Europe, but broader concerns about irregular migration and asylum seekers from the Middle East, Africa, and Afghanistan remained elevated. European respondents made distinctions between different migration flows — demonstrating that sentiment was not simply xenophobic but responsive to perceived differences in refugee characteristics, legal status, and integration prospects.

ELECTORAL RESULTS AND PARTY PERFORMANCE

The electoral translation of anti-immigration sentiment was dramatic. In Sweden, the Sweden Democrats — a party founded with neo-Nazi roots that had transformed into a mainstream hard-right party with immigration as its core issue — became the second largest party in the 2022 elections and provided parliamentary support to a centre-right government. In Italy, Giorgia Meloni won the 2022 elections with Fratelli d'Italia, promising strict enforcement of maritime pushbacks against Mediterranean crossings. In the Netherlands, Geert Wilders' Party for Freedom (PVV) won the November 2023 election — the largest parliamentary party in the Netherlands, an unprecedented result for an explicitly anti-Islam, anti-immigration platform. In Germany, the AfD consistently polled above 20 percent through 2024. The 2024 European Parliament elections produced the largest hard-right and far-right groupings in the Parliament's history, with the ECR and ID groups combined holding over 130 seats.

DRIVERS: ECONOMIC ANXIETY, CULTURAL THREAT, AND ELITE DISCONNECT

Research by political scientists including Cas Mudde, Pippa Norris, and Ronald Inglehart identified multiple interacting drivers of anti-immigration electoral success. Economic anxiety — competition for housing, social services, and low-skilled employment — played a role, particularly in regions experiencing deindustrialization. But cultural threat perception — the sense that rapid demographic change threatened national identity, language, and social norms — was often a more powerful driver, particularly among voters who were not personally economically vulnerable. Equally important was the perception of elite dismissiveness: mainstream parties' apparent unwillingness to acknowledge legitimate concerns about integration, crime statistics (particularly around sexual violence incidents in Germany and Sweden), and religious cultural conflicts created a vacuum that anti-immigration parties filled by offering to say what established parties would not.

POLICY RESPONSES AND THE MAINSTREAMING DYNAMIC

European governments' policy responses to anti-immigration electoral pressure accelerated a "mainstreaming" dynamic: center-right and even center-left parties adopted stricter immigration language and policies to compete with the hard-right fringe. Denmark's Social Democrats under Mette Frederiksen arguably pioneered this approach, combining left-wing economic policies with extremely restrictive immigration and asylum stances. The EU-level 2023 New Pact on Migration and Asylum, which introduced mandatory responsibility-sharing and accelerated border procedures, reflected political acknowledgment that the previous system had failed to generate public confidence. European leaders were simultaneously competing with far-right parties and trying to demonstrate that mainstream politics could address immigration concerns. The resulting policy convergence toward restriction moved the Overton Window significantly rightward on migration from 2019 to 2025.

CONCLUSION

Anti-immigration sentiment's electoral power in Europe through 2025 reflects genuine societal tensions around demographic change, integration challenges, and cultural anxiety that mainstream politics long avoided addressing directly. The political consequences — far-right normalization, EU policy shifts, and domestic political transformation in multiple member states — are substantial. Going forward, the challenge for European democracies is to develop immigration and integration policies that are both operationally effective and publicly perceived as fair and controlled, without surrendering to ethnic nationalist framing that threatens fundamental rights. The polling data suggests Europeans are not uniformly anti-immigration but want governance they trust to manage flows and integration competently — a demand that remains more legitimately addressable than the extreme positions that have captured the debate.
