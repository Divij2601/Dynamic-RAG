ANTI-AMERICANISM TRENDS: GLOBAL PEW RESEARCH FINDINGS 2018-2025

OVERVIEW

Anti-Americanism — defined as sustained negative sentiment toward the United States as a country, its foreign policy, or its cultural influence — has been a persistent thread in global public opinion polling. Pew Research Center has tracked favorable and unfavorable ratings of the United States across dozens of countries for over two decades. Between 2018 and 2025, these ratings underwent dramatic swings tied to presidential transitions, foreign policy crises, and shifting perceptions of American democratic stability.

TRUMP ERA AND THE 2017-2020 DECLINE

The first Trump administration triggered a measurable collapse in U.S. favorability across traditional allies. Pew's 2018 Global Attitudes Survey showed median favorable ratings of the U.S. dropping to 50 percent among surveyed nations, down from 64 percent in 2015 under Obama. Germany registered some of the sharpest drops, with favorable views falling from 57 percent to 30 percent between 2016 and 2018. France, Sweden, Canada, and Australia all recorded double-digit declines. Pew consistently found that disapproval of U.S. leadership was highest among countries with strong democratic institutions and longstanding alliance relationships — a counterintuitive finding suggesting that shared values, when perceived to be violated, produce more acute disappointment than indifference.

THE BIDEN RECOVERY AND ITS LIMITS

The 2021 transition to the Biden administration partially reversed these trends. Pew's 2021 Spring survey found U.S. favorability recovering to 62 percent median across allied nations. Western European confidence in the U.S. president specifically surged, with 75 percent of Germans and 84 percent of French respondents expressing confidence in Biden — compared to single digits under Trump. However, the 2021 chaotic withdrawal from Afghanistan dented the recovery, particularly in countries with troops deployed there. By 2022, the U.S. had regained credibility as a leader in supporting Ukraine after Russia's full-scale invasion, temporarily boosting favorable ratings in Eastern Europe. Poland, Estonia, and Lithuania registered some of the highest U.S. favorability scores ever recorded, exceeding 80 percent.

STRUCTURAL ANTI-AMERICANISM IN THE GLOBAL SOUTH

In the Middle East, Africa, and parts of Asia and Latin America, anti-Americanism proved more structural and policy-driven. Pew data from 2023 showed U.S. favorability at only 28 percent in Jordan, 32 percent in Turkey, and 36 percent in Pakistan. These persistently low numbers correlated with perceptions of U.S. support for Israel, historical interventions, and drone strike programs. In Sub-Saharan Africa, views were more mixed but trending downward. Nigerian favorable opinion fell from 69 percent in 2013 to 51 percent in 2024. In Latin America, persistent concern about U.S. immigration enforcement policy and historical interference in sovereign governance kept ratings moderate even in countries with democratic alignment.

THE SECOND TRUMP TERM AND 2025 MEASUREMENTS

The return of Donald Trump to the presidency in January 2025 produced another sharp deterioration in allied-nation sentiment. Preliminary polling through spring 2025 showed that traditional allies were registering deep concern about U.S. commitment to NATO, trade tariff escalation, and democratic backsliding. A YouGov-Cambridge survey released in April 2025 found that only 26 percent of British respondents and 18 percent of German respondents held a favorable view of U.S. foreign policy direction under Trump's second term. Pew's 2025 Global Attitudes survey, expected for mid-year release, was anticipated to show the steepest declines in allied confidence since the Iraq War era. Global South ambivalence remained, but some countries interpreted U.S. withdrawal from multilateralism as creating space for their own strategic autonomy.

CONCLUSION

Anti-Americanism is not monolithic. Pew and affiliated polling organizations consistently distinguish between dislike of specific U.S. policies versus distrust of American people and culture. Even at low points, American pop culture, universities, and technology companies retain admiration internationally. The structural drivers of anti-Americanism — military interventionism, perceived double standards on democracy and human rights, trade coercion, and cultural imperialism claims — persist across administrations. However, leadership style and foreign policy posture demonstrably shift short-term ratings by margins large enough to reshape diplomatic relationships.
