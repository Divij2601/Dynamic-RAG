NATIONALISM INDEX: RISING TRENDS 2020-2025

OVERVIEW

Nationalism — the political belief that the nation is the primary unit of collective identity and that national interests should take precedence over international obligations — has been rising measurably across multiple regions between 2020 and 2025. This period encompassed major catalysts: COVID-19 resurrected border controls and supply chain nationalism; Russia's invasion of Ukraine redefined national sovereignty debates in Europe; China-Western competition boosted economic nationalism and technology decoupling arguments; and far-right electoral victories in multiple democracies normalized nationalist rhetoric in mainstream discourse. Researchers including the V-Dem Institute, the Democracy Index (EIU), and Freedom House documented these trends, as did commercial political risk firms and academic nationalism scholars.

MEASUREMENT APPROACHES AND INDICATORS

Defining and measuring nationalism creates methodological challenges: the term encompasses ethnic nationalism, civic nationalism, economic nationalism, and cultural nationalism, each with distinct political expressions. The V-Dem Institute's Nationalist Politics Index tracks the degree to which major political parties and governments emphasize national identity over other political values. By this measure, nationalist party strength in legislatures increased in 28 of 48 tracked democracies between 2019 and 2024. The Economist Intelligence Unit's Democracy Index, while primarily measuring democratic quality, captures related phenomena through its "participation and civic culture" dimension, where civic nationalism sometimes supports or undermines democratic norms. Pew Research's tracking of "national pride" and the related question of whether national culture is superior to others provides a complementary survey-based measure.

EUROPE: FAR RIGHT AND MAINSTREAM CONVERGENCE

European nationalism demonstrated both electoral gains and mainstreaming in 2020-2025. In France, Marine Le Pen's Rassemblement National reached the second round of presidential elections in 2022 and performed strongly in 2027 campaign positioning. In Italy, Giorgia Meloni's Fratelli d'Italia — a party with postfascist roots — won the September 2022 elections and governed as prime minister, marking a significant normalization of hard-right nationalism in a founding EU member. Germany's AfD (Alternative für Deutschland) reached 20-22 percent in national polls by 2024 before various controversies, but sustained its presence. Sweden's Democrats entered government as coalition support partners. The common thread across these movements was a fusion of cultural protectionism, anti-immigration sentiment, Euroscepticism (or EU reform demands), and rejection of perceived globalist elite consensus.

ASIA: COMPETITIVE NATIONALISM SPIRALS

In Asia, nationalism rose in forms specific to regional dynamics. In China, Xi Jinping's governance was characterized by explicit promotion of "national rejuvenation" and Wolf Warrior diplomatic assertiveness, reflecting and reinforcing popular nationalist sentiment, especially among urban youth. Chinese nationalism showed a clear spike following COVID-related international criticism and perceived Western attempts to constrain China's rise. In India, the BJP's Hindu nationalist platform won significant electoral victories in 2019 and 2024, with Prime Minister Modi's personal popularity closely tied to national pride narratives. Japan's nationalism, historically more restrained by constitutional and historical constraints, showed modest increases, particularly around historical memory disputes with Korea and China. South Korea's own nationalism around Japan relations created reciprocal tensions. The Taiwan identity shift — discussed in a separate document — represented a distinct form of civic nationalism building.

ECONOMIC NATIONALISM AND POLICY CONVERGENCE

Perhaps the most consequential nationalism trend of 2020-2025 was economic nationalism's adoption by governments across the political spectrum. The United States' CHIPS Act, Inflation Reduction Act, and Buy American provisions under Biden represented deliberate industrial policy nationalism that would have been dismissed as economic heterodoxy a decade earlier. The EU's Strategic Autonomy agenda, Chinese "dual circulation" economic strategy, and India's "Atmanirbhar Bharat" (self-reliant India) initiative all reflected a global convergence toward economic nationalism driven by supply chain vulnerability lessons from COVID-19 and strategic competition anxieties. This form of nationalism transcended traditional left-right divisions, creating unusual alliances between economic nationalists from different political traditions.

CONCLUSION

The nationalism trends of 2020-2025 are neither uniform nor uniformly threatening to the international order. Economic nationalism has been partially normalized and partially institutionalized. Cultural and ethnic nationalism remains more politically volatile, particularly in multiethnic societies where it tends to produce zero-sum conflict. The period's lesson is that nationalism is not a temporary aberration but a persistent structural feature of political systems under stress. Globalization's distributional failures, cultural displacement anxieties, and elite-mass disconnections created the demand conditions that nationalism's entrepreneurs successfully exploited across diverse contexts. Managing these impulses within democratic and multilateral frameworks — rather than either suppressing or fully accommodating them — remains the central political challenge of the era.
