GLOBAL SOUTH AMBIVALENCE TOWARD THE WESTERN-RUSSIA CONFLICT

OVERVIEW

When Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, Western governments expected broad global condemnation. The United Nations General Assembly vote demanding Russia's withdrawal passed with 141 in favor — but a significant bloc of Global South nations abstained, voted against, or found ways to signal neutrality despite public alignment with sovereignty principles. This pattern of ambivalence, captured in diplomatic positions and confirmed by public opinion research, reflected deep structural grievances, strategic calculations, and historical memory that distinguished Global South perspectives from those in Western democracies. Understanding this ambivalence is essential for interpreting the fracturing of the so-called "rules-based international order."

DIPLOMATIC PATTERNS AND ABSTENTION POLITICS

The March 2022 UN General Assembly resolution condemning Russia's invasion received 141 votes in favor, 5 against, and 35 abstentions. The abstaining nations included India, China, Pakistan, South Africa, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, and others that collectively represented over 3.5 billion people. A subsequent October 2022 resolution demanding Russian withdrawal from occupied territories saw similar patterns. These abstentions were not diplomatic accidents — they reflected genuine political calculations. India maintained defense and energy relationships with Russia while also pursuing partnerships with the United States. South Africa cited historical solidarity with non-aligned movement principles. Brazil under Lula after January 2023 sought mediator status while refusing arms transfers to either side. These positions were broadly supported domestically in each country.

PUBLIC OPINION SURVEYS IN THE GLOBAL SOUTH

Survey data from the Global South on the Russia-Ukraine war revealed complex patterns. An April 2022 YouGov survey of 18 countries found that awareness of the conflict was nearly universal — 91 percent in Nigeria, 87 percent in India — but that assignment of blame and policy preferences diverged sharply from Western populations. Only 40 percent of Indian respondents believed Russia was "mainly responsible" for the war, compared to 90 percent in the United Kingdom. In Nigeria, only 34 percent held Russia primarily responsible. Multiple polls found that significant majorities in Global South countries supported a negotiated settlement, even one involving territorial concessions by Ukraine, while Western publics were more likely to support continued military resistance. A 2023 Afrobarometer survey found that most Africans saw both the U.S./NATO and Russia as contributing to the conflict's origins.

HISTORICAL GRIEVANCES AND COLONIAL MEMORY

A recurring theme in qualitative research and expert analysis was the role of historical memory in shaping Global South ambivalence. Many surveyed populations in Asia and Africa cited Western interventions in Iraq, Libya, Syria, and Afghanistan as undermining Western moral authority in condemning Russia. The perception of double standards — that civilian casualties in the Middle East or Africa attracted less Western outrage than those in Ukraine — was widespread and reinforced by local media framing. African leaders including those from Senegal, Ethiopia, and Uganda made explicit statements connecting their abstentions to unresolved colonial grievances and perceived unequal treatment of African refugees relative to Ukrainian ones. This historical framing was politically effective domestically even if it strained Western diplomatic relationships.

ENERGY, FOOD, AND ECONOMIC INTERESTS

Beyond symbolic politics, concrete economic interests shaped Global South ambivalence. Many developing nations were more directly harmed by the war's economic consequences — particularly the grain and fertilizer supply disruptions and energy price spikes — than they were invested in its ideological dimensions. Countries like Egypt, which imports over 80 percent of its wheat from Russia and Ukraine, were primarily focused on food security rather than sovereignty norms. The Black Sea Grain Initiative and subsequent breakdown illustrated how war impacts on food supply generated ambivalence: Global South nations wanted the war to end for pragmatic reasons without necessarily taking sides on its legitimacy. Meanwhile, Russian energy exports continued flowing to India and China at discounted prices, creating tangible economic incentives for non-confrontational postures.

CONCLUSION

Global South ambivalence toward the Russia-Ukraine war reveals the limitations of Western-centric narratives about a unified international community. Rather than reflecting pro-Russian sympathy, most ambivalent nations sought to preserve economic relationships, honor non-aligned historical traditions, and exercise strategic autonomy in an increasingly multipolar world. Public opinion in these regions supported peace over continued conflict and was skeptical of both sides' narratives. This ambivalence has had lasting consequences for the post-war multilateral order, signaling that future crises will require more inclusive coalition-building approaches if Western nations seek genuine global consensus rather than performative majorities.
