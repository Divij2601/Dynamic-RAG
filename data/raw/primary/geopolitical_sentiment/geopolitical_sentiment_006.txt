NATO APPROVAL RATINGS: EASTERN EUROPE VS. WESTERN EUROPE DIVERGENCE

OVERVIEW

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization's public approval ratings revealed a striking geographical divergence in the 2020-2025 period. While the alliance's eastern flank — Poland, the Baltic states, Romania, and the Czech Republic — showed some of the highest NATO support ever recorded, several Western European member states demonstrated more ambivalent or declining enthusiasm. Russia's 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine dramatically accelerated this divergence, reinforcing NATO as an existential shield for eastern members while forcing a more complex reconsideration of alliance costs and commitments in the west. Understanding this gradient is essential for assessing NATO's internal cohesion and its capacity for unified strategic action.

EASTERN EUROPE: RECORD HIGH APPROVAL

Countries on NATO's eastern flank have historically demonstrated the strongest support for the alliance, given their proximity to Russia and Soviet-era memory. After February 2022, this support intensified markedly. Polls conducted by the Baltic Survey, GLOBSEC, and the German Marshall Fund found that Poland registered 90 percent or higher NATO support through 2022-2024. Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania — which share borders with Russia or its ally Belarus — consistently showed 85-92 percent favorable views of NATO membership. Romania and Slovakia also showed elevated support, as did Finland and Sweden, which joined NATO in 2023 and 2024 respectively following the invasion. Finnish support for NATO membership had been below 30 percent for most of the post-Cold War era but jumped to over 75 percent by spring 2022 — one of the most remarkable public opinion reversals in NATO's history.

WESTERN EUROPE: AMBIVALENCE AND DEFENSE SPENDING DEBATES

Western European attitudes were more complicated. Traditional NATO members including Germany, France, Italy, and Spain maintained positive overall views of the alliance but showed greater internal divisions. German support for NATO membership remained high at around 75 percent, but support for increasing defense spending — required by the NATO 2 percent of GDP benchmark — was more contested. Only after the 2022 invasion did German public opinion swing toward accepting the necessity of increased military expenditure, enabling the government's "Zeitenwende" defense spending commitment. In France, President Macron's "brain death" comment about NATO in 2019 had resonated with portions of the public who questioned U.S. reliability, and Gaullist sentiment for European strategic autonomy remained influential. Italy and Spain showed the lowest NATO approval among founding members, with significant domestic constituencies skeptical of military commitments.

DEFENSE SPENDING AS PUBLIC OPINION PROXY

The NATO debate often crystallized around the 2 percent GDP defense spending target. Eastern European nations not only met this target but often exceeded it — Poland committed to spending 4 percent of GDP on defense by 2023, and the Baltic states similarly exceeded the benchmark. Western European governments historically underperformed the target and faced domestic political constraints on increasing military budgets. Public opinion surveys showed that willingness to pay for defense correlated strongly with perceived threat proximity: populations closer to Russia were more willing to accept defense spending costs, while populations in Western Europe showed more resistance to direct taxation or reallocation needed to fund increases. The German Marshall Fund's annual Transatlantic Trends survey consistently found this east-west gradient on willingness to use military force and to increase defense budgets.

THE TRUMP FACTOR AND ARTICLE 5 CREDIBILITY

A major complication for NATO's approval ratings landscape was U.S. political uncertainty. President Trump's statements during his 2024 campaign — including suggesting he would "not protect" NATO members who didn't meet defense spending targets and would "encourage Russia to do whatever the hell it wants" — generated acute anxiety in Eastern Europe and cold fury in Western Europe. Polls conducted in NATO countries after these statements found that confidence in U.S. Article 5 commitments dropped sharply. In Germany, only 31 percent of respondents in a 2025 survey expressed confidence that the United States would defend a NATO ally under attack. Ironically, Trump's rhetoric may have unified Eastern and Western European NATO opinion around the need for greater European defense independence — both agreed the U.S. could not be fully relied upon, though for different reasons.

CONCLUSION

NATO's approval ratings across member states in 2025 revealed an alliance of genuinely shared threat perceptions in the east and more varied strategic cultures in the west. The Russia-Ukraine war transformed NATO from a Cold War legacy institution into a live security guarantee in the minds of Eastern European publics. Managing the defense spending burden-sharing debate, maintaining U.S. commitment under political uncertainty, and developing coherent European strategic autonomy without fracturing the alliance remain the central challenges visible in the approval rating data. NATO's continued relevance depends on demonstrating that its eastern and western members share not just a security umbrella but a sufficiently common strategic vision.
