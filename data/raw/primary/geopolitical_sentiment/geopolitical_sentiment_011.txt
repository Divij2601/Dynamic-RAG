AUTHORITARIANISM APPEAL: THE ECONOMIC PERFORMANCE NARRATIVE

OVERVIEW

A persistent strand in comparative political science examines why authoritarian systems attract public support and what role economic performance plays in that appeal. The "performance legitimacy" thesis holds that authoritarian governments can sustain public acquiescence or active support through delivering economic growth, stability, and national prestige, even in the absence of democratic accountability. This thesis has been tested extensively against empirical data on China, Singapore, Russia, Gulf monarchies, and other non-democratic systems through 2025. The findings are nuanced: economic performance correlates with authoritarianism's popular legitimacy but does not fully explain it, and economic downturns test the thesis in ways that reveal its limits.

CHINA: THE CORE CASE STUDY

China represents the most widely studied case of authoritarian performance legitimacy. From 1978 to 2020, China's GDP growth averaged approximately 9 percent annually, lifting hundreds of millions from poverty and creating the largest middle class in history. Surveys conducted in China by Pew Research (before its surveys were suspended), the Ash Center at Harvard, and independent researchers consistently found high satisfaction with the central government — figures of 80-90 percent approval in national samples, with urban and rural variations. The Ash Center's Edward Cunningham and colleagues tracked Chinese citizen satisfaction from 2003 to 2016, finding that national government approval remained consistently high while local government ratings were more variable. Xi Jinping's consolidation of power from 2012 onward was accompanied by anti-corruption campaigns that actually increased popular approval, at least in the short term.

COVID-19 AND THE LIMITS OF PERFORMANCE LEGITIMACY

The COVID-19 pandemic subjected performance legitimacy to stress testing. China's initial response — characterized by suppression of early warnings and delayed disclosure — generated domestic frustration, but subsequent mass lockdown and vaccination efforts restored a narrative of state capacity. However, the Zero COVID policy extended through 2022 generated protests in November of that year — among the most significant public demonstrations since Tiananmen 1989 — indicating that even high-performing authoritarian systems face legitimacy limits when performance failures become undeniable and concentrated. The subsequent abrupt abandonment of Zero COVID without public consultation revealed the arbitrary nature of policy-making, creating a cognitive dissonance between performance narratives and the visible confusion of governance decisions.

GULF MONARCHIES AND PERFORMANCE LEGITIMACY

Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar offer alternative performance legitimacy models where oil wealth funds welfare states, subsidized housing, and citizen employment in ways that generate genuine popular satisfaction. Survey data available from rare independent polls and academic studies in the Gulf consistently found high reported satisfaction with government performance — though the absence of genuine political freedom makes it difficult to distinguish expressed satisfaction from socially desirable responses. Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's Vision 2030 economic diversification agenda was accompanied by deliberate social liberalization — women's driving rights, entertainment development, religious police restrictions — that expanded the regime's appeal among younger Saudis. A 2024 YouGov survey conducted in Saudi Arabia found 78 percent of respondents expressing optimism about the country's direction — figures that reflected genuine optimism about economic transformation even absent democratic accountability.

RUSSIA AND THE COLLAPSE OF PERFORMANCE LEGITIMACY

Russia under Vladimir Putin illustrates performance legitimacy's vulnerability. Putin's 2000s popularity rested heavily on economic improvement following the chaotic 1990s — rising oil prices enabled wage increases, pension improvements, and a renewed sense of national dignity. Levada Center surveys documented approval ratings consistently above 60 percent through 2011. However, the 2014 Crimea annexation and subsequent sanctions created an alternative nationalist legitimacy that partially substituted for economic performance as oil prices fell. By 2022, the invasion of Ukraine triggered a "rally around the flag" effect that boosted Putin's approval to above 80 percent in Levada Center polls, though the reliability of this data under wartime conditions and the absence of genuine political alternatives is questionable. Russia's economic contraction under sanctions, military manpower losses, and long-term isolation costs were creating conditions for a future legitimacy crisis that economic performance narratives could not indefinitely defer.

CONCLUSION

The economic performance narrative is a genuine pillar of authoritarian legitimacy but not a sufficient explanation for it. Performance legitimacy operates alongside nationalist sentiment, elite coalition management, repression, and historical comparisons. When economic performance falters, authoritarian systems do not automatically collapse — they shift to alternative legitimacy narratives, intensify repression, or manufacture external threats. The comparative evidence through 2025 suggests that economic performance can sustain authoritarian systems through good economic times but creates hidden fragility: when growth slows, governments lack the institutional capacity for peaceful policy correction that democratic systems provide, making legitimacy crises sharper and harder to manage when they eventually arrive.
