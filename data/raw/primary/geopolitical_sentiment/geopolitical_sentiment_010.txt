DEMOCRACY SATISFACTION DEFICIT AMONG YOUNG POPULATIONS: GLOBAL TRENDS

OVERVIEW

Young people worldwide are showing unprecedented levels of dissatisfaction with democracy — not as a preferred system of government, but as it actually functions in their countries. This democracy satisfaction deficit, distinct from anti-democratic sentiment, has been documented across mature democracies and newer ones alike. The Centre for the Future of Democracy at Cambridge University, Pew Research Center, Gallup, and the World Values Survey have all produced data showing that younger generations (generally defined as those born after 1990) express higher disillusionment with democratic performance than any previous generation at comparable life stages. Understanding the drivers and implications of this sentiment is crucial for democratic resilience in the 2025-2030 period.

CAMBRIDGE RESEARCH: A GLOBAL MEASUREMENT FRAMEWORK

The landmark 2020 study "A Crisis of Democratic Legitimacy" by Roberto Stefan Foa and Yascha Mounk, followed by expanded Cambridge Democracy Dashboard research, provided the most comprehensive global measurement of democratic satisfaction. Analyzing data from over 4 million respondents across 160 countries from 1973 to 2020, the research found that millennials and Generation Z showed dramatically lower satisfaction with democracy than previous generations at the same age, and that this gap was largest in wealthy, established democracies — precisely the countries where democratic performance was ostensibly best. By 2024, Cambridge's updated datasets showed that satisfaction with democracy in the United States had fallen to 50 percent among all age groups but was below 40 percent among 18-34 year olds — historically low figures.

DRIVERS OF YOUTH DISSATISFACTION

Multiple studies identified consistent drivers of youth democratic dissatisfaction. Economic grievance was primary: housing unaffordability, student debt burdens, precarious employment, and stagnant intergenerational wealth transfer created an economic environment in which young people felt the democratic system was not delivering for them. A 2023 Gallup/Wellcome Trust survey found that 63 percent of 18-35 year olds globally agreed that "the economy is rigged for the wealthy." Climate anxiety was a second major driver: young people expressed particular frustration that democratic systems seemed structurally incapable of decisive action on climate change, reinforcing perceptions of institutional inadequacy. Political polarization and perceived corruption were also consistently cited — the sense that representative institutions served partisan interests and elite donors rather than the general public eroded systemic trust.

REGIONAL VARIATIONS AND UNEXPECTED PATTERNS

The youth democracy deficit was not uniform across regions. In Eastern Europe, countries that had experienced Soviet occupation — Poland, Czech Republic, Estonia — showed higher youth commitment to democracy, with living memory of authoritarianism serving as a reference point. In Latin America, where many democracies are younger and had experienced military rule within living memory of older generations, youth dissatisfaction was high but often focused on corruption and inequality rather than democratic values per se. The most striking finding was in established Western democracies: United States, United Kingdom, France, Germany, and Australia all showed youth dissatisfaction significantly above national averages, and rising over time. A 2024 Pew survey found that only 27 percent of U.S. adults aged 18-29 believed the U.S. political system was "working well" — compared to 52 percent of those over 65.

AUTHORITARIAN OPENNESS AND THE PERFORMANCE QUESTION

The satisfaction deficit created measurable openness to non-democratic alternatives among youth. A YouGov survey conducted across Western democracies in 2022 found that 35 percent of 18-24 year olds in the United States agreed that "having a strong leader who doesn't have to worry about elections" could be "good for the country in certain circumstances." Similar findings emerged in France, Germany, and the United Kingdom. Critically, however, these expressions of openness to alternatives were highly conditional: they correlated with economic frustration and institutional distrust, not with principled authoritarian preference. When presented with actual authoritarian outcomes — restricted speech, surveillance, political imprisonment — the same populations strongly rejected them. The data suggests volatility and frustration more than committed anti-democratic ideology.

CONCLUSION

The youth democracy satisfaction deficit of 2025 represents a serious but not fatal challenge to democratic systems. Young people remain committed to democratic values while expressing deep frustration with democratic performance. This creates both risk and opportunity: the risk is that persistent performance failures allow authoritarian populists to channel frustration into system-delegitimizing politics; the opportunity is that the demand for better democratic performance could drive genuine institutional renewal. Housing, economic opportunity, climate action, and reduced corruption — the core grievance issues — are addressable through better democratic governance. The deficit is, in important respects, a feedback signal that democratic systems need to earn the trust of their youngest citizens through demonstrable performance rather than inherited legitimacy.
