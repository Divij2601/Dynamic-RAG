INDIA'S GLOBAL FAVORABLE RATINGS RISE: 2020-2025

OVERVIEW

India's international image underwent a significant positive transformation during the period from 2020 to 2025. Driven by economic growth, diplomatic assertiveness, hosting of the G20 presidency in 2023, and a perceived role as a bridge between the West and the Global South, India's favorability ratings improved across multiple regions. This rise was not without complexity — concerns about democratic backsliding under Prime Minister Narendra Modi's government coexisted with admiration for India's economic trajectory and global strategic positioning. Multiple polling organizations documented both dimensions, painting a picture of a country whose global standing grew substantially even as its domestic political freedoms faced external scrutiny.

ECONOMIC NARRATIVE AND GLOBAL PERCEPTION

India's emergence as the world's most populous nation (surpassing China in 2023), combined with being the fastest-growing major economy, created a compelling narrative for international observers. The International Monetary Fund repeatedly cited India as a "bright spot" in an otherwise sluggish global economy, with growth rates of 7-8 percent annually through 2024. This economic story was widely covered internationally and contributed to favorable impressions. A 2024 Pew Research survey found that a median of 46 percent across surveyed nations held favorable views of India — a notable improvement from 38 percent in 2019. In the United States, India's favorability reached 61 percent, the highest since Pew began tracking. In Japan, it stood at 72 percent — reflecting both economic admiration and strategic convergence on China concerns.

G20 PRESIDENCY AND DIPLOMATIC VISIBILITY

India's G20 presidency in 2023 provided an unprecedented platform for soft power projection. The New Delhi summit in September 2023 was attended by almost all G20 leaders (Russian President Putin and Chinese President Xi Jinping were notable absences) and resulted in the historic inclusion of the African Union as a permanent member. Prime Minister Modi's management of the summit — achieving a consensus declaration despite the Ukraine war divide — was widely praised and received significant positive international media coverage. Pew's post-G20 2023 survey found favorable views of India ticked up in most major surveyed countries in the months following the summit. India's framing as a "Vishwabandhu" (friend of the world) aligned with global public appetite for inclusive, non-confrontational multilateral leadership.

DEMOCRACY CONCERNS AND DUAL-TRACK PERCEPTIONS

The rise in India's favorable ratings coexisted with documented concern about democratic health under the BJP government. Freedom House downgraded India from "Free" to "Partly Free" in 2021, and the Varieties of Democracy (V-Dem) Institute classified India as an "electoral autocracy." Press freedom indices placed India in the bottom third globally. Pew's own 2023 data showed a split: while overall India favorability improved, specific questions about respect for individual freedoms and press freedom in India generated more negative responses, particularly among European and North American respondents. This created a dual-track global perception: admiration for India's economic rise and diplomatic heft coexisting with unease about its internal democratic trajectory — a tension that several India-watchers described as "engagement with concern."

REGIONAL VARIATIONS AND SOFT POWER

India's favorability gains were unevenly distributed globally. In South Asia itself, relationships with Pakistan remained deeply hostile, while Bangladesh, Nepal, and Sri Lanka showed complex mixes of economic appreciation and concern about Indian regional dominance. In Southeast Asia, India's Act East Policy and growing trade ties contributed to modestly improved perceptions. In Africa, Prime Minister Modi's outreach at the G20 and subsequent bilateral engagements built goodwill, though China remained the dominant external power perception. Among the Indian diaspora in the United States, United Kingdom, and Canada — estimated at over 5 million in total — strong community pride in India's global ascent generated further positive PR. However, tensions around Canadian allegations of Indian government involvement in the killing of Canadian Sikh activist Hardeep Singh Nijjar created a significant diplomatic and reputational complication in 2023-2024, particularly among diaspora communities.

CONCLUSION

India's global favorable ratings rise from 2020 to 2025 reflected genuine soft power gains from economic performance, diplomatic proactivity, and skilled narrative management. The country successfully positioned itself as indispensable to multiple major powers simultaneously — maintaining Russia relations while deepening U.S. partnerships, engaging China while building the Quad, and championing Global South causes while pursuing Western investment. This strategic autonomy earned international respect even from those who disagreed with specific Indian positions. The challenge ahead lies in whether democratic governance indicators will align more closely with India's economic and diplomatic image, or whether the gap will become a significant constraint on its soft power trajectory.
