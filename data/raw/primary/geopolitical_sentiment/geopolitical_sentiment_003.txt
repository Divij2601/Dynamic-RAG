RUSSIA'S IMAGE COLLAPSE IN WESTERN DEMOCRACIES FOLLOWING THE 2022 INVASION

OVERVIEW

Russia's invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022 produced one of the most sudden and comprehensive collapses in national image ever recorded by public opinion researchers. Within weeks of the full-scale invasion, Russian favorability ratings in Western democracies fell to historic lows, in many cases dropping by thirty to fifty percentage points. This collapse was not merely a policy disagreement — it represented a wholesale reassessment of Russia as a state, Vladimir Putin as a leader, and Russian civilization's place within the European community. Polling from Pew Research, YouGov, Ipsos, and national pollsters across Europe, North America, and the Asia-Pacific documented the scale and durability of this shift through 2025.

THE FEBRUARY 2022 SHOCK

Prior to the invasion, Russian favorability was already modest in most Western nations following the 2014 Crimea annexation, the Salisbury chemical weapon attack, election interference allegations, and the 2016 downing of MH17. However, outright unfavorable ratings in many European countries had remained in the 60-70 percent range. The full-scale invasion and the accompanying images of civilian casualties in Bucha, the siege of Mariupol, and large-scale refugee flows immediately drove unfavorability above 80 percent in most Western European and North American countries. In the United Kingdom, YouGov found 91 percent of respondents held unfavorable views of Russia by March 2022. Germany's figure reached 87 percent. Even in nations with historically warmer Russia ties — Austria, Hungary, and Italy — unfavorability exceeded 70 percent. The speed of this shift was without precedent in modern polling.

PUTIN'S PERSONAL RATINGS

Confidence in Vladimir Putin as a world leader had been measured periodically by Pew Research since the early 2000s. In the United States, confidence in Putin to "do the right thing regarding world affairs" fell from a modest 22 percent in 2020 to just 9 percent by mid-2022. Across EU nations, the figure dropped into single digits. Even in China, where state media avoided condemning the invasion, independent polling suggested ambivalence rather than enthusiasm. The personal nature of Putin's image collapse meant that any future Russian leadership change, at least in theory, might allow for some image rehabilitation — though analysts noted the depth of institutional distrust of the Russian state now embedded in Western opinion.

DURABILITY OF THE DECLINE AND WAR FATIGUE

A key question for researchers was whether the image collapse would prove durable or temporary. Through 2023 and into 2024, Western polling showed that unfavorable ratings of Russia remained extremely high despite some early signs of "Ukraine fatigue" in public support for military aid. Importantly, image collapse and support fatigue operated somewhat independently: populations could simultaneously maintain negative views of Russia while growing skeptical of their governments' level of financial and military commitment to Ukraine. A 2024 European Council on Foreign Relations survey found that 20 percent of European respondents wanted to prioritize ending the war quickly even at the cost of Ukrainian territory — a position that implied ambivalence about sustaining pressure on Russia without necessarily rehabilitating its image.

EASTERN EUROPE VS. WESTERN EUROPE GRADIENTS

Russia's image collapse was not uniform across Europe. In Poland, the Baltic states, Finland, and Sweden — countries with direct historical experience of Soviet or Russian pressure — the collapse was near-total and accompanied by significant policy shifts including NATO membership applications from Finland and Sweden. In contrast, Western and Southern European nations showed slightly more gradual declines and somewhat earlier signs of nuanced positions. Hungary under Viktor Orban remained an outlier, with government rhetoric partially at odds with population sentiment. In Germany, the term "Zeitenwende" (historic turning point) coined by Chancellor Scholz captured a genuine public mood shift toward rearming and treating Russia as a permanent threat.

CONCLUSION

By 2025, Russia's image in Western democracies remained at historic lows, and there was no credible polling evidence of rehabilitation. The invasion transformed not just opinions about Russia's government but also popular attitudes toward Russian culture, energy dependence, and business relationships. This sustained image collapse has had concrete policy consequences: public opinion support for NATO expansion, defense spending increases, and sanctions regimes was in part enabled by the scale of Russia's reputational collapse. Rebuilding any form of trust would require not just a cessation of hostilities but fundamental changes in Russian political behavior that, as of 2025, showed no signs of materializing.
