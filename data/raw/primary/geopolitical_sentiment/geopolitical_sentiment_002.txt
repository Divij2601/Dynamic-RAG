CHINA FAVORABLE AND UNFAVORABLE RATINGS: THE GLOBAL DECLINE 2019-2025

OVERVIEW

China's international image underwent one of the most dramatic deteriorations in modern polling history during the 2019-2025 period. Once buoyed by narratives of economic development, infrastructure investment, and a peaceable rise, China's global favorability collapsed in advanced democracies following the COVID-19 pandemic, the Xinjiang detention revelations, Hong Kong's political transformation, and sustained tensions over Taiwan. Pew Research Center, Gallup, the Lowy Institute, and other polling organizations documented this shift comprehensively, finding that negative views of China reached historic highs in most G7 nations by 2022 and remained elevated through 2025.

THE COVID-19 INFLECTION POINT

Prior to 2020, China's favorability was declining in some Western nations but remained relatively stable globally. The COVID-19 pandemic and perceptions that China mismanaged early outbreak information, delayed WHO notification, and suppressed whistleblowers caused a sharp inflection in Western public opinion. Pew's 2020 survey found unfavorable views of China reached 73 percent in the United States, 74 percent in the United Kingdom, and 71 percent in Germany — figures that had not been seen previously. Australia's unfavorable rating reached 81 percent by 2021, partly driven by China's economic coercion campaign against Australian exports following calls for a COVID-19 origin investigation. The pandemic effectively converted latent skepticism into active negative sentiment across allied democracies.

XINJIANG, HONG KONG, AND HUMAN RIGHTS DRIVERS

Pew Research analysis consistently found that knowledge of Xinjiang detention policies — involving the mass internment of Uyghur Muslims — was strongly correlated with negative views of China among surveyed populations. As international media coverage expanded from 2020 onward and Western governments began using the term "genocide," public opinion hardened. Similarly, Hong Kong's 2020 National Security Law and the subsequent erosion of the "one country, two systems" framework generated negative press globally and converted previously neutral observers in many countries. European respondents in the Netherlands, France, and Sweden showed large jumps in unfavorable China ratings precisely during periods of heavy coverage of these events.

THE GLOBAL SOUTH EXCEPTION

The global decline narrative contained a significant exception: much of the developing world did not follow the Western trajectory. Gallup and Afrobarometer surveys found that China retained positive or neutral image ratings across much of Sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East through 2024. China's continued infrastructure investment via Belt and Road Initiative projects, vaccine diplomacy during COVID-19, and the absence of colonial history in many of these regions contributed to more favorable perceptions. In Kenya, 58 percent of respondents viewed China favorably in 2023. Indonesia showed 60 percent favorable ratings. These divergences illustrated the deep asymmetry in global opinion — where declining Western ratings coexisted with stable or positive Southern ratings, reinforcing a "two world" perceptual dynamic.

TAIWAN TENSIONS AND 2024-2025 DETERIORATION

The period around Taiwan's January 2024 presidential election, which returned the DPP and William Lai to power, renewed international attention on cross-strait tensions. Western media extensively covered Chinese military exercises and diplomatic pressure campaigns. Polls conducted in Europe and North America in 2024 showed continued high unfavorable ratings of China, with concerns about Taiwan conflict, trade practices, and technology competition driving sentiment. A 2024 Bertelsmann Stiftung survey found that over 60 percent of Germans believed China was a "systemic rival" rather than a partner. By 2025, U.S.-China trade war escalation under the second Trump administration introduced a new variable: while anti-China sentiment remained high on security grounds, economic interdependence arguments from business communities created some counterweight to political sentiment.

CONCLUSION

China's global image decline represents a significant strategic liability, even as Beijing has invested heavily in state media outreach, cultural diplomacy, and international broadcasting. The divergence between Western democracies and the Global South creates a bifurcated global information environment in which China is simultaneously a villain and a partner depending on audience. For Chinese foreign policy, managing this dual-track perception while avoiding the feedback loop between unfavorable ratings and diplomatic isolation represents a core soft power challenge of the 2020s.
