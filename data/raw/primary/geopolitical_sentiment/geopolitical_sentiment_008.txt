EUROPEAN UNION TRUST RATINGS AMONG MEMBER STATES: 2019-2025

OVERVIEW

The European Union has faced persistent trust challenges among citizens of its own member states, with Eurobarometer surveys documenting a recurring tension between support for the EU's existence in principle and skepticism about its performance in specific domains. The 2019-2025 period was particularly turbulent, encompassing COVID-19 and the vaccine procurement crisis, the rule of law disputes with Hungary and Poland, the Russia-Ukraine war and its associated energy and refugee pressures, and the emergence of far-right and Eurosceptic parties as significant forces in multiple member state elections. Trust levels in the EU among its own citizens provide a crucial indicator of the bloc's internal legitimacy and long-term political sustainability.

BASELINE TRUST LEVELS AND EUROBAROMETER METHODOLOGY

The European Commission's Eurobarometer surveys, conducted twice annually across all member states, provide the most comprehensive longitudinal data on EU trust. The standard indicator asks respondents whether they "tend to trust" or "tend not to trust" the European Union. In 2019, before COVID-19, the EU-wide average stood at approximately 45 percent tending to trust. This was an improvement from the depths of the Eurozone crisis around 2012-2013 when trust fell below 31 percent, but remained well below the approximately 55 percent trust levels of the early 2000s. There were substantial national variations: Nordic countries, the Netherlands, and Baltic states typically showed higher trust, while Czech Republic, France, Italy, and Greece showed persistently lower trust.

COVID-19 AND THE VACCINE PROCUREMENT CRISIS

The EU's centralized vaccine procurement strategy, intended to demonstrate solidarity and prevent member state competition, initially became a significant trust liability. Delivery delays in early 2021, a diplomatic confrontation with AstraZeneca, and perceived slower rollout compared to the UK (which had left the EU) generated public criticism. Several member states including Germany and Austria initially circumvented EU procurement to secure additional doses. Eurobarometer Spring 2021 showed EU trust declining in several large member states. However, the EU recovered quickly: by late 2021, as vaccination rates exceeded UK levels and the EU's Digital COVID Certificate eased travel, trust partially rebounded. The crisis demonstrated both the EU's institutional ambitions and the political costs of coordination failures.

RULE OF LAW, HUNGARY, AND DEMOCRATIC LEGITIMACY

The ongoing conflict between EU institutions and Hungary's Orban government over rule of law, media freedom, and judicial independence created a complex trust dynamic. In Hungary, EU trust fell among Fidesz voters as the government portrayed Brussels as a hostile external force, while opposition voters retained stronger EU identification. More broadly, the EU's prolonged failure to enforce rule of law conditionality — repeatedly withholding then partially releasing funds — undermined credibility with publics in other member states who felt the EU was ineffective at holding members to its stated values. A 2023 Eurobarometer found that 68 percent of EU citizens believed the EU should take stronger action against member states that violated democratic norms — reflecting a public mandate for enforcement the EU had been slow to exercise.

RUSSIA-UKRAINE WAR: RENEWED PURPOSE AND NEW PRESSURES

The 2022 Russian invasion paradoxically strengthened EU institutional trust in the short term by demonstrating the bloc's capacity for rapid collective action: unprecedented sanctions regimes, temporary protection status for Ukrainian refugees, and joint gas purchase initiatives. Eurobarometer Autumn 2022 showed EU trust rising to 47 percent — a multi-year high in several countries. However, subsequent energy price inflation, divisions over support levels for Ukraine, and debates about EU defense integration created new pressures. By 2024, Eurosceptic parties performed strongly in European Parliament elections, with hard-right groupings gaining seats. This did not immediately reduce EU trust among the general public — voters can simultaneously support EU membership while voting for parties that criticize the EU's direction.

CONCLUSION

EU trust ratings among member states in 2025 reflected an institution that had navigated multiple existential challenges without fracturing, while accumulating significant legitimacy deficits in specific domains — institutional responsiveness, rule of law enforcement, and democratic accountability. The EU's fundamental value proposition — peace, economic integration, and collective weight in a multipolar world — retained broad public endorsement. But the gap between institutional ambition and delivery capacity continued to generate frustration. The 2024 European Parliament elections' rightward shift signaled that even pro-EU populations wanted institutional reform and were willing to use protest votes to demand it. Managing this reformist pressure within a consensus-based decision-making structure remains the EU's central governance challenge.
