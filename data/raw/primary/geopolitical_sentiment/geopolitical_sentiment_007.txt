THE UN TRUST DEFICIT: PUBLIC CONFIDENCE IN MULTILATERAL INSTITUTIONS

OVERVIEW

Public trust in the United Nations and multilateral institutions more broadly has been in measurable decline across multiple regions since the early 2010s, with particularly sharp deteriorations following perceived institutional failures during the COVID-19 pandemic, the Russia-Ukraine war, and the Gaza conflict. The United Nations, established in 1945 with the mandate to maintain international peace and security, has faced growing public skepticism about its effectiveness, impartiality, and relevance in an era of great power competition. This trust deficit has implications not only for the UN itself but for the entire framework of rules-based multilateral governance.

GLOBAL POLLING ON UN EFFECTIVENESS

Gallup's annual World Affairs surveys and the Edelman Trust Barometer have tracked multilateral institution confidence over time. By 2022, a Gallup survey found that only 45 percent of American respondents approved of the United Nations — down from 62 percent in 2009. European populations showed similarly declining trends, with the EU's Eurobarometer showing declining trust in international institutions from the mid-2010s onward. A 2023 United Nations Association-USA survey found that while Americans still valued the concept of multilateral cooperation, they were skeptical about the UN's ability to enforce its decisions — 67 percent believed the UN was "ineffective at resolving major conflicts." The Security Council's structural design, with P5 veto powers routinely blocking action against member states' interests, was widely cited as the primary institutional failure.

THE RUSSIA-UKRAINE VETO CRISIS

Russia's use of its Security Council veto to block resolutions condemning its own invasion of Ukraine brought the veto problem into sharp public relief. The February-March 2022 period, when Russia vetoed a Security Council resolution calling for withdrawal, pushed the UN General Assembly to convene emergency sessions under the "Uniting for Peace" mechanism — itself an indication that the Security Council had failed. Public commentary in Western democracies was broadly contemptuous of an institution that could not respond to a permanent member's flagrant violation of the UN Charter's core prohibition on wars of aggression. Survey data from the Chicago Council on Global Affairs found that 71 percent of U.S. respondents believed the Security Council needed structural reform, and 64 percent supported removing or limiting veto powers for nations involved in active conflicts.

THE WHO AND PANDEMIC TRUST EROSION

The World Health Organization's handling of COVID-19 generated one of the most severe blows to multilateral institutional trust. Criticism centered on delayed emergency declaration, deference to Chinese information on human-to-human transmission, and the exclusion of Taiwan from WHO participation at China's insistence. U.S. President Trump's decision to withdraw from the WHO in 2020 both reflected and amplified public skepticism. Pew Research found that confidence in the WHO fell among both Republicans and Democrats in the United States, though for different reasons. The subsequent development of vaccines — largely by national or private entities rather than through WHO coordination — reinforced a narrative that multilateral health institutions added limited operational value even in their core domain. Biden rejoined the WHO in 2021, but Trump's second administration withdrew again in early 2025.

REGIONAL VARIATIONS IN MULTILATERAL TRUST

Trust in multilateral institutions varied significantly by region. In the Global South, international institutions including the UN maintained higher credibility partly because they offered smaller nations a forum and a voice they lacked in bilateral relationships with major powers. African nations showed relatively higher trust in the UN system, though growing frustration with the Security Council's failure to address African conflicts equitably — and a sense that African issues received less attention than comparable crises elsewhere — created an emerging trust deficit even there. In East Asia, U.S.-China competition over multilateral institutions (including battles over WHO leadership, ITU standards-setting, and WTO reform) created a complex environment in which countries had to navigate institutional politics carefully.

CONCLUSION

The UN's trust deficit reflects genuine institutional limitations combined with the expectations gap created by the organization's foundational promises. Public opinion is not simply wrong: the Security Council veto system does paralyze action in cases involving major powers, and multilateral institutions have demonstrated real limitations in pandemic response, climate action implementation, and conflict prevention. Yet the alternatives — purely bilateral diplomacy, regional organizations, or great power concerts — also have significant limitations. The trust deficit may be most productively understood as a demand signal for institutional reform rather than institutional abandonment. Public opinion consistently supports international cooperation even as it criticizes current institutional performance — suggesting that reform, not replacement, is what populations broadly want.
