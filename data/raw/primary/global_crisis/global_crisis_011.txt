DRC CONGO DISPLACEMENT CRISIS: THE WORLD'S FORGOTTEN EMERGENCY

The Democratic Republic of Congo has experienced what UNHCR has described as one of the world's most severe and neglected displacement crises for over two decades. By 2024, the DRC hosted the largest number of internally displaced persons in Africa, with over seven million people forced from their homes by armed conflict, primarily in the eastern provinces. The resurgence of the M23 rebel movement backed by Rwanda, combined with the proliferation of armed groups and resource competition, has intensified displacement and humanitarian need significantly since 2021.

CONFLICT DYNAMICS IN EASTERN DRC

Eastern DRC has been wracked by overlapping armed conflicts since the 1990s, rooted in ethnic tensions, competition for mineral resources, the legacy of the Rwandan genocide, and weak state capacity. The M23 rebellion, which first emerged in 2012 and was temporarily defeated in 2013, re-emerged in 2021 with expanded military capability widely attributed to Rwandan military support, a claim Rwanda denied but which UN Group of Experts investigations documented extensively. By 2022-2023, M23 forces had seized significant territory in North Kivu, including major towns and supply routes, displacing hundreds of thousands of civilians. A military standoff around Goma, North Kivu's major city and humanitarian hub, created a direct threat to nearly two million civilians in and around the city. In early 2025, M23 forces seized Goma itself, the largest city in eastern DRC, triggering mass panic, looting, and displacement of hundreds of thousands of residents who fled southward.

SCALE AND CONDITIONS OF DISPLACEMENT

The DRC's internal displacement crisis is extraordinary in scale, with over 7.2 million IDPs recorded by UNHCR in 2024, more than any other country in Africa. People are displaced by a complex mix of M23 operations, the Allied Democratic Forces (ADF) attacks in Beni territory which have massacred hundreds of civilians, FDLR remnants, local Mai-Mai militias, and intercommunal violence. Displacement sites around Goma and in Ituri province have grown into massive spontaneous settlements lacking basic services. Civilians face multiple displacements as conflict moves, with many displaced five or more times without ever reaching safety or durable solutions. Children have been recruited into armed groups, sexual violence has been used systematically as a weapon of war, and humanitarian workers have been killed in significant numbers.

HUMANITARIAN RESPONSE AND FUNDING GAPS

Despite its scale, the DRC crisis has historically received a fraction of international attention and funding relative to European or Middle Eastern crises. Annual humanitarian response plans for DRC have been funded at 40 to 50 percent of assessed need. Competition from Ukraine, Gaza, and Sudan has further squeezed DRC's funding share since 2022. Access for humanitarian organizations varies by region, with some areas entirely inaccessible due to security threats. Major humanitarian actors including UNHCR, WFP, UNICEF, ICRC, and MSF operate in extremely difficult conditions, with aid convoys attacked and staff killed. The humanitarian situation is compounded by very high rates of food insecurity, with nearly 25 million people in DRC facing acute food insecurity in 2024, more than any other country in the world.

REGIONAL AND INTERNATIONAL DIMENSIONS

The DRC crisis has significant regional geopolitical dimensions. Rwanda's relationship with DRC is deeply antagonistic, rooted in accusations of Rwandan support for M23 and DRC's hosting of FDLR remnants connected to the 1994 genocide perpetrators. The East African Community attempted a mediation process and deployed a regional force, which DRC later asked to depart. Angola played a mediation role. The African Union, UN, and bilateral actors including the United States and European Union engaged in diplomacy, but a durable peace framework remained elusive. The DRC's massive mineral wealth, including cobalt essential for electric vehicle batteries, creates economic incentives for resource extraction that feed armed group financing, complicating governance reform. International advocacy to address the DRC's crisis faced the structural challenge that the crisis has persisted for so long and received so little sustained attention that it has been naturalized as a permanent feature of the regional landscape.
