ETHIOPIA TIGRAY FAMINE AND HUMANITARIAN ACCESS CRISIS

The conflict in Ethiopia's Tigray region, which began in November 2020, produced a humanitarian catastrophe of extraordinary scale, including famine conditions affecting hundreds of thousands of people and deliberate obstruction of humanitarian access that the UN and international observers characterized as using starvation as a weapon of war. A peace agreement signed in November 2022 ended the most intense phase of fighting but left profound humanitarian challenges unresolved.

CONFLICT ORIGINS AND ESCALATION

The Tigray conflict emerged from a power struggle between the Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF), which had dominated Ethiopian federal politics for nearly three decades before being marginalized by Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed following his 2018 Nobel Peace Prize-winning political reforms, and the federal government allied with the Amhara regional government and Eritrea. The TPLF's decision to hold regional elections in defiance of federal government postponements due to COVID-19, and subsequently to attack federal military bases in November 2020, prompted a massive federal military response. Ethiopian federal forces, allied with Eritrean forces and Amhara militias, initially overran Tigray rapidly, but the TPLF regrouped and launched a major counter-offensive in mid-2021 that brought its forces within reach of the Ethiopian capital Addis Ababa before being turned back.

FAMINE AND DELIBERATE STARVATION

The humanitarian situation in Tigray during the conflict reached extreme severity. In June 2021, the IPC classified 350,000 people in Tigray as experiencing famine conditions, representing the first widely recognized famine anywhere in the world since Somalia in 2011. The causes of famine were not primarily natural but resulted directly from the conflict: the destruction of crops, livestock, and farm equipment; displacement of farming populations; banking blockades preventing remittances; and most critically, the deliberate obstruction of humanitarian aid. The Ethiopian federal government, in coordination with Eritrea, restricted food, medicine, and fuel deliveries into Tigray to what aid organizations characterized as a small fraction of assessed need. Convoys were turned back, UN staff were expelled from Ethiopia, and journalists were severely restricted. Aid workers reported that only a few hundred trucks reached Tigray when thousands per month were needed. UN officials and aid agencies used the word famine publicly and privately stated that civilians were being deliberately starved.

ATROCITIES AND INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE

The conflict was marked by large-scale atrocities by multiple parties. Eritrean forces committed massacres of civilians, including the killing of hundreds in the Axum massacre. Ethiopian air strikes hit civilian areas. TPLF forces committed killings, sexual violence, and looting in the Amhara and Afar regions during their advances. The UN and various human rights organizations documented war crimes and potential crimes against humanity by all sides. A joint UN-Ethiopian Human Rights Commission investigation in November 2021 documented extensive violations. The response of the international community was limited: diplomatic pressure, the suspension of U.S. and European budget support aid to Ethiopia, and eventual suspension of Ethipoia from AGOA trade preferences, but no Security Council action due to opposition from China and Russia.

POST-PEACE AGREEMENT HUMANITARIAN CHALLENGES

The Pretoria peace agreement signed in November 2022 between the Ethiopian federal government and TPLF ended active large-scale fighting and opened humanitarian corridors. Aid deliveries to Tigray increased substantially after the agreement. However, the humanitarian situation remained severe: massive agricultural losses during the conflict years, displaced populations not yet returned, livestock losses, and health system collapse required years of recovery investment. The peace agreement did not immediately address Eritrean forces' continued presence in some Tigray areas, nor the Amhara militia control of western Tigray, complicating returns. A new conflict erupted in the Amhara region in 2023 as the federal government moved to disarm regional special forces, adding new displacement and humanitarian needs elsewhere in Ethiopia while drawing resources from Tigray recovery.
