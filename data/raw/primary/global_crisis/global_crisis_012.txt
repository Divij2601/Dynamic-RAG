HAITI HUMANITARIAN CRISIS AND STATE COLLAPSE

Haiti's humanitarian situation reached a critical inflection point in 2023 and 2024 as the country descended into what international observers described as state collapse, with armed gangs controlling most of the capital Port-au-Prince, displacing hundreds of thousands, paralyzing governance, and preventing delivery of basic services. Haiti's long history of political instability, natural disasters, and economic underdevelopment converged in a crisis of exceptional severity by 2024.

POLITICAL COLLAPSE AND GANG CONTROL

Haiti's political situation deteriorated progressively following the July 2021 assassination of President Jovenel Moise, who was killed in his private residence by a mercenary team in still-incompletely explained circumstances. The subsequent power vacuum created conditions for dramatic gang expansion. The G9 coalition led by Jimmy Cherizier, known as Barbecue, and a rival coalition called Viv Ansanm emerged as dominant armed actors in Port-au-Prince, controlling an estimated 80 percent of the capital by early 2024. Gang violence included mass killings of civilians, sexual violence used systematically, kidnappings of both Haitians and foreigners for ransom, and the blockade of key infrastructure including the main fuel terminal. The Varreux fuel terminal blockade in 2022 caused fuel shortages that shut down hospitals and water treatment plants. Acting Prime Minister Ariel Henry, who had governed without an elected parliament since elections had not been held, was unable to return to Haiti in early 2024 while abroad seeking to organize international security support.

IMPACT ON POPULATION AND HUMANITARIAN CONDITIONS

The gang violence and governance collapse have had catastrophic humanitarian consequences. By mid-2024, approximately 578,000 people were internally displaced in Haiti, many living in spontaneous settlements in schools, sports facilities, and public spaces with minimal sanitation and no privacy. Sexual violence against displaced women and girls was reported at alarming rates. The blockade of routes into and within Port-au-Prince prevented food distribution, medicine delivery, and access to healthcare. Cholera, which had been eradicated in Haiti after the epidemic introduced following the 2010 earthquake, re-emerged in 2022 and continued to spread in displacement settings with contaminated water. Schools were closed across much of Port-au-Prince for extended periods. An estimated 5.5 million Haitians faced acute food insecurity by 2024, representing over half the population.

INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE AND MULTINATIONAL SECURITY MISSION

The international community's response to Haiti's security collapse was notably slow given the scale of the crisis. The UN Security Council authorized a Multinational Security Support Mission in October 2023 to support the Haitian National Police, with Kenya agreeing to lead the mission. However, deployment was delayed by legal challenges in Kenya, bureaucratic and logistical obstacles, and difficulty securing sufficient troop contributions from other countries. The first Kenyan forces did not arrive until June 2024, months after authorization. By the time the mission deployed, conditions had further deteriorated, and the multinational force faced immense challenges restoring security against well-armed and entrenched gang networks. The mission's mandate and resources were widely considered insufficient for the scale of the security problem.

STRUCTURAL ROOTS OF CRISIS AND LONG-TERM OUTLOOK

Haiti's crisis has deep structural roots that transcend any immediate political solution. As the poorest country in the Western Hemisphere, with a per capita income of approximately 1,800 dollars annually before the current crisis, Haiti has long faced governance deficits, environmental degradation from extreme deforestation, and extreme vulnerability to natural disasters. The 2010 earthquake killed over 200,000 people and set back development by decades. International reconstruction aid was poorly coordinated and delivered limited lasting impact. Political elites have historically captured state institutions and resisted reforms that would threaten their economic privileges. The gang phenomenon partly reflects the longstanding use of armed groups as political instruments by competing elite factions. Long-term stabilization requires not merely security interventions but fundamental political reform, institution building, and sustained economic development investment that the international community has repeatedly attempted and failed to achieve.
