SUDAN HUMANITARIAN CRISIS 2023-2025: MASS DISPLACEMENT AND FAMINE

The outbreak of conflict between Sudan's Armed Forces and the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces in April 2023 plunged Sudan into what the United Nations described by 2024 as the world's largest displacement crisis and one of its worst humanitarian emergencies. By early 2025, the war had killed tens of thousands, displaced over twelve million people internally, and driven millions more across borders while threatening famine on a massive scale.

ORIGINS OF THE CONFLICT

The conflict emerged from a power struggle between Sudan Armed Forces (SAF) General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan and RSF commander General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo, known as Hemeti, both of whom had jointly led the military coup that ousted civilian transitional government leader Abdalla Hamdok in October 2021. Tensions over the planned integration of RSF forces into the national military, timelines for a transition to civilian rule, and control over Sudan's strategic and economic resources precipitated open hostilities in April 2023. Fighting erupted initially in Khartoum, devastating the capital city, before spreading to Darfur, Kordofan, and other regions. The RSF's conduct in Darfur, particularly in El Fasher and across the broader region, was characterized by mass atrocities against non-Arab communities that UN officials and international observers described as ethnic cleansing and potentially genocide, echoing the Darfur crisis of the early 2000s.

DISPLACEMENT SCALE AND CROSS-BORDER FLOWS

Sudan's conflict produced displacement figures that dwarfed most contemporary crises. By late 2024, over eight million people were internally displaced within Sudan, in addition to pre-existing displaced populations. Over two million Sudanese fled to neighboring countries, including Chad, South Sudan, Egypt, Ethiopia, and the Central African Republic. Chad, itself one of the world's poorest countries, absorbed over 700,000 Sudanese refugees in addition to hundreds of thousands of Chadians who had previously taken refuge in Sudan and were forced to return. The massive cross-border displacement strained host community resources and aid systems in some of the most fragile states in the world. UNHCR and other agencies scrambled to register new arrivals and provide emergency shelter, food, and medical care in conditions of extreme difficulty.

FAMINE AND HUMANITARIAN ACCESS

By mid-2024, Sudan was experiencing what analysts described as one of the worst food crises in its modern history. The IPC famine review found famine conditions in the Zamzam displacement camp in North Darfur by mid-2024, with an estimated 755,000 people experiencing catastrophic food insecurity across the country. Aid access was severely hampered by both warring parties, with RSF blocking convoys in areas under its control while bureaucratic obstruction delayed UN approvals. The destruction of agricultural systems, disruption of markets, and displacement of farming communities compounded acute food shortages. International humanitarian funding for Sudan remained critically underfunded relative to needs, partly because donor attention was concentrated on the Ukraine and Gaza crises occurring simultaneously.

INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE AND DIPLOMATIC EFFORTS

International mediation efforts struggled to gain traction. The Jeddah Process, led by Saudi Arabia and the United States beginning in May 2023, produced a short-lived ceasefire that quickly collapsed. The African Union, Intergovernmental Authority on Development, and Egypt attempted parallel mediation initiatives with limited success. Western nations imposed targeted sanctions on RSF leadership and some SAF officials but stopped short of more comprehensive measures. The conflict's geopolitical dimensions complicated international response, with the UAE accused of supplying the RSF through Chad while Egypt backed the SAF, effectively internationalizing what began as an internal power struggle. By early 2025, no peace framework had gained traction and humanitarian conditions continued to deteriorate, with the UN warning that Sudan risked becoming the world's worst humanitarian crisis if international attention and funding did not increase substantially.
