COVID-19 VACCINE NATIONALISM AND GEOPOLITICAL COMPETITION

The development and distribution of COVID-19 vaccines in 2020 and 2021 became a defining arena of geopolitical competition, as major powers raced to produce, procure, and deploy vaccines both domestically and as instruments of foreign policy. The resulting "vaccine nationalism" deepened global inequalities and exposed the fragility of cooperative international health governance.

RACE TO DEVELOP AND PROCURE VACCINES

The unprecedented speed of COVID-19 vaccine development reflected both scientific advances, particularly in mRNA technology pioneered by BioNTech and Moderna, and massive public investment by wealthy governments. The United States committed billions of dollars through Operation Warp Speed, securing advance purchase agreements for hundreds of millions of doses before clinical trials were complete. The United Kingdom, European Union, Canada, and Australia similarly locked up supplies through bilateral deals with manufacturers. This advance purchasing model, while effective for wealthy nations, left developing countries with limited access in the critical early months of 2021 when global vaccine supply was severely constrained. By mid-2021, wealthy nations had administered multiple doses per person while most low-income countries had vaccinated less than two percent of their populations.

COVAX AND THE LIMITS OF MULTILATERALISM

The COVAX facility, co-led by Gavi, CEPI, and the WHO, was designed to provide equitable global vaccine access by pooling purchasing power and ensuring at least 20 percent of populations in participating nations received vaccines. However, COVAX was repeatedly undermined by wealthy nation hoarding, export restrictions, and insufficient funding. India's decision in April 2021 to halt Serum Institute exports to address its own devastating second wave devastated COVAX delivery timelines, as India had been COVAX's primary supplier. The intellectual property debate became a major geopolitical battleground, with South Africa and India leading a push at the World Trade Organization for a TRIPS waiver that would allow developing nations to manufacture vaccines without licensing restrictions. The United States eventually supported the waiver in principle, but negotiations stalled amid opposition from pharmaceutical companies and European nations, ultimately yielding a limited agreement in 2022.

VACCINE DIPLOMACY AS GEOPOLITICAL TOOL

China and Russia deployed their domestically developed vaccines, Sinovac, Sinopharm, and Sputnik V, as instruments of soft power, supplying doses to developing nations in Latin America, Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East when Western vaccines remained unavailable. China distributed hundreds of millions of doses globally, often through bilateral agreements with nations in its Belt and Road Initiative network, strengthening political ties and building goodwill. Russia's Sputnik V, developed by the Gamaleya Institute and announced before full Phase 3 trial data was published, was deployed in over 70 countries despite initial skepticism from Western health authorities. The United States and United Kingdom also engaged in vaccine diplomacy, with the Biden administration pledging over one billion doses through COVAX and bilateral donations, though deliveries often lagged behind pledges.

CONSEQUENCES AND LESSONS FOR GLOBAL HEALTH GOVERNANCE

The COVID-19 vaccine experience revealed deep structural flaws in global health equity. By the time vaccines became widely available to low-income nations in late 2021 and 2022, the Omicron variant had spread globally, partly enabled by low vaccination rates in under-served populations creating conditions for viral evolution. The episode energized calls for fundamental reform of global health governance, including proposals for a pandemic treaty under the WHO. Negotiations for such a treaty, begun in 2022, proved contentious and difficult, with disputes over intellectual property, pathogen sharing, and national sovereignty reflecting the same geopolitical tensions that had hampered vaccine equity. The experience also accelerated investment in regional vaccine manufacturing capacity in Africa, South Asia, and Latin America, reducing dependence on a handful of producing nations for future health emergencies. The geopolitical competition in vaccines mirrored broader great-power dynamics and illustrated how public health had become inseparable from strategic competition.
