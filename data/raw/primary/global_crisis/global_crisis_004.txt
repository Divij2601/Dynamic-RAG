GAZA HUMANITARIAN CATASTROPHE 2023-2025

The Hamas attacks on Israel on October 7, 2023 and the subsequent Israeli military campaign in Gaza triggered one of the most severe humanitarian catastrophes of the twenty-first century. By 2025, the Gaza Strip had experienced near-total destruction of civilian infrastructure, unprecedented civilian casualties, and a man-made famine threatening the survival of its entire population of approximately 2.3 million people.

SCALE OF DESTRUCTION AND CIVILIAN CASUALTIES

Israeli military operations in Gaza following the October 7 attacks, which killed approximately 1,200 Israelis and resulted in around 250 hostages taken to Gaza, caused devastation on a scale that drew widespread international condemnation. By early 2025, Palestinian health authorities reported over 47,000 confirmed deaths in Gaza, with the vast majority being civilians including thousands of children. Independent analyses suggested true mortality, including those buried under rubble or dying from disease and malnutrition, significantly exceeded official figures. An estimated 80 to 90 percent of Gaza's buildings had been damaged or destroyed. Hospitals were repeatedly struck or rendered non-functional due to fuel shortages, staff displacement, and direct damage. The World Health Organization reported that Gaza's healthcare system had effectively collapsed, with only a fraction of pre-war hospital capacity remaining functional by mid-2024.

FAMINE AND FOOD SECURITY CRISIS

The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification famine review panel declared famine conditions in parts of northern Gaza in June 2024, making it one of the first official famine declarations in a heavily urbanized setting. The entire population of Gaza was classified as experiencing acute food insecurity by early 2025, with severe restrictions on humanitarian aid entry through land crossings and the closure of the Rafah crossing following Israel's military incursion into Rafah in May 2024. International humanitarian organizations including the World Food Programme, UNRWA, and UNICEF repeatedly warned that famine was spreading southward and that children were dying of malnutrition. Israel's restrictions on humanitarian convoys, including incidents in which aid workers were killed, drew condemnation from longtime allies. Seven World Central Kitchen workers were killed in an Israeli airstrike in April 2024, prompting global outrage and temporary adjustments to aid coordination.

INTERNATIONAL LEGAL AND DIPLOMATIC DIMENSIONS

The humanitarian catastrophe in Gaza generated significant international legal activity. South Africa filed a case against Israel at the International Court of Justice under the Genocide Convention in December 2023, with Nicaragua, Ireland, and other states subsequently joining proceedings. The ICJ ordered provisional measures in January 2024 requiring Israel to take steps to prevent genocide and ensure humanitarian access, though implementation remained disputed. The International Criminal Court's prosecutor applied for arrest warrants for Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and Defense Minister Yoav Gallant in May 2024, alongside warrants for Hamas leaders, citing crimes against humanity and war crimes. These developments deepened international polarization, with Western nations largely supporting Israel's right to self-defense while much of the Global South condemned the scale and methods of military operations.

REGIONAL SPILLOVER AND HUMANITARIAN DISPLACEMENT

The Gaza war created significant regional humanitarian dynamics. Egyptian authorities firmly refused large-scale Palestinian entry into Sinai, fearing both the security implications and the potential permanent displacement of Palestinians from Gaza. Jordan similarly refused expanded Palestinian presence given the demographic sensitivity of its existing large Palestinian population. Within Gaza, the entire population experienced repeated forced displacement, with Israeli military orders directing people southward while operations then expanded southward as well, leaving no genuinely safe zone. The population of approximately 1.5 million people crowded into the Rafah area faced catastrophic conditions before and during the Israeli military offensive there. By early 2025, ceasefire negotiations mediated by Qatar, Egypt, and the United States had yielded temporary pauses and partial hostage-prisoner exchanges but had not produced a durable agreement to end hostilities or restore adequate humanitarian access.
