YEMEN HUMANITARIAN CRISIS: CHOLERA, FAMINE, AND PROTRACTED WAR

Yemen's armed conflict, which escalated dramatically with the Saudi-led military coalition intervention in March 2015, has produced what the United Nations has repeatedly called the world's worst humanitarian crisis. A decade of conflict by 2025 had killed hundreds of thousands of people through direct violence and indirect causes, driven mass displacement, caused recurrent famine conditions, and generated the world's largest cholera outbreak in modern history.

CONFLICT ORIGINS AND DIMENSIONS

Yemen's civil war pits the Houthi movement, known formally as Ansar Allah, which controls most of northern Yemen including the capital Sanaa, against a coalition of forces aligned with the internationally recognized government based in Aden, backed by Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates. The conflict has roots in Yemen's failed political transition following the Arab Spring, the collapse of the transitional arrangement that had ended the previous Saleh government, and deep sectarian, regional, and tribal tensions. Saudi Arabia's intervention was motivated by fears of an Iranian-backed force on its southern border, while Iran provided the Houthis with weapons, including ballistic missiles and drones used to strike Saudi and UAE territory. The conflict became a proxy arena for Saudi-Iranian competition, complicating both military dynamics and peace efforts. A UN-mediated truce in April 2022 brought significant reduction in fighting and some improvements in humanitarian access, but a comprehensive peace agreement remained elusive through 2025.

CHOLERA CRISIS AND DISEASE BURDEN

Yemen experienced the largest cholera outbreak in recorded history beginning in 2016, driven by the collapse of water, sanitation, and healthcare infrastructure. By 2019, the outbreak had infected over three million people. Cholera surged again repeatedly through subsequent years, fueled by the destruction of water treatment facilities, salary arrears for health workers, and inadequate sanitation in displacement settings. Other disease outbreaks, including dengue fever and COVID-19, compounded an already overwhelmed health system. By 2020, more than half of Yemen's health facilities were non-functional or only partially functioning. Maternal mortality rates soared as skilled birth attendance collapsed. Child mortality from preventable diseases increased substantially. The Save the Children organization estimated that a child died every ten minutes in Yemen from preventable causes during peak crisis periods.

FAMINE CONDITIONS AND FOOD INSECURITY

Yemen has been at the brink of famine for much of the conflict period. In 2021, the UN declared that 16 million Yemenis faced acute food insecurity, with 47,000 living in famine-like conditions. The primary drivers include economic collapse, currency devaluation, import disruptions caused by the blockade of Hodeidah port, and the destruction of agricultural livelihoods. The Saudi-led coalition imposed restrictions on Hodeidah port, through which 70 percent of Yemen's food imports entered, citing Houthi weapons smuggling. These restrictions, combined with Houthi economic governance failures and currency manipulation in northern Yemen, created a perfect storm of food system collapse. The Houthis' diversion of humanitarian aid and obstruction of access in areas under their control further hampered relief efforts. Despite massive international humanitarian investment, with Yemen receiving billions in aid annually, the structural drivers of the crisis remained unaddressed.

HUMANITARIAN FUNDING AND REGIONAL DYNAMICS

Yemen's humanitarian response plan has been chronically underfunded relative to UN assessments of need. Donor fatigue, competition from other crises, and frustration with aid diversion contributed to funding gaps. The Houthi attacks on Saudi Arabia and the UAE, including drone and missile strikes on critical infrastructure, complicated donor willingness to pressure the coalition on humanitarian access. The Houthi decision to attack commercial shipping in the Red Sea beginning in late 2023, ostensibly in solidarity with Gaza, drew U.S. and UK military strikes and significantly escalated international involvement in Yemen's conflict. These attacks disrupted global shipping through the Suez Canal route and added new geopolitical dimensions to an already complex crisis, with no clear pathway to the comprehensive peace settlement that alone can address Yemen's underlying humanitarian emergency.
