SOMALIA FAMINE, AL-SHABAAB OBSTRUCTION, AND RECURRING CRISIS

Somalia has experienced multiple overlapping crises for decades, combining state fragility, jihadist insurgency, climate-driven drought and flood cycles, and chronic underdevelopment. The country came perilously close to a declared famine in 2022, the fourth time in thirty years it has approached or experienced such conditions. Al-Shabaab's territorial control and deliberate obstruction of humanitarian aid has been a persistent factor amplifying the human cost of Somalia's crises.

DROUGHT CRISIS OF 2022 AND NEAR-FAMINE

Somalia experienced five consecutive failed rainy seasons between 2020 and 2022, the longest drought in at least four decades. By late 2022, an estimated 7.8 million Somalis faced acute food insecurity out of a population of approximately 17 million, and over 200,000 people were classified as experiencing catastrophic food insecurity. Over one million Somalis were internally displaced by the drought, with hundreds of thousands reaching urban displacement sites in Mogadishu, Baidoa, and Kismayo in desperate condition. International agencies warned repeatedly that Somalia was "weeks away" from a formal famine declaration through much of the second half of 2022. An unprecedented international response effort, prompted by the catastrophic 2011 Somalia famine in which approximately 260,000 people died, including massive donations from the United States and Gulf states, and improved access by aid agencies, narrowly averted a formal famine declaration, though severe acute malnutrition among children remained at emergency levels.

AL-SHABAAB AID OBSTRUCTION

Al-Shabaab, the al-Qaeda affiliated jihadist group that controls significant portions of south-central Somalia, has systematically obstructed humanitarian aid delivery in areas under its control or influence. The group has blocked aid convoys, expelled NGOs, banned certain organizations it accuses of information gathering, imposed taxation on aid operations, and physically prevented displaced populations from receiving assistance. In 2011, al-Shabaab's ban on several major humanitarian organizations including the World Food Programme was directly linked to avoidable deaths during that year's famine. The obstruction dynamic has recurred in subsequent crises, with aid agencies constantly navigating negotiations with al-Shabaab-aligned local structures to gain access. Areas in the Jubbaland, Lower Shabelle, and Bay regions, which are among the most food-insecure zones, have experienced the worst access constraints.

RECURRING CLIMATE SHOCKS

Somalia's crisis is compounded by extreme climate vulnerability. The country experiences cyclical droughts and floods related to El Nino and La Nina patterns, with the Horn of Africa's climate becoming increasingly erratic. After the devastating drought of 2022, Somalia experienced flooding in 2023 related to El Nino conditions that displaced hundreds of thousands who had not recovered from the drought. A single country experiencing both severe drought and flooding within a twelve-month period illustrates the climate adaptation challenges facing pastoralist and agropastoralist communities whose livelihoods are inherently weather-dependent. The destruction of livestock during droughts eliminates both food sources and productive assets for pastoral communities, requiring years of recovery after each climate shock.

GOVERNANCE, SECURITY, AND LONG-TERM RECOVERY

Somalia's Provisional Federal Government has consolidated some territorial control, particularly following President Hassan Sheikh Mohamud's 2022 election and renewed military operations against al-Shabaab that achieved significant territorial gains in 2023. The African Union Transition Mission in Somalia (ATMIS), the successor to AMISOM, provided critical security support. However, governance challenges remain profound: clan-based political competition, corruption, very limited revenue collection, and the need to maintain the cooperation of federal member states constrain the government's ability to deliver services or mount effective humanitarian responses. International investment in drought-resistant agriculture, livestock insurance schemes, social protection systems, and urban economic opportunities has grown but remains insufficient relative to need. Somalia's recurrent crisis cycle, in which the country reaches near-famine conditions every few years, represents a fundamental failure of both development investment and the global humanitarian system to address root causes.
