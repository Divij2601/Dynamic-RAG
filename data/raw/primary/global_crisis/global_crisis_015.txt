BANGLADESH FLOODING AND CLIMATE DISPLACEMENT

Bangladesh is one of the world's most climate-vulnerable countries, regularly experiencing devastating floods, cyclones, and riverbank erosion that collectively displace millions of people annually. With a population of over 170 million living at an average elevation of less than ten meters above sea level in a highly flood-prone delta system, Bangladesh faces existential challenges from climate change that are already manifesting in escalating disaster frequency and intensity.

GEOGRAPHY AND CLIMATE VULNERABILITY

Bangladesh occupies the delta of three major Himalayan river systems: the Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Meghna. Approximately 80 percent of the country is floodplain, and seasonal monsoon flooding is a natural and historically manageable feature of the landscape that sustains agricultural productivity. However, climate change is altering the character of flooding: sea level rise increases tidal and storm surge intrusion into coastal areas; glacial melt in the Himalayas augments dry season river flows while intensifying flash floods; and changing precipitation patterns create more extreme wet and dry cycles. Cyclones from the Bay of Bengal have historically been major killers, though improved early warning and cyclone shelter systems have dramatically reduced mortality from storm events since the catastrophic 1991 cyclone that killed over 130,000 people.

MAJOR FLOOD EVENTS AND DISPLACEMENT

Bangladesh experiences annual monsoon flooding that in severe years inundates one-third or more of the country's territory. The 2022 Sylhet region floods were among the worst in two decades, inundating over four million people, displacing hundreds of thousands, destroying crops and infrastructure, and affecting populations still recovering from COVID-19 economic shocks. The northeastern regions, including Sylhet and Sunamganj, experienced flooding that submerged homes to rooftop level, with aid access severely hampered by the scale of inundation. In 2024, severe flooding in the Feni, Comilla, and Noakhali districts of eastern Bangladesh was linked to upstream water release from dams in India's Tripura state, adding a transboundary dimension that provoked public debate in Bangladesh about water-sharing arrangements. These flood events displace millions temporarily each year and a smaller but significant number permanently, particularly in coastal and riverine erosion-prone areas.

COASTAL EROSION AND CLIMATE MIGRATION

Permanent displacement from climate-related processes is a significant and growing phenomenon in Bangladesh. River erosion eats away at char islands and riverbanks, permanently displacing communities with no prospect of return. Saline water intrusion into coastal agricultural land from sea level rise and storm surges renders farming impossible, forcing migration. The coastal district of Satkhira and the Sundarban mangrove region have experienced significant livelihood loss and out-migration driven by increasing salinity and cyclone damage. Research by organizations including the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre has documented hundreds of thousands of climate-linked permanent internal migrants in Bangladesh annually, many of whom settle in urban slums in Dhaka, Chittagong, and other cities with very limited services and economic opportunities.

ADAPTATION POLICY AND INTERNATIONAL CLIMATE JUSTICE

Bangladesh has been a global leader in climate adaptation investment relative to its economic size, building cyclone shelters, improving early warning systems, developing flood-resistant agricultural varieties, and implementing coastal embankment programs. The Bangladesh Delta Plan 2100 is one of the most comprehensive long-term delta management frameworks in the developing world. However, the scale of investment required to adequately protect Bangladesh's population from worsening climate impacts vastly exceeds domestic resources. Bangladesh has been a leading voice in climate justice arguments at international forums including COP climate negotiations, arguing that developing nations most vulnerable to climate impacts caused primarily by wealthy nation emissions deserve substantial climate finance for both adaptation and loss and damage. The Loss and Damage fund established at COP27 in 2022, while a political breakthrough, remained vastly underfunded relative to assessed needs of vulnerable nations like Bangladesh as of 2025.
