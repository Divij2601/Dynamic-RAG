ROHINGYA CRISIS: BANGLADESH, COX'S BAZAR, AND A PROTRACTED DISPLACEMENT

The Rohingya crisis represents one of the world's most protracted and severe refugee situations. Following the Myanmar military's violent crackdown against the Rohingya Muslim minority in Rakhine State in August 2017, described by the UN as a textbook example of ethnic cleansing and investigated as genocide, over 700,000 Rohingya fled to Bangladesh within months. By 2025, approximately one million Rohingya refugees remained in the world's largest refugee settlement at Cox's Bazar, with no durable solution in sight.

ORIGINS AND 2017 CRISIS

The Rohingya are a predominantly Muslim ethnic minority from Myanmar's Rakhine State who have faced systematic discrimination, statelessness, and periodic violence for decades. Myanmar's government and Buddhist majority have largely refused to recognize them as citizens, rendering them stateless under the 1982 Citizenship Law. The August 2017 military crackdown, launched nominally in response to Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army attacks on police posts, involved mass killings, sexual violence, and the burning of hundreds of villages in what the UN Fact-Finding Mission concluded were acts constituting crimes against humanity and genocide. The speed and scale of the exodus to Bangladesh overwhelmed existing refugee settlements and created the Kutupalong-Balukhali mega-camp complex in Cox's Bazar district, which rapidly became the world's largest refugee settlement housing over 900,000 people in highly congested conditions.

CONDITIONS IN COX'S BAZAR

Life in Cox's Bazar refugee settlements has remained extremely difficult years after the initial emergency phase. Bamboo and plastic shelters in flood-prone and cyclone-exposed terrain create persistent vulnerability to natural disasters. Kutupalong and surrounding camps have experienced devastating fires, with one in 2021 destroying tens of thousands of shelters and displacing over 45,000 people in a single day. Educational access is severely limited, with Bangladesh restricting formal education for Rohingya children and allowing only informal learning centers, leaving an entire generation without recognized qualifications. Freedom of movement outside camps has been restricted, preventing meaningful economic self-reliance. Mental health needs are immense, given the trauma of the 2017 violence and years of confined, uncertain existence. The UN and NGOs provide food rations, healthcare, and basic services, but humanitarian funding has declined relative to peak emergency levels despite the crisis's continuity.

REPATRIATION OBSTACLES AND STATELESSNESS

Prospects for the voluntary, safe, and dignified repatriation of Rohingya to Myanmar have remained essentially zero throughout the crisis period. Myanmar's military government, which seized power in the February 2021 coup, has shown no willingness to grant Rohingya citizenship, guarantee safety, or create conditions for return. A pilot repatriation scheme in 2018 that was supposed to return an initial group of individuals failed completely when no refugees agreed to go, citing the absence of safety guarantees. Bangladesh has repeatedly sought international support for repatriation but lacks leverage over Myanmar. The February 2021 military coup worsened prospects further, as the junta has been preoccupied with suppressing nationwide pro-democracy resistance and shows no interest in addressing Rohingya rights. The International Court of Justice proceedings brought by Gambia under the Genocide Convention have continued, but judicial processes offer no near-term protection.

REGIONAL DYNAMICS AND ONWARD MOVEMENT

Prolonged confinement, restricted livelihoods, and hopelessness have driven increasing numbers of Rohingya to attempt dangerous sea journeys to Malaysia, Indonesia, and Thailand. Boat crossings through the Bay of Bengal and Andaman Sea have resulted in hundreds of deaths from drowning, dehydration, and violence by traffickers. Regional states have periodically refused to allow Rohingya boats to land, creating humanitarian emergencies at sea. Malaysia and Thailand have large existing Rohingya diaspora populations living without legal status. The international community has struggled to develop regional responses, with ASEAN's non-interference norms and reluctance to criticize member state Myanmar severely limiting the bloc's effectiveness. The crisis illustrates the failure of international protection systems when a neighboring state refuses return and host state capacity is exceeded, leaving a stateless population in indefinite limbo.
