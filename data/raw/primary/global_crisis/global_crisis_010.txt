SAHEL HUMANITARIAN EMERGENCY AND FOOD INSECURITY

The Sahel region of West Africa, encompassing countries including Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Chad, Mauritania, and the Lake Chad Basin, has experienced a converging set of crises since the 2010s that by 2024 constituted one of the world's most severe and under-resourced humanitarian emergencies. The intersection of jihadist insurgency, military coups, climate-driven food insecurity, and governance collapse has displaced millions and left tens of millions facing acute hunger.

SECURITY DETERIORATION AND JIHADIST EXPANSION

The security situation in the Sahel has deteriorated dramatically since the collapse of Libya in 2011 and the subsequent regional spread of weapons and armed groups. Mali, where a military coup in 2012 created space for jihadist expansion in the north, became the epicenter of a regional insurgency that subsequently spread to Burkina Faso and Niger. Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin (JNIM), affiliated with al-Qaeda, and the Islamic State in the Greater Sahara have expanded their territorial control, driving out government forces, closing schools and health facilities, and targeting civilians perceived as collaborators. Burkina Faso has been particularly devastated, with 40 percent of the country's territory effectively outside government control by 2024, over two million internally displaced, and the capital Ouagadougou surrounded by territories under insurgent influence. Military coups in Mali (2020, 2021), Burkina Faso (2022), and Niger (2023) have replaced elected governments with military juntas that expelled French forces and turned to Russia's Wagner Group (rebranded as Africa Corps) for security assistance.

FOOD INSECURITY AND CLIMATE DRIVERS

Food insecurity in the Sahel is driven by a combination of conflict-induced displacement, climate variability, and structural underdevelopment. The Lake Chad Basin has experienced a 90 percent shrinkage of Lake Chad over recent decades due to climate change and water extraction, devastating the livelihoods of millions who depended on fishing, farming, and pastoralism. Rainfall variability, locust infestations, and soil degradation have reduced agricultural yields. The displacement of farming communities by conflict compounds food insecurity by reducing production. By 2024, the UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs estimated that approximately 18.4 million people in Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger, and Chad faced acute food insecurity. The Liptako-Gourma tri-border area between Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger, known as the "three borders" zone, had become one of the most dangerous and food-insecure regions on earth.

DISPLACEMENT AND HUMANITARIAN ACCESS

The combination of security threats and military junta governance has severely restricted humanitarian access across the Sahel. Burkina Faso's military government expelled French NGOs and imposed restrictions on humanitarian operations, while also limiting media coverage of the humanitarian situation. Aid workers have been killed, kidnapped, and expelled, forcing the suspension of critical programs. Over 2.7 million people in Burkina Faso were internally displaced by late 2024, with a significant proportion in areas inaccessible to humanitarian organizations. Humanitarian funding for the Sahel has been chronically insufficient, with annual funding appeals rarely exceeding 50 percent of assessed need. The relative lack of media coverage compared to crises in Europe or the Middle East contributes to donor disengagement.

GEOPOLITICAL REALIGNMENT AND REGIONAL IMPLICATIONS

The political realignment of Sahel military governments away from France and Western partners toward Russia represents a significant geopolitical shift with humanitarian consequences. The departure of French forces from Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger under agreements with their respective military juntas reduced both counterterrorism capacity and, indirectly, some humanitarian protection. The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) imposed sanctions on coup governments and threatened military intervention after the Niger coup in 2023, but ultimately backed down. The formation of the Alliance of Sahel States by Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger in 2023, and their announcement of withdrawal from ECOWAS in 2024, signaled a restructuring of West African regional architecture. The humanitarian implications of this geopolitical upheaval are significant: reduced Western engagement, ineffective regional governance responses, and persistent insecurity create conditions for continued crisis escalation.
