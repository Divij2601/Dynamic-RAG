WORLD WAR II: HISTORICAL OVERVIEW

Overview

World War II, also called the Second World War, was a global conflict that lasted from 1939 to 1945. It was the deadliest conflict in human history, resulting in an estimated seventy to eighty-five million deaths, including military personnel and civilians. The war involved the vast majority of the world's nations, organized into two opposing military alliances: the Allies and the Axis. It ended with the defeat of the Axis powers, the emergence of the United States and the Soviet Union as superpowers, and the beginning of the Cold War.

Causes

The causes of World War II are rooted in the unresolved tensions of World War I and the harsh terms of the Treaty of Versailles. The global economic crisis of the Great Depression, beginning in 1929, fueled political extremism. In Germany, Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party rose to power in 1933, pursuing aggressive expansionism, rearmament, and a racist ideology. Italy under Benito Mussolini and Japan under a militarist government also pursued expansion. The policy of appeasement by Britain and France, which allowed Germany to annex Austria and parts of Czechoslovakia, failed to prevent further aggression.

The Major Powers

The Axis powers were led by Germany, Italy, and Japan. The Allied powers were led by the United Kingdom, the Soviet Union, the United States, and China, along with many other nations and resistance movements.

The Outbreak and Course of the War

The war in Europe began on September 1, 1939, when Germany invaded Poland, prompting Britain and France to declare war on Germany. Using a strategy of rapid mechanized warfare known as blitzkrieg, Germany conquered much of Western Europe by 1940, including France. Britain, led by Prime Minister Winston Churchill, resisted alone for a period, surviving the aerial campaign known as the Battle of Britain.

In 1941, the war expanded dramatically. Germany invaded the Soviet Union in June in Operation Barbarossa, opening a vast and brutal Eastern Front. In December, Japan attacked the United States naval base at Pearl Harbor, bringing the United States into the war.

The tide turned against the Axis in 1942 and 1943. The Soviet victory at the Battle of Stalingrad marked a decisive turning point on the Eastern Front. In the Pacific, the United States victory at the Battle of Midway halted Japanese expansion. The Allied invasion of Normandy on June 6, 1944, known as D-Day, opened a Western Front in France.

The Holocaust

During the war, Nazi Germany carried out the Holocaust, the systematic genocide of approximately six million Jews, along with the murder of millions of others, including Roma, disabled people, political prisoners, and prisoners of war. The Holocaust stands as one of the greatest atrocities in human history.

The End of the War

Germany surrendered on May 8, 1945, a date celebrated as Victory in Europe Day, following the fall of Berlin to Soviet forces and the death of Adolf Hitler. In the Pacific, the war ended after the United States dropped atomic bombs on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945. Japan surrendered on August 15, 1945, with the formal surrender signed on September 2, 1945.

Consequences

World War II reshaped the world order. It led to the creation of the United Nations in 1945 to prevent future global conflicts. Europe was divided between Western democracies and a Soviet-dominated Eastern bloc, setting the stage for the Cold War. The war accelerated decolonization, established the United States and the Soviet Union as superpowers, and ushered in the nuclear age.
