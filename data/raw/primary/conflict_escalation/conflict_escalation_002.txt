INDIA-PAKISTAN NUCLEAR THRESHOLD DYNAMICS

OVERVIEW
The India-Pakistan nuclear relationship is characterized by asymmetric doctrines, limited transparency, and structural incentives for early nuclear use on the Pakistani side. Unlike the US-Soviet Cold War dyad, neither state has a robust crisis stability architecture, and both have doctrines that, under certain scenarios, could generate pressure for rapid escalation to nuclear use. Understanding where the nuclear threshold lies — and what factors might lower or raise it — is central to conflict risk assessment in South Asia.

CONTRASTING DOCTRINES
India maintains a declared No First Use (NFU) policy, pledging not to initiate nuclear use but reserving the right to massive retaliation if attacked with nuclear, biological, or chemical weapons. However, Indian strategic thinkers and former officials have increasingly questioned whether NFU would survive a large-scale conventional loss or a Pakistani tactical nuclear detonation. India's 2003 nuclear doctrine update introduced ambiguity by extending the retaliation pledge to CBW attacks, suggesting a willingness to respond with nuclear weapons to non-nuclear provocations under some circumstances. Pakistan has explicitly rejected NFU, maintaining a posture of "Full Spectrum Deterrence" that encompasses first use of nuclear weapons if Pakistan faces an existential conventional military defeat. Pakistani nuclear red lines include deep conventional penetration of Pakistani territory, destruction of a significant portion of its armed forces, economic stranglehold, or internal destabilization attributable to Indian interference.

TACTICAL NUCLEAR WEAPONS AND THRESHOLD LOWERING
Pakistan's development of short-range tactical nuclear systems — particularly the Nasr (Hatf-IX) missile with an estimated range of 60-70 km — is widely assessed as a threshold-lowering development. Designed to be used against Indian armored columns advancing into Pakistani territory under the Cold Start doctrine, Nasr weapons may be stored in forward-deployed locations with delegated use authorities, raising risks of unauthorized use, accidents, or rapid escalation during a conventional clash. India's response has been to assert that any nuclear use by Pakistan, regardless of yield or target, would trigger massive Indian retaliation, creating a strategic paradox where Pakistani tactical use is intended to stop conventional war but could instead produce genocidal exchanges.

ARSENAL DYNAMICS AND STABILITY
Both arsenals are expanding. Pakistan is estimated to possess 170-180 nuclear warheads as of 2025, while India possesses roughly 160-170. Both states are developing sea-based deterrents: India has operationalized the Arihant-class submarine-launched ballistic missile (SLBM) capability, while Pakistan is developing submarine-launched cruise missile options. The emergence of survivable second-strike capabilities on both sides theoretically enhances deterrence stability, but the opacity of command and control arrangements and the absence of agreed verification mechanisms create uncertainty about crisis behavior.

CRISIS STABILITY VULNERABILITIES
Several structural factors undermine crisis stability. First, the tight coupling between conventional and nuclear operations means that conventional military actions could inadvertently trigger nuclear responses. Second, the short flight times — as little as 3-4 minutes for ballistic missiles between major cities — compress decision-making timelines dangerously. Third, both sides have limited missile defense capabilities, meaning both leadership and population centers remain highly vulnerable to first strikes, creating use-it-or-lose-it pressures during crises. The 2019 Balakot crisis demonstrated that nuclear-armed states can exchange airstrikes without immediate escalation to nuclear use, but the outcome depended heavily on Pakistani restraint after the aerial engagement — a restraint that may not recur under more extreme scenarios involving deeper Indian strikes or greater territorial losses.

INTERNATIONAL COMMUNITY AND STABILIZATION EFFORTS
External actors, particularly the United States, China, and Gulf states, have played important de-escalatory roles during past India-Pakistan crises. However, the geopolitical realignment of recent years — with India deepening ties to the US-led Quad framework and Pakistan maintaining close relations with China — has complicated the ability of any single power to exercise effective restraint on both sides simultaneously. The absence of formal arms control dialogue between India and Pakistan, unlike the extensive US-Soviet treaty architecture, means that stabilization depends on informal norms and ad hoc crisis management rather than binding agreements.
