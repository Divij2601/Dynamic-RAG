RUSSIA-NATO ARTICLE 5 ESCALATION RISK

OVERVIEW
The Russia-Ukraine war that began with full-scale invasion in February 2022 has created unprecedented escalation risks on NATO's eastern flank. Russia and NATO are engaged in a proxy war in which NATO supplies weapons, intelligence, and training to Ukraine while Russia wages conventional and hybrid warfare against a NATO aspirant state. The risk of direct Russia-NATO military confrontation — which would trigger Article 5 collective defense obligations — has been elevated throughout the conflict and remains a central concern for European security planning in 2025-2026.

ARTICLE 5 TRIGGER SCENARIOS
Article 5 of the North Atlantic Treaty declares that an armed attack against one member is an attack against all, obligating allies to take "such action as it deems necessary." The most plausible Article 5 trigger scenarios arising from the Ukraine conflict include: Russian missiles or drones straying into Polish, Romanian, or Baltic state territory (a Ukrainian air defense missile fell in Polish territory in November 2022, causing brief alarm); deliberate Russian strikes on NATO territory in retaliation for weapons supplies or hosting of Ukrainian forces; Russian operations against NATO logistics corridors in Poland or Romania; and Russian operations against Baltic state infrastructure interpreted as consistent with the Gerasimov doctrine of hybrid warfare below kinetic thresholds.

BALTIC STATE VULNERABILITY
The three Baltic states — Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania — are NATO's most exposed members. Their proximity to Kaliningrad (a Russian exclave with significant military assets including Iskander-M short-range ballistic missiles) and the Suwalki Gap (the 60-70 km land corridor between Belarus and Kaliningrad connecting the Baltics to the rest of NATO) makes them strategically vulnerable. NATO's Enhanced Forward Presence, with multinational battalions in each Baltic state and Poland, provides a tripwire deterrent but is insufficient for sustained territorial defense against a major Russian offensive. NATO has gradually escalated its commitments: reinforcing the EFP, pre-positioning heavy equipment, and pledging to defend every inch of allied territory.

ESCALATION MANAGEMENT DURING UKRAINE WAR
NATO has managed a complex escalation balance throughout the Ukraine war, providing increasingly capable weapons systems — from MANPADS to artillery to HIMARS to Abrams tanks to F-16 aircraft — while calibrating deliveries to maintain deterrence without triggering direct Russia-NATO conflict. The US has generally imposed restrictions on weapons use against targets inside Russian territory, which Ukraine sought and gradually received partial permission to circumvent, particularly for cross-border counterfire. Each weapons decision involved escalation calculus: would Russian red lines be credible, and what was the cost of accommodating them versus the benefit of greater Ukrainian military capability?

RUSSIAN ESCALATION SIGNALING AND CREDIBILITY
Russia has extensively used nuclear signaling to deter Western support for Ukraine, including alerts of nuclear forces, withdrawal from New START (which Russia suspended in February 2023), and explicit threats by senior officials including former President Medvedev. NATO has generally assessed these signals as largely rhetorical but has been cautious about actions that could be interpreted as escalating to direct confrontation. The credibility question is central: if NATO over-accommodates Russian nuclear threats, it sets a precedent that nuclear coercion works, potentially encouraging future Russian escalation or emboldening other nuclear-armed states.

STRUCTURAL ESCALATION RISKS
Several structural factors elevate Article 5 escalation risk beyond explicit Russian policy decisions. First, the fog of war in Ukraine creates conditions where Russian strikes near the Polish or Romanian borders could accidentally cross NATO territory. Second, Russian intelligence operations against NATO members — including alleged sabotage of Baltic undersea cables and pipeline infrastructure — constitute hybrid attacks that may not cross the Article 5 threshold but progressively erode the norm against attacking allied territory. Third, Russian military setbacks in Ukraine could generate domestic pressure on the Kremlin for escalatory action to demonstrate resolve and avoid appearing weak, creating a paradox where Ukrainian military success may increase the risk of Russian escalation against NATO.
