SAUDI ARABIA-IRAN HOUTHI PROXY ESCALATION

OVERVIEW
The Yemen conflict, which erupted into full-scale civil war in 2015 following the Houthi takeover of Sanaa and subsequent Saudi-led military intervention, represents the central theater of the Saudi Arabia-Iran proxy competition. The Houthi movement (Ansar Allah), supported by Iran through weapons supplies, training, and intelligence, has transformed from a regional insurgency into a significant regional military actor capable of striking targets across the Arabian Peninsula and the Red Sea. The conflict has escalated progressively, incorporating drone and missile attacks on Saudi infrastructure, Red Sea shipping interdiction, and direct attacks on Israel, creating a complex multi-party escalation dynamic.

IRAN'S PROXY INVESTMENT IN HOUTHIS
Iran's support for the Houthis follows its "axis of resistance" model: providing lethal capability to proxy forces to project power and impose costs on adversaries without direct Iranian military engagement. Weapons supplies to the Houthis have included Iranian-designed Shahed kamikaze drones, anti-ship missiles, ballistic missiles with ranges exceeding 1,000 kilometers, and naval mines. The weapons supply chain runs through Oman and is periodically disrupted by US and allied interdiction operations. Iran denies supplying weapons, claiming the Houthis manufacture their own — a claim that weapons analysis consistently refutes.

MAJOR ESCALATION EVENTS
The September 2019 drone and missile attack on Saudi Aramco's Abqaiq and Khurais oil processing facilities — the most consequential attack on global energy infrastructure in decades — temporarily knocked out approximately 5% of global oil supply. The US attributed the attack to Iran (or Iran-directed forces), but the Houthis claimed responsibility. Saudi Arabia's response was restrained — no military retaliation against Iran or the perpetrators — a fact that Iran's proxies interpreted as demonstrating Saudi vulnerability without consequences. From 2023 onward, Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping in "solidarity" with Palestinians amid the Gaza war disrupted a major global trade artery, triggering US and UK airstrikes on Houthi targets in Yemen beginning in January 2024 under Operation Prosperity Guardian.

RED SEA SHIPPING CAMPAIGN AND INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE
The Houthi campaign against Red Sea shipping that began in November 2023 forced major shipping companies to reroute vessels around Africa's Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-14 days and significant fuel costs to Asia-Europe trade routes. More than 100 commercial vessels were attacked through 2024, with several sunk or seized. The US-led multinational naval force (Operation Prosperity Guardian) and subsequent Operation Poseidon Archer airstrikes on Houthi targets achieved degraded but not eliminated Houthi capabilities. Houthi resilience against intensive bombing demonstrated the limits of air power against a distributed, underground, and ideologically committed armed group — lessons with broader implications for proxy conflict management.

SAUDI-IRAN NORMALIZATION AND ITS LIMITS
The March 2023 Chinese-brokered Saudi-Iran normalization agreement, which restored diplomatic relations after a seven-year rupture, was hailed as a potential de-escalation breakthrough. However, the normalization has been limited in scope and has not translated into Houthi cessation of attacks on Saudi-linked or international targets. Iran's leverage over the Houthis is real but not absolute — the movement has its own strategic interests, domestic political dynamics, and tribal structure that limit Iranian control. The normalization has reduced direct Saudi-Iranian military risk but has not resolved the proxy competition that sustains Yemen's conflict, suggesting a prolonged pattern of managed hostility rather than genuine reconciliation.

ESCALATION OUTLOOK
The Yemen conflict and the Saudi-Iran proxy dynamic are likely to persist in a form of managed competition through 2026 and beyond. Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 economic transformation requires regional stability, creating incentives for negotiated outcomes. Iran's proxy deterrent provides it leverage that it is unlikely to surrender without significant concessions. The Houthi movement has its own interests in perpetuating conflict as a source of revenue (through smuggling and taxation) and political legitimacy. A ceasefire negotiated under UN auspices has held tenuously since April 2022 but has been repeatedly violated. Full resolution requires political settlement of Yemen's internal governance crisis, which remains distant.
