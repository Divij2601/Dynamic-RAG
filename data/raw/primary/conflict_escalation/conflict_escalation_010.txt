RUSSIA NUCLEAR SIGNALING IN THE UKRAINE CONFLICT

OVERVIEW
Russia's nuclear signaling since the February 2022 invasion of Ukraine represents the most extensive and intensive use of nuclear threats by a major power since the Cold War. President Putin, senior officials, and state media have repeatedly invoked Russia's nuclear arsenal to deter Western military intervention, limit weapons supplies to Ukraine, and coerce Ukraine into accepting Russian territorial gains. Analyzing the content, context, and credibility of this signaling is essential for understanding escalation dynamics in Europe and for developing appropriate deterrence responses.

FORMS OF NUCLEAR SIGNALING
Russian nuclear signaling has operated across multiple channels simultaneously. Presidential speeches have referenced Russia's "weapons of mass destruction" and made explicit comparisons to the USSR's deterrence posture. Former President Medvedev has made the most explicit threats, including statements that Russia could use nuclear weapons if its territorial integrity — including annexed Ukrainian territories — was threatened, and references to Sarmat ICBMs capable of striking "unsinkable" aircraft carriers. State media, particularly Rossiya-1, hosted extensive discussions of nuclear strike scenarios against Western capitals, functioning as psychological warfare. Military exercises, including snap inspections of strategic nuclear forces and exercises of tactical nuclear delivery systems in Belarus, provided kinetic demonstrations to accompany rhetorical signals.

SEPTEMBER 2022 ANNEXATION AND DOCTRINAL IMPLICATIONS
Putin's September 2022 announcement of the annexation of four Ukrainian regions — Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhzhia, and Kherson — accompanied by a nuclear speech explicitly stating Russia would use "all available means" to defend these territories created a doctrinal ambiguity. Russian nuclear doctrine allows nuclear use in defense of Russian territory. If these annexed regions are treated as Russian territory under that doctrine, then Ukrainian conventional operations to recapture them could theoretically cross a nuclear threshold. Western governments largely dismissed this framing as illegitimate, but the ambiguity created some restraint in weapons supply decisions.

ASSESSMENT OF CREDIBILITY
Western intelligence agencies and independent analysts have generally assessed Russia's nuclear threats as primarily coercive rather than pre-operational. The Kremlin's calculus against actual nuclear use includes: catastrophic international isolation (including potential loss of Chinese and Indian support), NATO conventional retaliation options, physical risks to Russian forces from fallout, and the precedent-setting consequences for global nonproliferation. CIA Director Bill Burns and other officials stated publicly in late 2022 that the US saw no signs of imminent nuclear use. However, this credibility assessment is not uniform: some analysts argue the bar for tactical nuclear use is lower than the West believes, particularly if Russia faces catastrophic conventional defeat that threatens regime stability.

WESTERN COUNTER-SIGNALING
The Biden administration developed a nuanced counter-signaling strategy during the conflict, combining public statements of consequences ("catastrophic consequences") with private messages through direct communication channels with Moscow. The administration calibrated weapons supplies to avoid directly enabling strikes on Russian territory while maintaining Ukrainian military capability. This approach has been criticized for being over-accommodating to Russian nuclear threats, creating a precedent that nuclear coercion constrains US policy. The Trump administration's approach from early 2025 shifted toward seeking a negotiated settlement, reducing the intensity of the escalation management challenge but raising questions about whether a compromised peace would embolden future Russian nuclear coercion.

LONG-TERM IMPLICATIONS FOR DETERRENCE NORMS
The extensive Russian nuclear signaling during the Ukraine war, combined with Western restraint in some weapons decisions, risks establishing a norm that nuclear threats constrain conventional responses by non-nuclear adversaries or nuclear-armed allies. This "nuclear blackmail" dynamic, if perceived as successful, could encourage other nuclear-armed states to employ similar tactics in future conflicts. The maintenance of NATO conventional deterrence credibility while avoiding escalation has been a central tension throughout the conflict, with long-term implications for European security architecture and extended deterrence commitments that will persist well beyond the resolution of the Ukraine conflict itself.
