IRAN-ISRAEL SHADOW WAR ESCALATION

OVERVIEW
The Iran-Israel conflict has been conducted predominantly through covert operations, proxy forces, and limited direct strikes rather than open conventional warfare. This "shadow war" has intensified dramatically since 2019, encompassing Israeli strikes on Iranian personnel in Syria, Iranian-linked attacks on Israeli-linked shipping in the Gulf, cyber operations by both sides, and unprecedented direct military exchanges in April and October 2024. The trajectory of this conflict — from shadows into the open — represents a significant escalation that raises questions about whether the shadow war framework can continue to contain the rivalry.

THE SHADOW WAR ARCHITECTURE
Israel has conducted hundreds of airstrikes on Iranian military assets in Syria since approximately 2013, targeting weapons convoys destined for Hezbollah, Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) advisers, and precision-guided munitions manufacturing facilities. Iran has responded through proxy harassment: Hezbollah rocket and missile stockpiling in Lebanon, Iranian-linked drone attacks on Israeli-linked ships in the Red Sea and Gulf of Oman, and intelligence operations against Israeli nationals abroad. The shadow war maintained a tacit understanding: neither side would directly strike the other's home territory, allowing escalation management through proxy and third-country operations.

THE APRIL 2024 BREAKING POINT
The April 1, 2024 Israeli airstrike on the Iranian consular annex in Damascus — which killed seven IRGC officers including two senior generals — crossed a threshold that Iran could not absorb without direct response without losing deterrence credibility. On April 13-14, 2024, Iran launched the first direct attack on Israeli territory in history: over 300 drones, cruise missiles, and ballistic missiles aimed at Israeli territory. Approximately 99% were intercepted by Israel's layered missile defense system — Iron Dome, David's Sling, Arrow — with assistance from US, UK, French, and Jordanian forces. While the attack caused minimal damage, it shattered the framework of implicit rules governing the shadow war and demonstrated that direct interstate exchanges were now possible.

OCTOBER 2024 ESCALATION
Following Israel's assassination of Hamas leader Ismail Haniyeh in Tehran in July 2024 and the killing of Hezbollah Secretary-General Hassan Nasrallah in September 2024, Iran launched a second direct ballistic missile attack on Israel on October 1, 2024, firing approximately 200 Fattah and Shahab-3 missiles. Several missiles struck Israeli territory, including near military bases. Israel's October 26 retaliatory strike targeted Iranian air defense systems, including S-300 batteries, demonstrating Israel's ability to penetrate Iranian air space. This exchange established a new norm of direct bilateral military strikes, fundamentally altering the escalation architecture.

ESCALATION PATHWAYS GOING FORWARD
The most dangerous escalation scenarios involve Iran deciding to cross the nuclear threshold, either by expelling IAEA inspectors and pursuing breakout, or by deploying a nuclear device as an ultimate deterrent against Israeli conventional superiority. A second scenario involves a massive Israeli conventional strike on Iranian nuclear facilities — potentially requiring US support for tanker aircraft and munitions — that Iran interprets as existential, triggering a maximal regional response through Hezbollah, Houthi, and Iraqi militia proxies. A third scenario involves conflict spillover from Gaza or Lebanon drawing Iran into direct conventional engagement it sought to avoid.

STRUCTURAL FACTORS SUSTAINING THE CONFLICT
Iran's ideological commitment to Palestinian liberation and its strategic interest in encircling Israel with proxy forces ensures that the underlying drivers of conflict persist regardless of tactical escalation management. Israel's existential perception of Iranian nuclear capability means it cannot accept a nuclear-armed Iran and retains contingency options for unilateral military action. The weakening of Hezbollah and Hamas in 2024-2025 following Israeli military operations has temporarily degraded Iran's proxy deterrent but has also increased incentives for Iran to more directly threaten Israel to restore deterrence credibility, creating a paradox where proxy weakening may accelerate direct escalation.
