US-CHINA SOUTH CHINA SEA ESCALATION

OVERVIEW
The South China Sea (SCS) is a maritime theater in which overlapping territorial claims, strategic waterways, and competing great-power interests converge. China claims approximately 90% of the SCS based on its "Nine-Dash Line," a historical claim rejected by the 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration ruling, which Beijing dismisses. The United States conducts Freedom of Navigation Operations (FONOPs) to contest Chinese maritime claims, while China has constructed and militarized artificial islands. The Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan all have competing claims. Escalation in this theater can emerge from multiple nodes, each with distinct dynamics.

ARTIFICIAL ISLAND MILITARIZATION AND STANDOFFS
China's rapid construction of artificial islands on reefs and shoals in the Spratly and Paracel chains between 2013 and 2016 created new facts on the water. The islands now feature military airstrips, radar installations, anti-ship missile batteries, and surface-to-air missiles. Philippine resupply missions to BRP Sierra Madre grounded on Ayungin Shoal (Second Thomas Shoal) have repeatedly been intercepted by China Coast Guard and People's Armed Forces Maritime Militia vessels using water cannons, lasers, and physical boarding. These incidents escalated dramatically in 2023-2025, with injuries to Filipino sailors and the seizure of supplies. Under the 1951 US-Philippines Mutual Defense Treaty, a Philippines-China armed clash could trigger US involvement.

FREEDOM OF NAVIGATION OPERATIONS
US Navy FONOPs transit within 12 nautical miles of Chinese-claimed features to reject excessive maritime claims. China responds with naval and coast guard vessels that shadow or challenge these transits. In several incidents, PLA Navy vessels have made aggressive intercepts of US ships and aircraft — most notably the 2023 incident in which a Chinese fighter jet flew within meters of a US reconnaissance aircraft, and the 2024 incidents involving laser illumination of US and allied aircraft. These tactical-level interactions carry escalation potential if a miscalculation results in a collision or exchange of fire. The USS Cowpens incident in 2013 and the EP-3 collision in 2001 illustrate the historical pattern.

PHILIPPINES AS ESCALATION NEXUS
The Philippines under President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. (2022-present) has adopted a significantly more assertive posture toward China than the Duterte administration, reopening and expanding access to US military bases under EDCA, conducting joint patrols with US forces, and publicly documenting Chinese harassment. The US has explicitly invoked the Mutual Defense Treaty in relation to Philippine Coast Guard and armed forces in the SCS, reducing the ambiguity that previously allowed China to harass Philippine vessels with lower escalation risk. This treaty clarity is a deterrence improvement but also means that if China escalates beyond a threshold, the US is now committed to respond.

ENERGY RESOURCES AND STRATEGIC INCENTIVES
The SCS contains significant hydrocarbon resources, with estimates ranging from 11 billion to 125 billion barrels of oil equivalent depending on methodology. Competition over Exclusive Economic Zone fishing rights and energy development rights drives state behavior beyond pure sovereignty claims. Vietnam has been the most active driller in disputed waters, periodically leading to Chinese pressure on international energy companies operating in Vietnamese contract areas. Resource competition sustained over years can create cumulative grievances that make political de-escalation harder to achieve, even when both sides have incentives to avoid armed conflict.

DE-ESCALATION MECHANISMS AND THEIR LIMITS
ASEAN's Code of Conduct (COC) negotiations for the SCS have proceeded at a glacial pace, with China insisting on provisions that would exclude external navies — i.e., the United States — from operating in the region. Even if concluded, a non-binding COC would lack enforcement mechanisms. Bilateral hotlines between the US and Chinese militaries exist but were suspended by Beijing following the 2022 Pelosi visit to Taiwan and were only partially restored in late 2023-2024. The combination of competing sovereignty claims, resource interests, domestic nationalist pressures, and great-power competition makes the SCS a persistently high-risk escalation environment throughout the 2025-2030 forecast period.
