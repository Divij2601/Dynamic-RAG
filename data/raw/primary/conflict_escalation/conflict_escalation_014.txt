ISRAEL-HEZBOLLAH LEBANON ESCALATION DYNAMICS

OVERVIEW
The Israel-Hezbollah conflict entered a new and catastrophic phase in September-October 2024, following nearly a year of low-intensity exchanges along the Lebanon-Israel border that had persisted since Hezbollah opened its "solidarity front" with Hamas after the October 7, 2023 attacks. Israel's targeted killing of Hezbollah Secretary-General Hassan Nasrallah on September 27, 2024, and the subsequent Israeli ground invasion of southern Lebanon, represented the most significant escalation in the conflict since the 2006 Second Lebanon War. The 2024 war substantially degraded Hezbollah's military capability but did not resolve the underlying political and strategic conditions that generate the conflict.

HEZBOLLAH'S MILITARY BUILDUP SINCE 2006
The 2006 war's conclusion under UN Security Council Resolution 1701 called for Hezbollah's disarmament and the establishment of a weapons-free zone south of the Litani River. These provisions were never implemented, and Hezbollah used the subsequent eighteen years to build what was arguably the most powerful non-state military force in history. By late 2023, Hezbollah possessed an estimated 120,000-150,000 rockets and missiles, including precision-guided munitions capable of striking specific Israeli military and infrastructure targets. The organization had developed sophisticated anti-tank guided missile capabilities, drone warfare capacity, and tunnel networks in southern Lebanon that posed significant challenges to conventional military operations.

THE 2024 WAR: COURSE AND ESCALATION DYNAMICS
The assassination of Hezbollah commanders in July-September 2024, including senior military commander Fuad Shukr in Beirut, preceded the dramatic September 2024 pager-bomb operation that simultaneously detonated communications devices used by Hezbollah operatives, killing and maiming hundreds. This sophisticated intelligence operation was followed within days by the killing of the Hezbollah leadership in a massive bunker-buster strike on Hezbollah's Dahiya headquarters in Beirut. The ground invasion of southern Lebanon, launched in late September 2024, encountered significant resistance but progressively dismantled Hezbollah's tunnel network and forward fighting positions. A US-brokered ceasefire in late November 2024 halted the main fighting, with Israeli forces withdrawing from Lebanon over subsequent months.

POST-WAR HEZBOLLAH RECONSTITUTION
The November 2024 ceasefire agreement, which Lebanon's army was to enforce under US and French guarantees, has been challenged by the pace of Hezbollah reconstitution. The organization, significantly degraded by the 2024 war — which killed thousands of fighters, destroyed weapons stockpiles, and eliminated much of the senior leadership — began rebuilding under new Secretary-General Naim Qassem. Iran's restocking capacity and Hezbollah's organizational resilience suggest a partial reconstitution is underway, though the pace is constrained by Israeli strikes on weapons supply routes from Syria and Lebanon. The long-term strategic question is whether the 2024 war fundamentally changed the deterrence equilibrium or merely set back Hezbollah's military capacity temporarily.

STRUCTURAL DRIVERS OF RECURRING CONFLICT
The Israel-Hezbollah conflict is structurally driven by factors that no military operation resolves. Hezbollah's integration into Lebanese politics — it holds significant parliamentary representation and cabinet posts — means it cannot be treated as a purely external military threat without destabilizing the Lebanese state. Iran's strategic interest in maintaining a deterrent capability against Israel through Hezbollah means restocking operations will continue unless Iran's regional posture fundamentally changes. Israel's security doctrine requires elimination of threats on its northern border, creating periodic military imperatives regardless of ceasefire agreements. Lebanon's state weakness and sectarian governance structure prevent Beirut from asserting sovereignty over Hezbollah's armed wing.

ESCALATION RISKS IN 2025-2026
The most dangerous near-term escalation scenario involves Iran deciding to use Hezbollah's remaining capabilities to retaliate for Israeli strikes on Iran, or Hezbollah initiating a new campaign to reassert deterrence credibility following the devastating losses of 2024. A second scenario involves Israeli intelligence operations in Lebanon triggering Hezbollah responses that escalate into another round of open warfare before Hezbollah has rebuilt sufficiently to sustain a prolonged conflict — a mismatch that could drive Hezbollah toward quick escalation or Iran toward direct engagement.
