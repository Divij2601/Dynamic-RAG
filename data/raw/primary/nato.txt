THE NORTH ATLANTIC TREATY ORGANIZATION (NATO): ALLIANCE PROFILE

Overview

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization, commonly known as NATO, is an intergovernmental military alliance between North American and European countries. It was established to provide collective security against the Soviet Union during the early Cold War. As of the mid-2020s, following the accession of Finland in 2023 and Sweden in 2024, NATO has thirty-two member states. Its political headquarters is in Brussels, Belgium, and its principal military command, Supreme Headquarters Allied Powers Europe, is located near Mons, Belgium.

Founding and the North Atlantic Treaty

NATO was founded by the North Atlantic Treaty, also called the Washington Treaty, signed on April 4, 1949. The twelve founding members were the United States, the United Kingdom, France, Canada, Italy, the Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, Denmark, Norway, Portugal, and Iceland. The alliance was created in the context of rising tensions with the Soviet Union and the desire of Western European states to secure an American commitment to their defense.

Article 5 and Collective Defense

The cornerstone of NATO is Article 5 of the North Atlantic Treaty, which establishes the principle of collective defense. Under Article 5, an armed attack against one member is considered an attack against all members, and each member commits to assist the attacked party. Article 5 has been formally invoked only once in NATO's history, by the United States following the terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001. The credibility of Article 5 is central to NATO's deterrent value.

Structure and Decision-Making

NATO's principal political decision-making body is the North Atlantic Council, in which all members are represented and decisions are made by consensus, meaning every member effectively has a veto. The Secretary General is the alliance's senior international civil servant and chief spokesperson, while military command is exercised through an integrated command structure led by the Supreme Allied Commander Europe, a position traditionally held by an American officer.

Cold War Role

Throughout the Cold War, NATO confronted the Warsaw Pact, the rival military alliance led by the Soviet Union and established in 1955. The two blocs faced each other across a divided Europe, with the heaviest concentration of forces in divided Germany. NATO's strategy combined conventional forces with the threat of nuclear retaliation to deter Soviet attack.

Post-Cold War Evolution and Enlargement

After the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 and the dissolution of the Warsaw Pact, NATO redefined its purpose and expanded eastward to include former Communist states of Central and Eastern Europe. Enlargement brought in countries such as Poland, the Czech Republic, and Hungary in 1999, and the Baltic states and others in 2004. NATO also undertook operations beyond its traditional area, including in the Balkans during the 1990s and in Afghanistan after 2001.

NATO and the War in Ukraine

The Russian annexation of Crimea in 2014 and the full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 revitalized NATO and reaffirmed its core mission of collective defense in Europe. The invasion prompted historically neutral Finland and Sweden to abandon non-alignment and join the alliance, significantly expanding NATO's presence along Russia's borders. Member states have increased defense spending and reinforced their eastern flank.

Contemporary Role

In the 2020s, NATO remains the most powerful military alliance in the world and the central institution of transatlantic security. It faces debates over burden-sharing among members, the appropriate level of defense spending, its relationship with an increasingly assertive Russia, and the long-term commitment of the United States to European defense.
