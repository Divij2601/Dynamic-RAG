APPLE SUPPLY CHAIN DIVERSIFICATION: INDIA, VIETNAM, AND THE CHINA PLUS ONE STRATEGY

OVERVIEW

Apple Inc., the world's most valuable company, has embarked on a multi-year effort to diversify its supply chain away from its overwhelming dependence on Chinese manufacturing. The effort, accelerating since 2020 in response to COVID-19 factory shutdowns, US-China trade tensions, and geopolitical risk concerns, has brought India and Vietnam to the forefront of electronics manufacturing. Apple's diversification is significant not merely for the company itself, but as a bellwether: given Apple's scale—roughly $400 billion in annual revenue—and its demands on suppliers, where Apple goes, the electronics supply chain follows.

CHINA'S HISTORICAL CENTRALITY TO APPLE MANUFACTURING

For most of its modern history, Apple's supply chain was synonymous with China, and specifically with Foxconn's enormous manufacturing complex in Zhengzhou—known informally as "iPhone City"—which employed over 300,000 workers at peak production. Apple's reliance on China reflected the country's unique advantages: a massive, disciplined industrial workforce; a deep ecosystem of component suppliers within short logistics distances; manufacturing infrastructure built specifically around electronics assembly; and government cooperation. At peak, China assembled over 95 percent of iPhones, 100 percent of Macs, and essentially all other Apple hardware categories. This concentration made Apple acutely vulnerable to COVID-related disruptions, as demonstrated in November 2022 when Foxconn Zhengzhou worker unrest during COVID lockdowns disrupted iPhone 14 Pro production at a critical holiday selling season.

INDIA AS AN IPHONE MANUFACTURING HUB

Apple began pilot iPhone production in India in 2017 through Wistron, and expanded through Foxconn and Tata Group. By 2024, India was assembling approximately 14-15 percent of global iPhones, up from near zero in 2020. The Indian government's Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme for mobile devices provided direct financial subsidies—roughly 4-6 percent of incremental sales—to manufacturers who increased production in India, making the economics viable. Tata Group's acquisition of Wistron's India operations in 2024 and its construction of a major iPhone assembly complex in Hosur, Tamil Nadu, signaled deep Indian industrial commitment to the sector. Apple has indicated its intention to produce a majority of iPhones sold in the US market from India-based facilities, driven partly by import tariff risk from US-China trade tensions.

VIETNAM'S ELECTRONICS ROLE

Vietnam has become Apple's primary location for AirPods, Apple Watch, and iPad assembly, as well as MacBook components. The country attracted Foxconn, Luxshare, and other Apple suppliers through competitive labor costs, stable governance, and membership in trade agreements including the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP) and EU-Vietnam Free Trade Agreement. Vietnam's electronics exports grew from approximately $3 billion in 2010 to over $120 billion in 2024, reflecting the country's emergence as a major electronics manufacturing hub. However, Vietnam's supply chain capacity remains deeply integrated with Chinese inputs: most components assembled in Vietnam are sourced from China, meaning the diversification reduces final assembly risk but not necessarily component supply risk.

LIMITS OF DIVERSIFICATION

Despite Apple's diversification efforts, China remains overwhelmingly important to its supply chain. In 2024, China still assembled approximately 80 percent of iPhones and a higher share of higher-margin products including MacBooks, whose complex manufacturing requires capabilities not yet replicated elsewhere at scale. Apple's supplier list includes approximately 150-200 companies, of which roughly 40 percent have facilities primarily in China. The specialized component ecosystem—precision optics, advanced connectors, display drivers, custom alloys—is concentrated in China and Japan, with Chinese suppliers deepening their penetration of Apple's bill of materials as domestic capabilities improve. True supply chain independence from China would require replicating entire industrial ecosystems in India, Vietnam, or other countries—a process measured in decades, not years.

CONCLUSION

Apple's diversification represents the most significant corporate-level restructuring of a major supply chain in the current geopolitical era. It validates the China Plus One strategy and demonstrates that large-scale manufacturing can be shifted to South and Southeast Asia with sufficient investment and government support. However, it also illustrates the limits: deep component and materials dependencies mean that diversification of final assembly is the beginning, not the end, of reducing Chinese supply chain concentration risk.
