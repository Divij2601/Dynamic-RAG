INDIA AS A GLOBAL MANUFACTURING HUB: PLI SCHEME AND SUPPLY CHAIN AMBITIONS

OVERVIEW

India has embarked on a major strategic effort to position itself as a global manufacturing hub, capitalizing on its large and young labor force, growing domestic market, and the global trend toward supply chain diversification away from China. The centerpiece of India's manufacturing policy is the Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme, launched in 2020-2021 across fourteen sectors, offering direct financial incentives to companies that grow production in India above baseline levels. Combined with improvements in infrastructure, regulatory reform, and a geopolitical environment that makes India an attractive alternative to China for Western companies, the PLI scheme has driven significant manufacturing investment. However, India faces substantial structural challenges that temper the pace of its manufacturing transformation.

THE PRODUCTION LINKED INCENTIVE SCHEME

The PLI scheme provides direct cash incentives—typically 4-6 percent of incremental sales over a base year—to manufacturers who grow production in India. Eligible sectors include mobile phones and electronic components, pharmaceuticals and medical devices, advanced chemistry cells for batteries, textiles, food processing, telecom equipment, white goods (air conditioners and LED lights), specialty steel, automobiles and auto components, solar photovoltaic modules, and others. Total budgetary outlay across all PLI schemes exceeds 1.97 trillion rupees ($24 billion) over five years. The scheme has attracted commitments from global companies including Samsung, Apple supply chain firms, Foxconn, Tata, Mahindra, and numerous pharmaceutical manufacturers.

MOBILE PHONES AND ELECTRONICS SUCCESS

India's most prominent PLI success has been in mobile phone manufacturing, where the scheme catalyzed Apple's expansion of iPhone production. India's smartphone exports grew from approximately $300 million in 2019-20 to over $15 billion in 2023-24, a dramatic transformation. Samsung, which already had a large manufacturing presence in India, expanded its Noida facility. Foxconn, Pegatron, and Wistron (now Tata) established or expanded iPhone assembly plants in Tamil Nadu and Karnataka. The government's goal is to make India one of the world's top three smartphone manufacturers by value within the decade. Success in mobile phones has provided a template that the government is trying to replicate in semiconductors, with a semiconductor PLI offering up to 50 percent capital subsidy for fab construction.

PHARMACEUTICAL MANUFACTURING STRENGTH

India is already a global pharmaceutical manufacturing powerhouse—the world's largest supplier of generic medicines by volume, producing approximately 20 percent of global supply and serving as "pharmacy to the world." The PLI for pharmaceuticals targets high-value products: biopharmaceuticals, complex generics, cell and gene therapies, and specialty chemicals. India's pharmaceutical sector employs over 3 million people and generates roughly $50 billion in annual exports. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted both India's importance and its vulnerability: the country's API dependence on China created risk, and domestic PLI investment in bulk drug parks and API manufacturing aims to address this. India's vaccine manufacturing capacity—demonstrated through Covishield production at the Serum Institute—has bolstered its global health supply chain credibility.

STRUCTURAL CHALLENGES TO MANUFACTURING SCALE

India's manufacturing ambitions face persistent structural obstacles. Infrastructure quality—power reliability, road and rail logistics, port efficiency—lags China significantly in many regions, increasing operating costs and reducing competitiveness. Land acquisition for large manufacturing facilities remains slow due to regulatory complexity and local political opposition. Labor laws, while partially reformed, add rigidity to workforce management. The education and skills pipeline, while improving, has not kept pace with the technical requirements of advanced manufacturing. Supply chain ecosystems—networks of component suppliers, tooling companies, and specialty service providers—are thin relative to China, meaning manufacturers must either accept higher logistics costs for imported components or invest in building the ecosystem from scratch. World Bank ease of doing business indicators have improved but India remains ranked below competitors in many operational areas.

CONCLUSION

India's PLI-driven manufacturing push represents genuine policy commitment and is yielding measurable results, particularly in mobile phones and pharmaceuticals. Whether India can replicate China's manufacturing scale over the next decade depends on whether infrastructure investment, skill development, and regulatory reform can keep pace with the ambition—and whether global supply chain restructuring continues to generate demand for alternative manufacturing locations.
