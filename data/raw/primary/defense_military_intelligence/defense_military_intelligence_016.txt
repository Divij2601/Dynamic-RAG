AUKUS: NUCLEAR-POWERED SUBMARINES AND TRILATERAL DEFENSE COOPERATION

OVERVIEW

AUKUS, the trilateral security partnership between Australia, the United Kingdom, and the United States, was announced in September 2021 and immediately became one of the most significant strategic developments in the Indo-Pacific in decades. The partnership's headline initiative — providing Australia with nuclear-powered submarines (SSNs) — represented an unprecedented transfer of sensitive nuclear propulsion technology and signaled a major commitment by Washington and London to Australian deterrence capability. The partnership also covers advanced conventional capabilities including AI, quantum technologies, cyber, undersea systems, and hypersonics.

THE SUBMARINE DEAL: PILLAR I

Australia's acquisition of nuclear-powered submarines under Pillar I was structured in three phases announced at the San Diego Summit in March 2023. Beginning around 2027, Australia will host rotational deployments of US Virginia-class SSNs and UK Astute-class submarines at HMAS Stirling near Perth, familiarizing Australian sailors and building maintenance expertise. From the early 2030s, Australia will purchase three to five Virginia-class submarines directly from the United States. Finally, from the late 2030s, a new class of SSN — the SSN-AUKUS — will be jointly designed and built, based on a UK hull design with US technology, and built in both the UK (for Royal Navy use) and Australia (for Royal Australian Navy use).

The nuclear propulsion is crucial — SSNs are faster, quieter, have unlimited endurance (no need to surface for air), and can operate covertly across vast Indo-Pacific distances far more effectively than Australia's existing conventionally powered Collins-class submarines.

STRATEGIC RATIONALE

AUKUS was explicitly designed in the context of China's rapidly expanding military capabilities and growing assertiveness in the Indo-Pacific. Australia's existing submarine capability was assessed as insufficient to provide credible deterrence in a more contested regional environment, and the 12 conventionally powered Attack-class submarines Australia had previously contracted from France would not have been ready until the 2040s.

The French government reacted furiously to the cancellation of its submarine contract — the "contract of the century" worth approximately $90 billion — leading to a temporary diplomatic crisis with Australia and the US and a French recall of ambassadors from both capitals. The diplomatic fallout was eventually managed, but the episode highlighted the degree to which Indo-Pacific strategic priorities had shifted the transatlantic relationship.

PILLAR II: ADVANCED CAPABILITIES

Pillar II covers a broad range of emerging technologies beyond submarines, including advanced cyber capabilities, AI, quantum technologies, additional undersea capabilities, hypersonic and counter-hypersonic systems, electronic warfare, and information sharing. Working groups established under Pillar II have made varying degrees of progress, with AI and autonomous systems cooperation progressing relatively quickly. The breadth of Pillar II reflects recognition that conventional deterrence increasingly depends on cross-domain technological integration.

CHALLENGES AND TIMELINE CONCERNS

Several challenges have emerged. The US Navy's own shipbuilding industrial base is under strain, raising questions about whether American yards can simultaneously build submarines for the US Navy and transfer vessels to Australia under the agreed timeline. Australian domestic industrial capacity requires significant development to eventually build SSN-AUKUS hulls. Nuclear workforce training and regulatory frameworks for handling nuclear-powered vessels in Australia needed to be established from near-scratch. Congressional support for selling Virginia-class submarines to Australia, given the US Navy's own procurement shortfalls, required careful political management. Despite these challenges, AUKUS retains strong bipartisan support in all three countries.
