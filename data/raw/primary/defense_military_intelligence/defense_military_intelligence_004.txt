RUSSIA MILITARY PERFORMANCE IN UKRAINE: AN ASSESSMENT

OVERVIEW

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 exposed profound weaknesses in the Russian Armed Forces that had been obscured by earlier successes in Syria and the 2014 annexation of Crimea. The initial offensive's catastrophic failure to capture Kyiv within days — the apparent operational assumption — triggered a fundamental reassessment of Russian military capabilities among Western analysts, policymakers, and rival militaries. Over three years of attritional conflict, Russia has adapted but remains far from the modernized high-precision force its doctrine prescribed.

INITIAL FAILURES AND STRUCTURAL WEAKNESSES

The opening campaign revealed systemic problems in Russian logistics, combined arms integration, and tactical leadership. Columns of vehicles stalled due to poor fuel management and inadequate maintenance, while unit commanders operated with outdated maps and unreliable communications. Electronic warfare systems that performed well in exercises frequently failed in contested electromagnetic environments. The much-feared Battalion Tactical Group (BTG) construct proved poorly suited to sustained offensive operations, lacking adequate infantry to hold ground after armor advances.

Intelligence failures were significant. Russian planners appear to have assumed a rapid collapse of Ukrainian state resistance and limited Western intervention. Neither assumption held. Ukrainian forces, trained and equipped to NATO standards since 2014, executed a coherent defense exploiting Russian overextension. The loss of the cruiser Moskva in April 2022 to Ukrainian Neptune anti-ship missiles demonstrated vulnerabilities in Russian naval air defense.

ADAPTATION AND EVOLUTION

Following the Kharkiv counteroffensive in September 2022 and Kherson withdrawal in November 2022, Russia shifted to a grinding attritional strategy better suited to its manpower reserves and industrial capacity. Mobilization of 300,000 reservists in September 2022 addressed immediate manpower shortfalls, though training quality was uneven. Fortified defensive lines along the Zaporizhzhia and Donetsk fronts proved effective in slowing Ukrainian counteroffensives in 2023.

Russia expanded its use of Iranian-supplied Shahed-136 loitering munitions for long-range strikes against Ukrainian infrastructure, partially compensating for depleted precision missile stocks. Glide bomb conversions of unguided FAB-500 and FAB-1500 bombs provided standoff strike capability. By 2024, Russia's industrial production had partially caught up with expenditure rates, giving it a firepower advantage at the tactical level in certain sectors.

PERSONNEL AND ORDER OF BATTLE

Russia's total losses in killed, wounded, captured, and missing have been assessed by Western intelligence as exceeding 300,000 by mid-2024, representing a catastrophic degradation of its pre-war professional force. Convict recruitment, Wagner Group (before its dissolution), and Chechen Kadyrov units supplemented depleted regular formations. The killing of Wagner chief Yevgeny Prigozhin following the June 2023 mutiny restructured Russia's private military sector, reintegrating fighters into regular Russian command structures.

STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS

Despite heavy losses and initial failures, Russia has demonstrated the ability to sustain large-scale conventional warfare longer than many predicted. This resilience reflects genuine industrial capacity, authoritarian mobilization potential, and strategic prioritization. For NATO planners, the key lesson is that Russia — even degraded — poses a serious conventional threat to European security, necessitating continued high investment in Alliance defense and the provision of modern systems to partner nations resisting Russian aggression.
