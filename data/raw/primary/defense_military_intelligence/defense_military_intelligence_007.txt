NUCLEAR ARSENAL MODERNIZATION: US, RUSSIA, AND CHINA

OVERVIEW

All three major nuclear powers are simultaneously modernizing their strategic nuclear forces for the first time since the Cold War, creating what analysts describe as a trilemma with no Cold War precedent. The New START treaty — the last remaining strategic arms control agreement between the US and Russia — was suspended by Russia in February 2023, further eroding the institutional guardrails that had constrained nuclear competition for decades. China's rapid nuclear expansion adds a third dimension to a problem previously managed as a bilateral relationship.

US NUCLEAR MODERNIZATION PROGRAMS

The United States is pursuing a comprehensive modernization of all three legs of its nuclear triad at an estimated cost of $1.7 trillion over 30 years. The Sentinel ICBM program is replacing the aging Minuteman III, which dates to the 1970s, though Sentinel has faced significant cost overruns and schedule delays that triggered a statutory Nunn-McCurdy review. The Columbia-class ballistic missile submarine is replacing the Ohio-class, with the first boat expected to enter service in the early 2030s. The B-21 Raider stealth bomber achieved initial operational capability in 2023, providing a modernized airborne leg. The B61-12 gravity bomb and W80-4 warhead for the LRSO cruise missile represent upgrades to tactical and theater nuclear capabilities.

RUSSIAN NUCLEAR POSTURE

Russia has maintained an active nuclear modernization program, deploying the RS-28 Sarmat heavy ICBM, Avangard hypersonic glide vehicle, and the Poseidon nuclear-powered underwater drone — though several of these systems remain in limited numbers. Russia's nuclear doctrine has attracted intense scrutiny during the Ukraine war, with Putin making repeated implicit and explicit references to nuclear employment, including lowering the stated threshold for use in the 2024 revision of Russia's nuclear doctrine.

Russia has the world's largest nuclear arsenal, estimated at approximately 5,580 warheads, of which 1,674 are deployed on strategic delivery systems. The suspension of New START inspections has reduced transparency and verification, increasing the potential for miscalculation.

CHINA'S RAPID EXPANSION

The most significant shift in the global nuclear landscape is China's accelerating buildup. Satellite imagery revealed new ICBM silo fields in Xinjiang, Gansu, and Inner Mongolia between 2021 and 2023, capable of hosting hundreds of additional warheads. The US Defense Department projects China could possess up to 1,000 nuclear warheads by 2030 and potentially 1,500 by 2035 — a dramatic shift from decades of a minimal deterrence posture of roughly 200-300 warheads.

China is also developing a complete triad: the JL-3 submarine-launched ballistic missile extends the range of the Jin-class SSBN force, while the H-20 stealth bomber — expected to enter service in the late 2020s — would add a modern airborne leg. China has declined to participate in arms control talks, citing the size asymmetry with the US and Russian arsenals.

STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS AND ARMS CONTROL

The convergence of three-way nuclear competition with degraded arms control frameworks represents a significant escalation risk. Extended deterrence commitments to NATO, Japan, South Korea, and Australia are under increasing pressure as adversary capabilities grow. Debates within the US defense community about whether the current arsenal remains sufficient for two-near-peer deterrence — deterring both Russia and China simultaneously — have intensified, with some voices calling for significant quantitative increases to US nuclear forces.
