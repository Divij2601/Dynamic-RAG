CUBA US EMBARGO: HISTORY, LEGAL FRAMEWORK, AND ONGOING IMPACT

ORIGINS AND EARLY HISTORY

The United States embargo against Cuba—known in Cuba as "el bloqueo" (the blockade) and formally as the Cuban Assets Control Regulations—represents the longest-standing and most comprehensive bilateral trade embargo in modern history. Its origins lie in the aftermath of Fidel Castro's revolution in 1959 and the subsequent nationalization of American business assets worth approximately one billion dollars in contemporary value. President Eisenhower imposed partial trade restrictions in October 1960. President Kennedy expanded the restrictions to a near-total trade embargo in February 1962, following Cuba's alignment with the Soviet Union and the failed Bay of Pigs invasion. The Cuban Missile Crisis of October 1962 cemented the hostile relationship. Over the following decades, the embargo became a foundational feature of US-Cuba policy, politically entrenched through the influence of Cuban-American communities in Florida.

LEGISLATIVE CODIFICATION: TORRICELLI AND HELMS-BURTON

Congressional action transformed the executive-order embargo into statute, making it far harder to unilaterally lift through presidential action. The Cuban Democracy Act of 1992 (Torricelli Act) prohibited subsidiaries of US companies operating in third countries from trading with Cuba and banned ships that had visited Cuba from entering US ports for 180 days. The Cuban Liberty and Democratic Solidarity Act of 1996 (Helms-Burton Act) went further, conditioning the lifting of the embargo on Cuba meeting specific democratic transition criteria and providing liability to US nationals (including naturalized Cuban-Americans) whose property was confiscated after the revolution. Title III of Helms-Burton, authorizing lawsuits against companies "trafficking" in confiscated Cuban property, was waived by every administration from Clinton through Obama but activated by the Trump administration in April 2019, triggering claims against dozens of foreign companies and generating significant diplomatic friction.

SCOPE OF THE EMBARGO

The Cuban Assets Control Regulations administered by OFAC prohibit virtually all transactions between US persons and Cuba. Americans cannot travel to Cuba for tourism (though twelve authorized categories of travel exist), cannot spend money there, cannot import Cuban goods including the celebrated cigars, cannot export most goods to Cuba, and cannot invest in Cuba. US dollar-denominated transactions involving Cuba are effectively prohibited from the global financial system given the extraterritorial reach of US sanctions over dollar clearing. Foreign companies operating in Cuba that use any US technology, software, or financial services also face restrictions. The embargo's extraterritorial provisions are particularly contentious with US allies: the EU, Canada, and other partners have enacted blocking statutes asserting that their companies are not required to comply with US secondary sanctions regarding Cuba, creating legal conflicts of jurisdiction.

DIPLOMATIC THAW AND REVERSAL

The Obama administration's December 2014 announcement of normalization with Cuba—negotiated secretly with Pope Francis serving as mediator—marked the most significant policy change since the embargo's imposition. The United States and Cuba restored diplomatic relations in July 2015, reopening embassies. Obama used executive authority to expand authorized travel categories, allow limited imports and exports, and restore direct flights and cruise ship service. However, Obama could not lift the core embargo unilaterally given its codification in Helms-Burton; that required congressional action that a divided Congress would not provide. The Trump administration reversed many of Obama's executive measures, reinstituting restrictions on travel, limiting remittances, and activating Helms-Burton Title III. The Biden administration restored some eased regulations but did not return to the full Obama-era normalization trajectory.

CURRENT IMPACT AND ONGOING DEBATE

Cuba's economy remains severely constrained by the combination of embargo, inefficient state-directed economic model, and periodic shocks including COVID-19's devastation of tourism. The island faced its worst economic crisis in decades by 2022-2024, with widespread electricity blackouts, acute shortages of food and medicine, and an emigration wave exceeding 300,000 Cubans arriving in the United States in fiscal year 2022 alone—the highest in history. Defenders of the embargo argue it maintains moral pressure on an authoritarian government and honors the claims of dispossessed Cuban Americans. Critics argue it has failed to achieve regime change in over sixty years, imposes disproportionate suffering on ordinary Cubans, and ironically provides the Castro-era government with a powerful explanation for economic failures. The UN General Assembly has voted annually since 1992 to condemn the embargo, with overwhelming majorities and only the United States and Israel consistently voting against.
