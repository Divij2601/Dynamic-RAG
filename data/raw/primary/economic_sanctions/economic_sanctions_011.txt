MYANMAR MILITARY JUNTA SANCTIONS FOLLOWING THE 2021 COUP

THE COUP AND INITIAL INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE

On February 1, 2021, Myanmar's military (Tatmadaw) overthrew the democratically elected government of Aung San Suu Kyi and the National League for Democracy, detaining Suu Kyi and President Win Myint in a pre-dawn coup that ended a decade of democratic transition. The military declared a year-long state of emergency, later extended indefinitely, and installed Senior General Min Aung Hlaing as head of the State Administration Council. The coup triggered massive civil disobedience movement protests, a nationwide general strike, and the formation of the People's Defence Force armed resistance. The international response included condemnation from Western governments, the UN General Assembly, and ASEAN, followed by targeted sanctions against military leadership and entities.

US SANCTIONS MEASURES

The Biden administration responded to the coup through a series of executive orders and Congressional legislation. Executive Order 14014, signed February 10, 2021, authorized sanctions against individuals responsible for or complicit in the coup and undermining democratic institutions. OFAC designated Min Aung Hlaing and other senior junta figures, including members of the State Administration Council, under a blocking sanctions framework that froze their US-held assets and prohibited transactions. The Burma Act provisions within the 2023 National Defense Authorization Act further authorized secondary sanctions and expanded the legal basis for Burma-related designations. The United States also froze approximately $1 billion in Burmese government assets held in the United States, denying the military junta access to these funds. Export controls restricted military equipment sales.

EU AND UK MEASURES

The European Union and United Kingdom implemented parallel sanctions packages, coordinating with the United States. EU Council Decisions imposed asset freezes and travel bans on military leaders, members of the State Administration Council, and entities connected to the Tatmadaw. Crucially, EU sanctions targeted Myanmar Economic Holdings Limited (MEHL) and Myanmar Economic Corporation (MEC), the two military-owned conglomerates that control vast swaths of Myanmar's economy including mining, banking, transport, and telecommunications. The designation of these conglomerates aimed to constrain the military's economic power base. The EU also suspended preferential trade arrangements under its Everything But Arms initiative's investment chapters with Myanmar's garment sector, though the humanitarian implications of disrupting employment in the civilian-run garment industry complicated this decision.

SANCTIONS EFFECTIVENESS CHALLENGES

Myanmar sanctions faced fundamental structural limitations that reduced their impact. The military's business empire is deeply embedded in the domestic economy rather than dependent on Western financial system access. Myanmar's currency, the kyat, is not internationally traded. The country's primary economic relationships are with China, Thailand, India, and ASEAN partners that did not implement comparable sanctions. China—Myanmar's largest trading partner, investor, and arms supplier—explicitly rejected the sanctions framework and continued economic engagement, including pipeline infrastructure connecting Myanmar's Bay of Bengal ports to China's Yunnan province. Gas revenues from the Yadana pipeline to Thailand continued to flow, providing the junta with significant hard currency through Total and Chevron-linked infrastructure (both companies ultimately divested).

HUMANITARIAN CRISIS AND ONGOING CONFLICT

Myanmar descended into civil war as resistance forces, including ethnic armed organizations and the newly formed People's Defence Force, fought the military across multiple fronts. By 2024, the Tatmadaw had suffered significant territorial losses to the resistance, particularly in Shan, Chin, and Rakhine states. Sanctions contributed marginally to military constraints by limiting access to aviation fuel (particularly relevant given the military's use of airstrikes against civilian areas) and certain weapons components, though Russia and China remained willing arms suppliers. The humanitarian situation deteriorated severely: internally displaced persons exceeded 2 million, the banking system was disrupted by civilian resistance boycotts of military-affiliated banks, and economic output contracted sharply. Western sanctions, while symbolically important and constraining for some elite spending, were insufficient to decisively alter the military's calculus absent broader international coordination including from China.
