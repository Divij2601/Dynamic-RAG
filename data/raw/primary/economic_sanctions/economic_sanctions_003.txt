SWIFT BAN ON RUSSIA: FINANCIAL SYSTEM IMPACT AND ADAPTATION

WHAT IS SWIFT AND WHY IT MATTERS

The Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication, universally known as SWIFT, is the messaging network that underpins international financial communication among over 11,000 financial institutions in more than 200 countries. When a bank in Frankfurt sends a wire transfer to a correspondent bank in Singapore, SWIFT carries the standardized message containing payment instructions. SWIFT does not itself move money—it moves information—but without that information channel, international financial transactions become effectively impossible. The network processed over 40 million messages daily by 2022. Exclusion from SWIFT does not technically prevent a country from conducting international trade, but it forces reliance on far slower, costlier, and less reliable bilateral communication channels, dramatically increasing transaction friction.

RUSSIA'S PARTIAL EXCLUSION FROM SWIFT

On February 26, 2022, the European Commission, the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, France, Germany, and Italy announced that selected Russian banks would be removed from SWIFT. The exclusion was deliberately partial: initially affecting seven banks including VTB, Bank Rossiya, and Otkritie, but notably excluding Sberbank and Gazprombank, which processed the bulk of European payments for Russian energy exports. The carve-out reflected the EU's calculation that energy payments needed to continue to prevent an immediate supply crisis. Over subsequent months, additional Russian banks were removed, and Belarus's Belagroprombank was included. By mid-2022, approximately two-thirds of Russian banking assets by value were excluded from SWIFT, but the energy-critical banks retained access until the energy payment rationale weakened.

IMMEDIATE MARKET IMPACT

The SWIFT announcement combined with Central Bank asset freezes produced immediate turbulence. The Russian ruble lost approximately 30 percent of its value in the days following the announcement before Russia's Central Bank imposed capital controls and dramatically raised interest rates to 20 percent. The Moscow Exchange halted trading for several days. Russian corporations with dollar-denominated debt faced immediate difficulties executing interest payments, triggering technical defaults. Trade finance for Russian imports collapsed as foreign banks refused to issue letters of credit. Russian imports of consumer goods, industrial components, and pharmaceuticals fell sharply in the first months, creating supply shortages particularly acute in automotive manufacturing, which relies on global just-in-time supply chains.

RUSSIAN ADAPTATION AND WORKAROUNDS

Russia's adaptation to the SWIFT exclusion proceeded through multiple channels. The Central Bank's System for Transfer of Financial Messages (SPFS), developed after the 2014 sanctions threat, became more widely used domestically and expanded bilateral connectivity with Chinese banks using CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System). Direct correspondent banking arrangements were established with banks in Turkey, UAE, India, and China that maintained SWIFT access, allowing Russia to route international payments through these intermediaries. The adaptation was imperfect and costly: transaction times lengthened, fees increased, and currency conversion became more complex as the ruble was not freely traded in major markets. Nonetheless, Russia maintained sufficient financial connectivity to sustain trade flows, particularly with Asian partners.

LESSONS FOR GLOBAL FINANCIAL ARCHITECTURE

Russia's SWIFT exclusion accelerated existing conversations about reducing dependence on Western-controlled financial infrastructure. China doubled down on CIPS development, expanding its cross-border payment system. Several BRICS members engaged in bilateral local currency settlement arrangements to reduce dollar and euro exposure. The episode demonstrated both the power and the limits of financial infrastructure as a sanctions tool: powerful against countries deeply integrated in Western financial networks, less decisive against countries with alternative connectivity or large commodity export revenues that create reverse dependencies. The partial SWIFT exclusion also revealed the tension between maximizing sanctions pressure and maintaining European energy security, a tension that will shape future use of financial exclusion as a coercive instrument.
