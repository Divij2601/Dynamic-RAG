VENEZUELA OIL SANCTIONS AND THE MADURO REGIME

BACKGROUND AND INITIAL SANCTIONS

Venezuela's descent into economic crisis and authoritarian consolidation under Nicolas Maduro triggered a progressively intensifying US sanctions campaign beginning in 2015 and escalating sharply in 2019. The initial measures, taken under President Obama's executive order of March 2015, targeted specific Venezuelan officials accused of human rights abuses and corruption, establishing a targeted sanctions framework. These early measures had limited economic impact given their narrow scope. The political context—disputed elections, the creation of a constituent assembly that bypassed the opposition-controlled National Assembly, and the recognition by over fifty countries of opposition leader Juan Guaido as interim president—provided the political justification for much broader economic measures that followed under President Trump.

COMPREHENSIVE OIL SECTOR SANCTIONS 2019

On January 28, 2019, the Trump administration designated Petroleos de Venezuela (PDVSA), the state oil company, as a Specially Designated National, prohibiting US persons from conducting most transactions with the company and its subsidiaries. This was an extraordinary measure targeting a company that at its peak produced over 3 million barrels per day and remained the government's primary revenue source. PDVSA's US subsidiary, CITGO Petroleum—owner of three US Gulf Coast refineries and an extensive pipeline network—was protected by specific licenses allowing its continued operation for a defined period, intended to prevent immediate disruption to US refinery operations and preserve assets that might eventually be returned to a democratic Venezuelan government. The oil sector sanctions prohibited US persons from purchasing Venezuelan crude, providing vessels to transport it, or facilitating transactions with PDVSA.

ECONOMIC IMPACT ON VENEZUELA

The oil sector sanctions accelerated a collapse that had already begun due to mismanagement, falling oil prices, and Maduro's economic policies. Venezuelan oil production had fallen from over 3 million barrels per day in 1999 to approximately 1.4 million by 2018 even before comprehensive sanctions. By 2020, production had collapsed to around 400,000 barrels per day, partly due to sanctions-related inability to obtain diluents, technical services, and spare parts, and partly due to infrastructure degradation from chronic underinvestment. GDP contracted by approximately 75 percent over the Maduro period, producing Latin America's worst economic crisis in modern history, driving an exodus of over 7 million Venezuelans. Hyperinflation rendered the bolivar nearly worthless. Humanitarian conditions—food insecurity, medicine shortages, collapsing health infrastructure—became severe.

CIRCUMVENTION THROUGH IRAN AND ALTERNATIVE MARKETS

Venezuela found alternative markets primarily through Cuba, China, and Russia for residual oil exports. Iran provided a particularly notable form of solidarity: Iranian tankers delivered gasoline and diluents to Venezuela in violation of US sanctions on both countries, while Venezuelan crude was loaded onto Iranian vessels for sale in Asian markets. The US Treasury and State Departments designated these vessels and the companies involved, but enforcement against Iranian-flagged vessels was limited. China continued purchasing Venezuelan crude at significant discounts, providing PDVSA with revenue that sustained the regime's survival. Russia's Rosneft continued operating in Venezuela until secondary sanctions pressure caused it to restructure its Venezuelan interests through shell companies. By 2022-2024, Venezuela had partially recovered oil production toward one million barrels per day as international enforcement relaxed amid global energy price pressures.

BIDEN ADMINISTRATION ADJUSTMENTS

The Biden administration's approach to Venezuela sanctions reflected the tension between maintaining pressure for democratic change and addressing energy supply concerns and humanitarian needs. In November 2022, OFAC issued a license authorizing Chevron to resume limited oil extraction operations in Venezuela, in the context of political negotiations between the Maduro government and the opposition. This license was expanded in 2023, allowing Chevron to resume more significant operations as Venezuela-US dialogue continued. The administration also issued humanitarian exemptions and suspended some individual sanctions as part of political incentive structures. However, comprehensive sanctions against PDVSA and senior Venezuelan officials remained in place, with full relief conditional on credible democratization commitments that Maduro showed no genuine intention of fulfilling.
