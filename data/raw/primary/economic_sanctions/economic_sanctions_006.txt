IRAN OIL SANCTIONS: ENFORCEMENT, CIRCUMVENTION, AND SHADOW MARKETS

THE ARCHITECTURE OF OIL SANCTIONS ENFORCEMENT

Iran's oil sanctions represent one of the most extensively studied cases of commodity sanctions in modern history—and one of the most instructive regarding both the power and the limitations of economic coercion. The US framework for Iran oil sanctions relies on secondary sanctions: threatening foreign banks, companies, and countries with US market access denial if they facilitate Iranian oil transactions. Under the National Defense Authorization Act provisions codified in US law, any foreign financial institution that knowingly conducts significant transactions with the Central Bank of Iran for oil purchases risks being cut off from correspondent banking relationships with US institutions. Since nearly all significant international commercial banks maintain dollar clearing accounts at US banks, this threat creates powerful compliance incentives extending far beyond US jurisdiction.

CHINA AS THE DOMINANT SANCTIONS CIRCUMVENTION CHANNEL

The most significant channel through which Iran evaded oil sanctions was China, which declined to fully comply with US extraterritorial sanctions claims, particularly under administrations asserting US secondary sanctions jurisdiction over sovereign Chinese commercial decisions. Chinese independent refiners—known as "teapot refineries"—emerged as the primary purchasers of sanctioned Iranian crude during the Trump maximum pressure period. These smaller refineries, concentrated in Shandong province, were less exposed to US financial system connections than China's major state oil companies, making secondary sanctions threats less credible. Iranian oil was often loaded onto tankers that disabled their AIS transponders, transferred cargo at sea to other vessels, falsified documentation to disguise origin as Malaysian or Iraqi crude, and delivered to Chinese ports under opaque ownership chains. Estimates of Iranian crude reaching China during the maximum pressure period ranged from 500,000 to over 1 million barrels per day at the peak of enforcement relaxation.

GHOST FLEET AND MARITIME DECEPTION TACTICS

Iran developed a sophisticated maritime deception toolkit to obscure the movement of its crude. The Iranian National Tanker Company and affiliated entities operated a "ghost fleet" of dozens of tankers that used multiple identifiers, switched flags between Iran-friendly registries, conducted ship-to-ship transfers in international waters off Malaysia, Singapore, and Oman, and used falsified bills of lading declaring non-Iranian origin. Satellite tracking companies specializing in dark vessel monitoring—including TankerTrackers and Windward—provided detailed documentation of these practices to governments and compliance departments. Port state control authorities in Asia were generally less rigorous in investigating vessel histories than European counterparts. The use of front companies registered in third countries including Hong Kong, UAE, and Turkey allowed Iranian entities to maintain nominally arms-length relationships with end purchasers.

FINANCIAL FLOWS AND CURRENCY ARRANGEMENTS

Payment for sanctioned Iranian oil presented its own circumvention challenges since dollar-denominated transactions through Western banks were blocked. Iran developed several workarounds: accepting payment in Chinese yuan, held in accounts at Chinese banks insulated from US pressure; agreeing to payment through barter arrangements exchanging oil for goods; accumulating credits in third-country accounts in currencies including yuan, rupee, and lira; and using cryptocurrency for some transactions. The resulting fund flows were difficult to use for hard currency purchases, creating a phenomenon where Iran had accumulated revenues it could not readily convert or repatriate. During Biden-era negotiations, the unfreezing of approximately $6 billion in Iranian assets held in South Korean accounts—released in exchange for American hostages in 2023—became politically contentious in the United States.

ENFORCEMENT ACTIONS AND THEIR LIMITATIONS

OFAC and the Department of Justice pursued enforcement actions against maritime service providers, front companies, and financial institutions that facilitated Iranian oil sanctions evasion, with settlements totaling hundreds of millions of dollars from major international banks. However, enforcement against Chinese entities remained limited, constrained by diplomatic and economic considerations. The gap between formal sanctions coverage and practical enforcement effectiveness created a two-tier system: European and major Asian companies faced credible enforcement risk and largely complied, while Chinese teapot refineries operated with greater impunity. This enforcement gap undermined the overall efficacy of the maximum pressure strategy and provided Iran with sufficient revenue to maintain its nuclear program and regional proxy activities despite broader economic pain.
