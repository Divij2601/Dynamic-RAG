BELARUS LUKASHENKO REGIME SANCTIONS: RIGGED ELECTION TO RYANAIR INCIDENT

BACKGROUND AND INITIAL SANCTIONS

Alexander Lukashenko's Belarus was subject to intermittent Western sanctions from the early 2000s related to election fraud, media repression, and human rights violations. However, the sanctions landscape transformed fundamentally following the August 9, 2020 presidential election, which Lukashenko claimed to have won with 80 percent of the vote. Independent observers and the overwhelming evidence of popular mobilization—with hundreds of thousands of Belarusians taking to the streets in the largest protests in the country's history—indicated massive fraud. The EU and United States refused to recognize the electoral results, and the EU declined to recognize Lukashenko as the legitimate president. Subsequent violent crackdown on protesters, with thousands arrested and documented cases of torture in detention, prompted the first substantial coordinated Western sanctions since the 2006-2010 period.

EU SANCTIONS AND ECONOMIC MEASURES

The EU imposed four packages of sanctions on Belarus through 2021, targeting hundreds of individuals including Lukashenko himself (whom the EU designated in June 2021 after initially excluding him from the first package in an attempt to preserve dialogue space). The EU measures included asset freezes and travel bans for those responsible for the election fraud, violent repression of protesters, and judicial persecution of opposition figures. In August 2021, the EU banned Belarusian potash imports—a targeted economic sanction with significant bite given Belarus being among the world's largest potash exporters, with the sector representing a major hard currency earner. US and UK sanctions were coordinated to include the state-owned Belaruskali (the potash company) and related financial entities. Lithuania's closure of its port at Klaipeda to Belarusian potash transit amplified the economic pressure on this critical export.

THE RYANAIR INCIDENT AND ESCALATION

A dramatic escalation occurred on May 23, 2021, when Belarusian authorities scrambled a MiG-29 fighter jet and issued a bomb threat to force Ryanair Flight FR4978, traveling from Athens to Vilnius, to land in Minsk. The apparent purpose was to arrest dissident journalist Roman Protasevich and his Russian girlfriend Sofia Sapega, who were passengers on the flight. The incident—widely condemned as state-sponsored air piracy—triggered unprecedented aviation sanctions: the EU banned Belarusian carriers from EU airspace and airports, and EU carriers were instructed to avoid Belarusian airspace. Most international carriers followed. The incident also provided additional political impetus for more comprehensive economic sanctions, including the potash measures. The Council of the International Civil Aviation Organization condemned the incident, further isolating Minsk in international forums.

IMPACT OF ECONOMIC SANCTIONS

The combination of potash sanctions, financial restrictions, and political isolation imposed significant costs on Belarus. Potash revenues—representing approximately 15-20 percent of export earnings—were significantly curtailed as major purchasers in Europe and North America shifted to Canadian, Moroccan, and Russian suppliers. The exclusion from EU airspace increased costs for Belarusian exports and aviation. However, Russia's support substantially cushioned the impact: Russian energy subsidies (below-market natural gas prices and oil supply arrangements), Russian financial support, and the Russian market as an alternative destination for diverted Belarusian exports provided an economic lifeline. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022, launched in part from Belarusian territory, deepened the Belarus-Russia alignment and triggered additional comprehensive sanctions.

COMPREHENSIVE POST-UKRAINE INVASION MEASURES

Following Belarus's role in facilitating Russia's invasion of Ukraine—providing territory for the initial northern thrust toward Kyiv and supporting Russian military operations—the EU, US, UK, and Canada implemented more comprehensive measures. The EU's fifth and subsequent sanctions packages on Russia included expanded Belarus measures. Additional Belarusian banks were removed from SWIFT. Comprehensive sectoral sanctions targeted defense industries, financial institutions, and transport sectors. By 2024, Belarus had become almost entirely economically dependent on Russia, with the ruble increasingly pegged to the Russian ruble, trade overwhelmingly oriented toward Russia, and political sovereignty substantially constrained by the security dependence created by the post-2020 crisis. Western sanctions had not achieved the regime change they implicitly sought but had deepened Russia-Belarus integration, with ambiguous long-term strategic implications.
