ISRAEL-GAZA CEASEFIRE NEGOTIATIONS 2024-2025

The prolonged effort to achieve a ceasefire in Gaza became one of the defining diplomatic challenges of 2024 and into 2025. Qatar, Egypt, and the United States served as primary mediators in exhausting negotiations between Israel and Hamas, with repeated breakthroughs and collapses punctuating the conflict that continued to kill thousands of Palestinians and hold hostages in captivity.

NEGOTIATION FRAMEWORK AND KEY ISSUES

Ceasefire talks centered on several interlocked issues: the release of Israeli hostages held by Hamas in exchange for Palestinian prisoners held by Israel; the extent and permanence of any Israeli military withdrawal from Gaza; the post-war governance of the strip; the humanitarian aid access to be permitted; and Hamas's political future. These issues proved extraordinarily difficult to resolve because they reflected fundamentally incompatible goals: Israel sought the destruction of Hamas as a governing entity and the return of all hostages, while Hamas sought a permanent ceasefire that would guarantee its political survival and Israeli withdrawal.

THE MAY 2024 CEASEFIRE PROPOSAL

In May 2024, US President Joe Biden publicly outlined a three-phase ceasefire proposal, presenting it as an Israeli offer. The plan envisioned an initial six-week ceasefire with hostage-prisoner exchanges, followed by negotiations toward a permanent end to hostilities, and eventually a major reconstruction plan for Gaza. Hamas expressed conditional interest but sought guarantees of a permanent ceasefire upfront. Israel's far-right coalition partners threatened to collapse the government if Netanyahu agreed to terms that left Hamas in power. The proposal did not produce a deal despite intensive diplomatic pressure.

RAFAH OPERATION AND US TENSIONS

In May 2024, Israel launched a ground offensive into Rafah in southern Gaza, where over a million displaced Palestinians had sheltered. The Biden administration had warned against a major Rafah operation without a credible civilian protection plan and briefly paused a shipment of 2,000-pound bombs. The Rafah operation proceeded, destroying much of the city and prompting accusations from international critics that Israel had disregarded humanitarian obligations. The episode caused visible strain in US-Israel relations.

NOVEMBER 2024 PARTIAL AGREEMENT

Following months of intermittent negotiations, a partial agreement in late 2024 allowed the release of additional hostages in phased exchanges. However, a comprehensive deal remained elusive. The death toll in Gaza surpassed 44,000 by the end of 2024, according to Gaza health authorities, with the UN and international agencies warning of famine conditions.

JANUARY 2025 CEASEFIRE AGREEMENT

A significant breakthrough came in January 2025, when a ceasefire agreement was announced days before Donald Trump's inauguration. The deal, mediated by Qatar, Egypt, and the United States, provided for a phased release of hostages in exchange for Palestinian prisoners, a pause in fighting, and increased humanitarian access. The first phase began with the release of several hostages and facilitated increased aid deliveries. The agreement was fragile, however — questions about whether subsequent phases would be implemented, including Israeli withdrawal commitments, remained unresolved.

TRUMP ADMINISTRATION AND POST-CEASEFIRE DYNAMICS

The incoming Trump administration played a role in pressuring both sides toward the January deal. Trump's special envoy Steve Witkoff participated actively in final-stage negotiations. However, Trump subsequently proposed controversial plans for the United States to "take over" Gaza and relocate Palestinians to Egypt and Jordan — suggestions that were immediately rejected by regional governments and international legal experts as inconsistent with international law.

ONGOING DIPLOMATIC COMPLEXITY

By mid-2025, the Gaza conflict remained unresolved in its fundamental political dimensions. Hamas had not been destroyed as a governing force despite 18 months of intensive military operations. The question of Gaza's post-war governance — who would administer the territory and reconstruct it — remained deeply contested, with the Palestinian Authority, Arab states, and international organizations all involved in competing frameworks while humanitarian conditions remained catastrophic.
