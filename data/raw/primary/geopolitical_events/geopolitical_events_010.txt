ARMENIA-AZERBAIJAN 2023 KARABAKH OFFENSIVE

On September 19, 2023, Azerbaijan launched a lightning military offensive in Nagorno-Karabakh, completing its takeover of the disputed territory in less than 24 hours and ending three decades of Armenian control. The operation resulted in the total dissolution of the self-declared Republic of Artsakh and the mass exodus of almost the entire ethnic Armenian population of the enclave.

CONTEXT: POST-2020 PRESSURES

Following the 2020 war, the remaining Armenian-controlled portion of Nagorno-Karabakh — approximately 4,400 square kilometers — was connected to Armenia only via the Lachin corridor, under Russian peacekeeping observation. Azerbaijan gradually tightened its grip. In December 2022, Azerbaijani activists blocked the Lachin corridor, creating a de facto siege of Nagorno-Karabakh for months. The blockade severely restricted food, medicine, and fuel supplies, creating humanitarian shortages. Armenia was unable to resupply the enclave, and Russia's peacekeepers — their attention diverted by Ukraine — proved unwilling or unable to enforce free movement through the corridor.

By September 2023, the enclave's approximately 120,000 ethnic Armenian inhabitants had endured nine months of siege conditions. Fuel was scarce, food supplies were strained, and the local authorities' capacity to sustain the population was eroding.

THE SEPTEMBER 19 OFFENSIVE

On September 19, 2023, Azerbaijan announced a "local anti-terrorist operation" and launched simultaneous attacks along the entire contact line in Nagorno-Karabakh. Azerbaijani forces, equipped with drones and modern artillery, breached defensive positions that Armenian Karabakh forces — undersupplied, under-strength, and isolated — could not hold. Within hours, the defensive lines collapsed across multiple sectors.

Russian peacekeepers, deployed under the 2020 agreement, did not intervene militarily. Their mandate did not compel them to fight, and Russia's military and political attention was absorbed by Ukraine. Armenia's government under Pashinyan had already signaled it would not militarily intervene in defense of Karabakh, recognizing the territory as de jure Azerbaijani under international law as part of its diplomatic repositioning.

CEASEFIRE AND SURRENDER

On September 20, 2023, the Karabakh Armenian authorities announced a ceasefire and agreed to complete dissolution of Karabakh Armenian institutions and disarmament. The Republic of Artsakh formally ceased to exist on January 1, 2024. The speed of the collapse — less than 24 hours of fighting — reflected the impossible military situation facing defenders who had been besieged for nine months and faced an overwhelmingly better-equipped opponent.

THE MASS EXODUS

The ceasefire triggered one of the most rapid mass displacements in modern history. Within days of the offensive, over 100,000 of Nagorno-Karabakh's approximately 120,000 ethnic Armenians fled through the Lachin corridor to Armenia. Only a small number chose to remain under Azerbaijani administration. Families loaded their possessions onto vehicles in chaotic convoys, in many cases burning their homes before departing. Scenes of the exodus drew international attention and condemnation.

The displacement represented the near-total ethnic cleansing of a population that had inhabited the region for centuries, though Armenian and Azerbaijani narratives about the causes differed sharply.

GEOPOLITICAL IMPLICATIONS

The 2023 offensive completed Azerbaijan's post-Soviet territorial consolidation. For Armenia, it represented national trauma, raising existential questions about security arrangements. Relations with Russia — viewed by many Armenians as having failed to protect Karabakh Armenians — deteriorated sharply. Armenia began reorienting diplomatically toward Europe and the United States, suspending participation in Russia-led security structures and pursuing EU relationship-building. Armenia and Azerbaijan simultaneously continued direct peace negotiations toward a formal peace treaty, though unresolved border demarcation and domestic political sensitivities in both countries complicated progress.
