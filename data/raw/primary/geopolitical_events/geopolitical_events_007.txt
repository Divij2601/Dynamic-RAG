MYANMAR MILITARY COUP 2021 AND CIVIL WAR

Myanmar's military coup of February 1, 2021 ended the country's brief democratic experiment and plunged the nation into civil war. The Tatmadaw's seizure of power reversed years of tentative political opening and triggered a popular resistance that evolved into a full-scale armed conflict involving dozens of ethnic armed organizations and a newly formed People's Defence Force.

THE COUP AND IMMEDIATE AFTERMATH

In the early hours of February 1, 2021, the Myanmar military (Tatmadaw) arrested State Counsellor Aung San Suu Kyi, President Win Myint, and other elected officials from the National League for Democracy (NLD) government. The military declared a year-long state of emergency and announced Senior General Min Aung Hlaing as the effective head of state, citing unsubstantiated claims of electoral fraud in the November 2020 elections in which the NLD had won a landslide victory.

The coup provoked massive popular protests in Yangon, Mandalay, and cities across the country, forming the Civil Disobedience Movement (CDM). Millions participated in strikes and street demonstrations. Security forces responded with increasing violence, killing protesters, and by late 2021 the death toll from security force crackdowns had surpassed 1,000, with thousands arrested.

EMERGENCE OF THE PEOPLE'S DEFENCE FORCE

As peaceful protest was met with lethal force, resistance militarized. The National Unity Government (NUG), an alternative governing body formed by ousted lawmakers and activists operating largely in exile and underground, established the People's Defence Force (PDF) in May 2021 as a military wing. The PDF rapidly expanded through volunteer recruitment, drawing in youth who had participated in street protests.

The PDF began armed resistance operations against military installations, police stations, and informant networks. The Tatmadaw responded with brutal counterinsurgency tactics including airstrikes on villages, arson of civilian settlements, and mass arrests. Entire villages in Sagaing Region, Chin State, and other areas were burned to the ground.

ETHNIC ARMED ORGANIZATIONS AND THE THREE BROTHERHOOD ALLIANCE

Myanmar's political landscape included numerous ethnic armed organizations (EAOs) that had fought the Tatmadaw for decades over autonomy and ethnic rights. These groups' responses to the coup varied. Some coordinated with the NUG and PDF; others maintained ceasefires or pursued their own objectives. A pivotal development came in October 2023 when Operation 1027 was launched by the Three Brotherhood Alliance — comprising the Arakan Army, Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army, and Ta'ang National Liberation Army — in northern Shan State. The operation captured significant territory, including strategic towns along the China border, inflicting major defeats on the Tatmadaw and signaling a major shift in the conflict's momentum.

THE CONFLICT'S TRAJECTORY 2022-2025

By 2024, the Tatmadaw had lost control of substantial territory. The Arakan Army effectively controlled much of Rakhine State. The Brotherhood Alliance controlled large areas of northern Shan State. Chin National Front forces operated in Chin State. The PDF and aligned forces had degraded Tatmadaw capacity in Sagaing and Magway regions. The junta controlled the main cities but faced sustained challenges throughout the country.

Humanitarian conditions deteriorated dramatically. The UN estimated over 2 million internally displaced persons by 2024. The economy collapsed, with poverty rates soaring. Healthcare and education systems were severely disrupted by the civil disobedience movement and conflict.

INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE

ASEAN adopted a "Five-Point Consensus" in April 2021 calling for dialogue, humanitarian aid, and a special envoy, but implementation was essentially nonexistent as the junta blocked access. Western nations imposed sanctions on military leaders and suspended aid. China maintained engagement with the junta for economic and strategic reasons while also maintaining relations with some EAOs. The Myanmar conflict remained one of the most complex and underreported crises globally, with limited prospects for resolution.
