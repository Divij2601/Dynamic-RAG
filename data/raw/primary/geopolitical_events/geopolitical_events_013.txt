SAHEL REGION COUPS 2021-2023: MALI, BURKINA FASO, NIGER

Between 2021 and 2023, a succession of military coups swept through the Sahel region of West Africa, overthrowing civilian governments in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger. These coups reflected deep governance failures, security deterioration, and growing popular resentment against France and Western partners, dramatically reshaping the region's geopolitical alignment.

MALI: TWO COUPS IN FOURTEEN MONTHS

Mali experienced two coups in rapid succession. In August 2020, military officers removed President Ibrahim Boubacar Keïta, citing his government's failure to address the jihadist insurgency in northern and central Mali and corruption complaints. International condemnation was followed by negotiations producing a transitional civilian-military government. Before elections could be organized, a second coup occurred in May 2021, when military officers led by Colonel Assimi Goïta seized power more firmly, positioning the junta in firmer control of the transition.

Mali's junta expelled French forces — which had been deployed since 2013 under Operation Barkhane — and invited the Russian Wagner Group to deploy instead. The Wagner Group arrived in late 2021, bringing a different operational approach that included less concern for civilian casualties. Wagner operations in Mali, conducted alongside Malian Armed Forces, were linked to atrocities against civilians in incidents including the Moura massacre of March 2022, in which several hundred civilians were killed.

BURKINA FASO: DOUBLE COUP 2022

Burkina Faso, already struggling with an expanding jihadist insurgency linked to al-Qaeda (JNIM) and Islamic State (GSIM), experienced two coups in 2022. Lieutenant Colonel Paul-Henri Damiba overthrew President Roch Marc Christian Kaboré in January 2022, citing his government's failure to address the security situation. Eight months later, in September 2022, Damiba himself was overthrown by Captain Ibrahim Traoré, who argued that the first coup had not improved security sufficiently. Traoré, in his late 30s, became one of Africa's youngest heads of state.

Burkina Faso under Traoré pursued a similar trajectory to Mali, demanding the departure of French forces and turning toward Wagner Group and Russian security assistance. The security situation in Burkina Faso deteriorated further despite this shift, with large portions of the country's north and east effectively under jihadist control by 2024.

NIGER: COUP JULY 2023

Niger's coup on July 26, 2023 was the most geopolitically significant of the sequence. Presidential Guard commander General Abdourahamane Tchiani overthrew President Mohamed Bazoum, who had been regarded as one of West Africa's more stable democratic leaders and a key Western security partner. The coup triggered an intense international response: ECOWAS threatened military intervention to restore Bazoum, setting a deadline that ultimately was not enforced. The United States, which had a major drone base in Niger critical for Sahel counterterrorism operations, suspended some aid while avoiding the use of the word "coup" to preserve legal flexibility.

The Niger junta expelled French forces and, within months, requested the departure of US forces as well. Niger had been considered the last remaining major Western military partner in the Sahel core.

ALLIANCE OF SAHEL STATES

Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger formalized their alignment by establishing the Alliance of Sahel States (AES) in September 2023, positioning themselves as a bloc resistant to French and Western influence. They subsequently announced their withdrawal from ECOWAS in January 2024, signaling a fundamental rupture with the West African regional organization.

GEOPOLITICAL SIGNIFICANCE

The Sahel coup wave represented a dramatic realignment of a strategically important region. France's decade of military engagement had failed to stabilize the region and generated significant local resentment. Russia's influence expanded at France's expense. The US counterterrorism infrastructure in the region was severely disrupted. The broader implications for jihadist insurgencies, migration pressures on Europe, and regional stability made the Sahel's trajectory one of the most consequential geopolitical developments in Africa in the 2020s.
