SUDAN CIVIL WAR: RSF VS SAF 2023

In April 2023, Sudan erupted into catastrophic civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), transforming a power struggle between two military factions into one of the world's worst humanitarian disasters. The conflict has displaced millions, destroyed cities, and threatens Sudan's survival as a functioning state.

ORIGINS OF THE CONFLICT

The RSF traces its origins to the Janjaweed militias that committed atrocities during the Darfur conflict of the early 2000s. Sudan's long-ruling president Omar al-Bashir formally incorporated these forces as the Rapid Support Forces in 2013, using them as a counterinsurgency tool and personal power base. RSF commander General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo, known as Hemeti, built the RSF into a parallel military structure with significant economic resources derived from gold mining and commercial interests.

Following the 2019 revolution that ousted Bashir, and the 2021 military coup that derailed Sudan's democratic transition, power was shared uncomfortably between SAF General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan and RSF General Hemeti. The two agreed in principle to integrate the RSF into the SAF under a framework for civilian-led transition, but the timeline and terms of integration became a fatal point of contention. Disputes over command, the RSF's economic interests, and who would control a future security apparatus brought tensions to a breaking point.

OUTBREAK OF FIGHTING APRIL 2023

On April 15, 2023, fighting broke out in Khartoum and across Sudan simultaneously, suggesting pre-planned coordination by both sides. RSF forces seized key positions in the capital, including the airport and state broadcaster, while SAF conducted airstrikes against RSF positions. The speed and scale of initial fighting indicated both sides had anticipated confrontation.

The battle for Khartoum became one of the most destructive urban conflicts in Africa's recent history. Entire residential neighborhoods were destroyed. Hospitals were attacked and occupied by armed forces. Foreign nationals were evacuated in dramatic operations as the capital descended into chaos. Looting was widespread, with reports of RSF fighters systematically stripping homes and businesses.

HUMANITARIAN CATASTROPHE

The scale of displacement surpassed almost every contemporary conflict globally. By 2024, over 8 million people had been internally displaced — the largest internal displacement crisis in the world — and approximately 2 million had fled to neighboring countries including Egypt, Chad, and South Sudan. Chad, itself a fragile state, struggled to absorb the influx.

Famine conditions emerged in parts of Sudan by 2024, with the UN Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) declaring famine in several areas including parts of Darfur. Aid delivery was systematically obstructed by both parties, though the RSF was particularly accused of blocking humanitarian access and looting aid convoys.

RSF ATROCITIES IN DARFUR

The RSF's advance into Darfur revived memories of the 2003-2004 genocide. The city of El Geneina in West Darfur saw mass killings of members of the Masalit ethnic group, with credible reports of hundreds or thousands killed and survivors describing systematic targeting of ethnic African communities. International human rights organizations and US officials characterized the killings as ethnic cleansing and potential genocide, echoing the patterns of the Janjaweed atrocities 20 years earlier.

REGIONAL AND INTERNATIONAL DIMENSIONS

The conflict drew in regional actors. The United Arab Emirates was credibly accused of providing weapons and support to the RSF through arms shipments via Chad, which the UAE denied. Egypt supported the SAF, reflecting its longstanding military ties. The African Union, Arab League, and various international mediators launched successive peace initiatives — the Jeddah Process mediated by Saudi Arabia and the US — that produced ceasefires on paper but not in practice.

The conflict represented a failure of the international community to prevent or halt a rapidly deteriorating situation, with diplomatic efforts persistently lagging behind the pace of violence and displacement.
