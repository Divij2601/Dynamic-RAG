ARMENIA-AZERBAIJAN NAGORNO-KARABAKH: THE 2020 WAR

The 44-day war between Armenia and Azerbaijan in autumn 2020 over Nagorno-Karabakh ended three decades of frozen conflict and fundamentally altered the South Caucasus's geopolitical landscape. Azerbaijan's overwhelming military victory, facilitated by Turkish support and advanced drone technology, reshaped regional power dynamics and set the stage for the final resolution of 2023.

HISTORICAL BACKGROUND

Nagorno-Karabakh — known to Armenians as Artsakh — is a mountainous enclave internationally recognized as part of Azerbaijan but populated predominantly by ethnic Armenians. When the Soviet Union began dissolving in the late 1980s, the enclave's Armenian population sought unification with Armenia. Violence erupted in the final Soviet years, escalating into full-scale war from 1991 to 1994. The 1994 ceasefire left Armenia and Armenian Karabakh forces in control of the enclave and seven surrounding Azerbaijani districts — approximately 20% of internationally recognized Azerbaijani territory. Approximately 600,000 Azerbaijani civilians had been displaced from these areas during the war.

The conflict remained frozen under a ceasefire monitored by the OSCE Minsk Group co-chaired by France, Russia, and the United States. Multiple rounds of diplomacy failed to produce a final settlement acceptable to both sides.

OUTBREAK OF THE 2020 WAR

On September 27, 2020, Azerbaijan launched a large-scale military offensive along the Nagorno-Karabakh contact line. The operation was meticulously planned and leveraged multiple technological and military advantages Azerbaijan had built through substantial oil revenue-funded defense spending. Most significantly, Azerbaijan deployed large numbers of Turkish-supplied Bayraktar TB2 combat drones, which proved devastating against Armenian armor, artillery, and air defense systems. Israeli-supplied loitering munitions (Harop and Orbiter) were also extensively used.

Armenian forces, equipped with Soviet-era equipment and lacking effective counter-drone capabilities, were unable to prevent systematic destruction of their military assets. Azerbaijani forces advanced rapidly in the lowland areas south of the enclave, capturing the strategically vital city of Shusha (Shushi to Armenians) in November 2020 after intense fighting.

TURKISH INVOLVEMENT

Turkey provided decisive support to Azerbaijan including Bayraktar TB2 drones, military advisers, and the facilitation of Syrian mercenary fighters. Turkey's open backing of Azerbaijan marked a significant regional intervention and demonstrated the transformative impact of armed drone technology in modern conflict. Turkish-Azerbaijani cooperation proved far more effective than Russia's ambiguous stance toward Armenia, its nominal ally.

THE NOVEMBER 9 CEASEFIRE

After 44 days of fighting and devastating Armenian losses, Armenian Prime Minister Nikol Pashinyan signed a Russian-brokered ceasefire on November 9, 2020, accepting terms widely characterized as capitulation. Armenia agreed to withdraw from all seven surrounding districts, return Shusha to Azerbaijan, and accept a Russian peacekeeping force in the remaining Armenian-controlled portion of Nagorno-Karabakh — a corridor connecting it to Armenia. Azerbaijan recovered approximately 12,000 square kilometers of territory.

The agreement triggered massive protests in Armenia, with demonstrators storming the parliament and demanding Pashinyan's resignation. The political crisis in Armenia proved profound and lasting.

HUMANITARIAN AND GEOPOLITICAL CONSEQUENCES

Approximately 90,000 ethnic Armenians were displaced from areas that returned to Azerbaijani control. The war exposed Armenia's military weakness and the unreliability of the Russian security umbrella it had depended on. Russia's CSTO collective security alliance had not intervened to defend Armenia, shaking faith in Moscow as a security guarantor. Azerbaijan emerged significantly strengthened, with President Ilham Aliyev's domestic standing bolstered. The 2020 war established new facts on the ground that would define the final resolution of the Karabakh conflict in 2023.
