SUDAN: THE FALL OF EL FASHER 2025

El Fasher, capital of North Darfur and the last major city in the Darfur region not under RSF control, became the focal point of the Sudan conflict's most critical phase in 2024 and 2025. The city's fate represented not only a military outcome but the potential culmination of what international observers described as genocidal violence against Darfur's non-Arab communities.

EL FASHER AS HUMANITARIAN REFUGE

By 2024, El Fasher had swelled to harbor an estimated 1.5 to 2 million internally displaced persons who had fled RSF advances across Darfur. The city became the last refuge for survivors of RSF attacks in West, South, and Central Darfur, including Masalit, Fur, and Zaghawa communities subjected to systematic targeting. The city's Zamzam camp, one of the largest displacement camps in Africa, held hundreds of thousands in desperate conditions.

The SAF and allied armed movements — particularly the Sudan Liberation Army (SLA) factions — defended El Fasher against RSF encirclement. The RSF controlled the surrounding area and had besieged the city, severely restricting food, medicine, and fuel supplies.

THE SIEGE AND INTENSIFYING ATTACKS 2024

Throughout 2024, the RSF intensified its assault on El Fasher. Artillery bombardments struck residential areas, markets, and hospitals. The UN and humanitarian organizations documented repeated attacks on civilian infrastructure and aid convoys. International human rights groups and UN officials warned explicitly that an RSF assault on El Fasher could produce mass atrocities on a catastrophic scale against the civilian and displaced population sheltering there.

The UN Security Council passed resolutions calling for protection of civilians in El Fasher, but effective enforcement mechanisms were absent. Western governments issued statements of concern while the RSF's external backers — primarily the UAE — faced accusations of enabling the siege through continued arms supplies.

FALL OF EL FASHER 2025

By early 2025, RSF forces had tightened their encirclement and launched intensifying ground assaults. SAF defensive lines came under sustained pressure, and the logistical position of defenders deteriorated as the siege prevented resupply. In the first half of 2025, RSF forces breached key defensive positions around El Fasher and advanced into the city in the most significant military operation in the Darfur theater since the conflict began.

The fall of El Fasher to RSF forces precipitated what humanitarian organizations described as one of the worst atrocity situations of the 21st century. Witnesses and survivors described systematic killings targeting members of Darfur's non-Arab communities, sexual violence used as a weapon of war, and widespread looting. Displacement swelled further as those who could flee the city did so toward the border regions of Chad and Libya.

INTERNATIONAL RESPONSE AND ACCOUNTABILITY EFFORTS

The fall of El Fasher prompted renewed international outrage but limited effective action. The United States formally designated RSF attacks in Sudan as genocide in 2024, imposing targeted sanctions on RSF commander Hemeti and other leaders. The International Criminal Court, which had existing arrest warrants related to Darfur atrocities, opened new investigations into crimes committed in the 2023-2025 conflict.

The African Union and various mediators continued diplomatic engagement, but peace talks remained deadlocked as the RSF sought international legitimacy while continuing military operations. The SAF, having lost Khartoum and now El Fasher, faced questions about its own capacity to maintain coherent governance.

HUMANITARIAN LEGACY

The Sudan conflict by mid-2025 had produced one of the most severe humanitarian crises globally, with over 10 million displaced, famine conditions across large areas, and documented mass atrocities. El Fasher's fall symbolized the failure of international mechanisms designed to prevent genocide from materializing in practice, echoing painful parallels to Rwanda in 1994 and Srebrenica in 1995.
