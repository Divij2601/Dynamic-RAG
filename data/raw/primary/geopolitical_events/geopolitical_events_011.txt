ETHIOPIA TIGRAY CIVIL WAR 2020-2022

The civil war in Ethiopia's Tigray region from November 2020 to November 2022 was among the deadliest conflicts globally in that period, producing a humanitarian catastrophe including famine, mass atrocities, and millions of displaced persons. The conflict pitted the Ethiopian federal government and its allies against the Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF), once the dominant force in Ethiopian national politics.

BACKGROUND: TPLF AND FEDERAL TENSIONS

The TPLF had been the dominant party in the ruling coalition, the Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front (EPRDF), from 1991 to 2018, effectively controlling Ethiopian politics for nearly three decades. When Abiy Ahmed became prime minister in 2018, implementing significant political reforms and winning the Nobel Peace Prize in 2019 for his peace agreement with Eritrea, he dismantled the EPRDF structure and the TPLF lost national influence. Tensions between the federal government and Tigray's regional authorities escalated over power-sharing, the postponement of elections, and control over military assets.

OUTBREAK OF WAR NOVEMBER 2020

On November 4, 2020, Abiy Ahmed announced military operations against the TPLF following what he characterized as an attack by TPLF forces on a federal military base in Tigray. Federal forces advanced into Tigray with support from the Amhara regional militia (Fano) and, crucially, from Eritrea — Ethiopia's former enemy with whom Abiy had negotiated peace but whose government harbored deep hostility to the TPLF.

Federal and Eritrean forces captured Tigray's capital Mekelle in late November 2020. However, the TPLF retreated into the mountains and regrouped.

HUMANITARIAN CRISIS AND ATROCITIES

From the outset, credible reports emerged of atrocities against Tigrayan civilians. The Axum massacre, in which Ethiopian federal and Eritrean forces killed hundreds of civilians in the ancient city of Axum in November 2020, was among the most documented incidents. Eritrean forces were implicated in widespread killings, looting, and sexual violence across Tigray. Ethiopian federal forces and Amhara militias were also implicated in human rights violations. Access for journalists and aid workers was severely restricted, making full documentation difficult.

A joint investigation by the UN Human Rights Office and Ethiopian Human Rights Commission found that all parties to the conflict had committed violations amounting to war crimes, with Eritrean forces bearing particularly heavy responsibility.

TPLF COUNTEROFFENSIVE AND EXPANSION

In June 2021, TPLF forces launched a stunning counteroffensive, retaking Mekelle and routing federal forces from most of Tigray. They then advanced into the neighboring Amhara and Afar regions, threatening to march toward Addis Ababa by late 2021. The Ethiopian government declared a state of emergency and called for citizen mobilization. A complex coalition of forces — including Fano militias, Oromo regional forces, and others — eventually halted the TPLF advance. The war's geographic expansion brought enormous suffering to populations in Amhara and Afar regions.

THE PRETORIA AGREEMENT AND END OF FIGHTING

On November 2, 2022, a Cessation of Hostilities Agreement was signed in Pretoria under African Union mediation. The agreement provided for the cessation of fighting, disarmament of TPLF fighters, and the restoration of federal administration in Tigray. Weapons collection began, and the federal government began restoring services to Tigray that had been suspended during the war. The agreement held, representing a significant diplomatic achievement for the African Union.

HUMANITARIAN LEGACY

The war's human cost was staggering. Estimates of conflict deaths ranged from several hundred thousand to as many as 600,000, with famine and disease accounting for many deaths beyond direct combat. The Tigray region's infrastructure was severely damaged. Over two million people remained displaced within Tigray and neighboring regions. The full accounting of atrocities and the path to reconciliation and justice remained contested and unresolved.
