IRAN-ISRAEL JUNE 2025 TWELVE-DAY WAR

In June 2025, Iran and Israel engaged in direct large-scale military conflict — the most significant direct confrontation between the two countries in history. The twelve-day war, marked by Israeli strikes on Iranian nuclear and military facilities and Iranian ballistic missile and drone responses, fundamentally altered the Middle East's security architecture and the trajectory of Iran's nuclear program.

ESCALATION PATHWAY

The June 2025 conflict was the product of escalating direct exchanges between Iran and Israel that began in April 2024. Iran's direct missile and drone attack on Israeli territory in April 2024 — in response to an Israeli strike on Iran's consulate in Damascus — was unprecedented. Israel responded with a limited strike on Iranian territory. These exchanges broke the decades-long pattern in which Iran and Israel conducted their conflict primarily through proxy forces and covert operations.

By 2025, several factors raised tensions to a breaking point: continued Israeli military operations in Gaza and Lebanon, which Iran framed as existential to its regional alliance network; progress in Iran's nuclear program with enrichment levels approaching weapons-grade; and Israeli intelligence assessments suggesting Iran was closer than ever to nuclear breakout capacity.

ISRAELI STRIKES ON IRANIAN NUCLEAR FACILITIES

Israeli forces launched coordinated strikes beginning in early June 2025, targeting Iran's nuclear infrastructure including the Natanz enrichment facility, the Fordow underground facility, and nuclear research sites at Isfahan. The strikes employed combinations of ballistic missiles, air-launched munitions, and reportedly special operations elements. The targeting of Fordow — a deeply buried facility — required specialized munitions including US-supplied bunker-busting bombs, raising questions about US knowledge and implicit authorization of the operation.

The strikes caused significant damage to Iran's centrifuge infrastructure and set back the enrichment program by an estimated one to several years, according to various assessments. However, the extent of damage to deeply buried elements remained uncertain.

IRANIAN RESPONSE

Iran responded with the largest ballistic missile and drone barrage it had ever launched, targeting Israeli cities, military bases, and the Dimona nuclear reactor in the Negev. Israel's multi-layered air defense system — including Iron Dome, David's Sling, and Arrow — intercepted the majority of incoming projectiles with substantial assistance from the United States, United Kingdom, France, and Jordan, which had provided air defense support to Israel since the 2024 exchanges. Nevertheless, some missiles reached Israeli territory, causing casualties and infrastructure damage.

Iran also directed Hezbollah to launch heavy rocket and missile attacks from Lebanon and activated remaining proxy network assets in Iraq and Syria. Israel responded with airstrikes across these threat networks.

US ROLE AND REGIONAL DYNAMICS

The United States' position was complex. Washington had urged restraint while privately providing intelligence and air defense support to Israel. The Biden successor administration faced intense pressure both to support Israel and to prevent broader regional war. The strikes caused a temporary spike in global oil prices as markets assessed the risk of Strait of Hormuz disruption.

AFTERMATH AND CEASEFIRE

A ceasefire brokered through Omani and Qatari mediation, with US diplomatic involvement, ended the twelve-day exchange. The post-conflict situation left several critical questions unresolved: the extent of damage to Iran's nuclear program, Iran's nuclear intentions going forward, the status of the broader Iran-Israel proxy conflict, and whether the ceasefire would hold. The conflict demonstrated Israel's willingness to strike directly at Iran while also revealing the limitations of airstrikes in permanently eliminating nuclear programs — a lesson echoing the Israeli strike on Iraq's Osirak reactor in 1981.
