RUSSIA-UKRAINE WAR 2022: OUTBREAK AND CAUSES

The full-scale Russian invasion of Ukraine that began on February 24, 2022 marked the largest conventional military conflict in Europe since World War II. Understanding this war requires examining decades of geopolitical friction, post-Soviet identity struggles, and competing security architectures.

HISTORICAL BACKGROUND AND ROOT CAUSES

The seeds of the 2022 war stretch back to the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991. Ukraine emerged as an independent state holding the third-largest nuclear arsenal in the world, which it surrendered under the 1994 Budapest Memorandum in exchange for security assurances from Russia, the United States, and the United Kingdom. Russia's subsequent disregard for those assurances would prove consequential.

Throughout the 1990s and 2000s, Ukraine navigated between Western integration and Russian influence. The Orange Revolution of 2004 and the Euromaidan protests of 2013-2014 reflected Ukrainian society's growing orientation toward Europe and democratic governance. Russia viewed NATO's eastward expansion — which admitted Poland, Hungary, and the Czech Republic in 1999, and the Baltic states in 2004 — as an existential encroachment on its sphere of influence.

THE 2014 TURNING POINT

The 2014 crisis was the immediate precursor to the 2022 war. After President Viktor Yanukovych rejected an EU association agreement under Russian pressure and fled to Russia following mass protests, Russia annexed Crimea in March 2014 and fomented separatist insurgencies in the Donbas region of eastern Ukraine. The Minsk Agreements of 2014 and 2015 attempted to freeze the conflict but were never fully implemented. By 2022, an estimated 14,000 people had been killed in the low-intensity Donbas war.

IMMEDIATE TRIGGERS AND RUSSIAN JUSTIFICATIONS

In late 2021, Russia began massing over 100,000 troops near Ukraine's borders while issuing ultimatums demanding NATO halt further expansion and withdraw forces from Eastern Europe. Western governments assessed these demands as non-starters designed to provide a pretext for invasion. Russia framed its February 24 assault as a "special military operation" aimed at the "denazification" and "demilitarization" of Ukraine — claims widely rejected as propaganda by Western governments and international observers.

Putin's worldview, articulated in a July 2021 essay, denied Ukrainian national identity as distinct from Russian, framing Ukrainians and Russians as "one people." This ideological foundation provided the philosophical underpinning for the invasion.

INITIAL INVASION AND WESTERN RESPONSE

Russian forces launched a multi-axis assault targeting Kyiv from Belarus, Kherson and Zaporizhzhia from the south, and Kharkiv from the northeast. The anticipated rapid collapse of Ukrainian resistance did not materialize. Ukrainian forces, bolstered by years of NATO training and equipped with Western weapons including Javelin anti-tank missiles, inflicted severe losses on advancing Russian columns.

Within days, Western nations imposed sweeping sanctions, including removing Russia from the SWIFT banking system and freezing Russian central bank assets worth approximately $300 billion. The United States, European Union, and United Kingdom began providing substantial military aid to Ukraine.

GEOPOLITICAL SIGNIFICANCE

The invasion shattered the post-Cold War security order in Europe. NATO unity was revitalized after years of internal strain. Germany reversed its longstanding pacifist defense posture and pledged to increase defense spending above 2% of GDP. Finland and Sweden applied for NATO membership, fundamentally altering the security landscape of northern Europe. Russia's action accelerated European efforts to reduce dependence on Russian energy, reshaping global energy markets.

The war also exposed deep divisions between the Global North, which broadly condemned the invasion, and many Global South nations that sought to remain neutral or maintain economic ties with Russia, reflecting divergent perspectives on sovereignty, sanctions, and great power competition.
