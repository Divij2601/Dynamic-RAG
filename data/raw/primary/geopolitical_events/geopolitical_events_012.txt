SOMALIA: AL-SHABAAB CONFLICT

Somalia's conflict with Al-Shabaab, the Al-Qaeda-affiliated militant group that controls large swaths of the country's south and center, represents one of Africa's most entrenched insurgencies. Despite years of international intervention, Al-Shabaab has demonstrated remarkable resilience, maintaining territorial control, financing operations through taxation, and conducting devastating attacks in Somalia and neighboring countries.

ORIGINS AND RISE OF AL-SHABAAB

Al-Shabaab emerged from the remnants of the Islamic Courts Union (ICU), which briefly controlled much of southern Somalia in 2006 before being ousted by Ethiopian military intervention. The group's name means "The Youth" in Arabic. It formally affiliated with Al-Qaeda in 2012. Al-Shabaab established a strict Islamist administration in territories under its control, enforcing its interpretation of sharia law, banning music and many forms of entertainment, and providing rough-and-ready governance in areas where the Somali state had been absent for decades.

The group funds itself substantially through taxation — charging businesses, travelers, and communities in areas it controls, including near-road taxation in areas technically controlled by the government. Estimates suggest Al-Shabaab collects tens of millions of dollars annually, making it one of the wealthiest militant groups in Africa.

AFRICAN UNION MISSION AND FEDERAL GOVERNMENT

The African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM), established in 2007 and later rebranded the African Union Transition Mission in Somalia (ATMIS), has deployed tens of thousands of troops primarily from Uganda, Burundi, Ethiopia, Kenya, and Djibouti. These forces, supported by US counterterrorism assistance including drone strikes and special operations advisers, pushed Al-Shabaab out of Mogadishu in 2011 and major cities subsequently. However, Al-Shabaab adapted to conventional military pressure, ceding urban centers while maintaining rural control and conducting spectacular urban attacks.

ATTACKS IN SOMALIA AND THE REGION

Al-Shabaab has demonstrated the capacity for devastating attacks despite military pressure. The 2017 truck bombing in Mogadishu's Zoobe junction killed over 500 people, one of the deadliest terrorist attacks in history. The group has repeatedly targeted the Somali government, security forces, hotels, restaurants, and civilian gatherings in Mogadishu. It has also conducted attacks in Kenya, including the 2013 Westgate Mall siege in Nairobi (67 killed) and the 2015 Garissa University attack (148 killed), and in Uganda's capital Kampala (2010 World Cup bombings).

SOMALIA'S MILITARY OFFENSIVE 2022-2023

Following Hassan Sheikh Mohamud's election as president in May 2022, Somalia embarked on an ambitious military offensive against Al-Shabaab, incorporating clan militia forces (the Macawisley) alongside the Somali National Army. The offensive achieved significant initial success in 2022, liberating dozens of towns in Hiran and Galmudug regions. Clan communities that had previously submitted to Al-Shabaab rose up against the group, providing critical intelligence and ground forces.

However, the offensive stalled in 2023. Al-Shabaab launched devastating counterattacks, retaking some areas and demonstrating its resilience. Questions arose about the sustainability of clan mobilization and the capacity of the Somali National Army to hold recaptured territory and govern it effectively.

INTERNATIONAL WITHDRAWAL TIMELINE

The ATMIS mission faced donor pressure to reduce its troop presence on a timeline toward a full withdrawal by December 2024, to be replaced by a Somali National Army capable of independent operations. This timeline was considered optimistic by most military analysts given Al-Shabaab's demonstrated capabilities. The phased withdrawal risked creating security vacuums that Al-Shabaab could exploit.

HUMANITARIAN DIMENSIONS

Somalia's conflict is compounded by chronic food insecurity, recurrent drought, and displacement. Approximately three million Somalis remain internally displaced. Al-Shabaab blocks humanitarian aid to areas under its control when politically convenient, exacerbating food insecurity. Climate-driven droughts have repeatedly pushed Somalia toward famine conditions, creating a vicious cycle of humanitarian crisis and conflict.
