THE UNITED NATIONS: ORGANIZATION PROFILE

Overview

The United Nations is an intergovernmental organization founded in 1945 to maintain international peace and security, develop friendly relations among nations, promote social progress and human rights, and serve as a center for harmonizing the actions of nations. It is the largest and most prominent international organization in the world. As of the mid-2020s it has 193 member states. Its headquarters is located in New York City, with major offices in Geneva, Vienna, and Nairobi.

Founding and the UN Charter

The United Nations was established in the aftermath of World War II, succeeding the failed League of Nations. Representatives of fifty countries met at the San Francisco Conference in 1945 and drafted the United Nations Charter, which was signed on June 26, 1945, and entered into force on October 24, 1945. October 24 is observed annually as United Nations Day. The Charter is the founding treaty of the organization and sets out the rights and obligations of member states and the structure of the organization's principal organs.

Principal Organs

The United Nations has six principal organs. The General Assembly is the main deliberative body, in which all member states have equal representation and one vote. The Security Council is responsible for maintaining international peace and security and is the only body whose decisions are binding on member states. The Secretariat carries out the day-to-day work of the organization and is headed by the Secretary-General. The International Court of Justice, located in The Hague, is the principal judicial organ. The Economic and Social Council coordinates economic and social work. The Trusteeship Council, which oversaw the administration of trust territories, suspended operations in 1994.

The Security Council

The Security Council has fifteen members, of which five are permanent members: the United States, the United Kingdom, France, Russia, and China. These five permanent members each hold veto power, meaning any one of them can block a substantive resolution. The remaining ten members are elected by the General Assembly for two-year terms. The veto power of the permanent members reflects the balance of power at the end of World War II and is a frequent subject of reform debates, as it can paralyze the Council when the interests of the major powers conflict.

The Secretary-General

The Secretary-General is the chief administrative officer of the United Nations and acts as its public face and chief diplomat. The Secretary-General is appointed by the General Assembly on the recommendation of the Security Council, typically serving five-year renewable terms. The role combines administrative leadership with moral and diplomatic authority on the world stage.

Specialized Agencies and Programs

The United Nations system includes numerous specialized agencies, funds, and programs. These include the World Health Organization, which addresses global public health; the United Nations Children's Fund, known as UNICEF; the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization, known as UNESCO; the Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees; and the World Food Programme. The Sustainable Development Goals, adopted in 2015, set out seventeen global objectives to be achieved by 2030.

Peacekeeping

United Nations peacekeeping operations deploy personnel, often called blue helmets, to conflict zones to help maintain ceasefires, protect civilians, and support political processes. Peacekeeping missions are authorized by the Security Council and rely on troops and police contributed by member states.

Strengths and Limitations

The United Nations provides a universal forum for diplomacy and has contributed to humanitarian relief, public health, decolonization, and the development of international law. However, its effectiveness in preventing or ending conflict is often limited by the veto power of the permanent Security Council members and by its dependence on member states for funding and military force. Critics argue that its structure reflects an outdated post-war order, while supporters maintain that it remains an indispensable institution for global cooperation.
