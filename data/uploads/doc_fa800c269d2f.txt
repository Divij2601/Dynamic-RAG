KEY CONCEPTS IN GEOPOLITICS AND INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS

Overview

Geopolitics and international relations rely on a set of core concepts that explain how states interact, how power is distributed, and how order is maintained or contested in the international system. This document defines several of the most important of these concepts.

Sovereignty

Sovereignty is the principle that a state has supreme authority within its own territory and is free from external interference in its internal affairs. It is the foundational concept of the modern state system. The principle of sovereignty is often traced to the Peace of Westphalia of 1648, which is why the modern international order is sometimes called the Westphalian system. Sovereignty implies both internal supremacy over a territory and population and external recognition by other states as an equal and independent actor.

Balance of Power

The balance of power is a concept and a strategy in which states seek to prevent any single state from becoming dominant enough to threaten the independence of others. States may form alliances or build up their own capabilities to counterbalance a rising or aggressive power. The balance of power was a central organizing principle of European diplomacy, particularly in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, and remains relevant in analyzing great-power competition today.

Hard Power and Soft Power

Power in international relations is often divided into hard power and soft power. Hard power refers to the use of military force and economic coercion to influence the behavior of other actors. Soft power, a term coined by the political scientist Joseph Nye, refers to the ability to attract and persuade others through the appeal of a country's culture, values, and policies, rather than through coercion. Many states combine the two in what is sometimes called smart power.

Deterrence

Deterrence is a strategy intended to discourage an adversary from taking an unwanted action by threatening unacceptable consequences. Nuclear deterrence was central to the Cold War, embodied in the doctrine of mutually assured destruction, under which the certainty of devastating retaliation deterred either superpower from launching a nuclear attack. Deterrence depends on the credibility of the threat and the capability to carry it out.

Spheres of Influence

A sphere of influence is a region over which a powerful state exercises significant political, economic, or military influence, even if it does not formally control the territory. Great powers have historically sought to establish spheres of influence in neighboring or strategically important regions. The concept remains relevant in analyzing the behavior of major powers toward their neighbors.

Multipolarity, Bipolarity, and Unipolarity

The structure of the international system is often described by the number of dominant powers. A bipolar system has two dominant powers, as during the Cold War, when the United States and the Soviet Union led opposing blocs. A unipolar system has a single dominant power, a description often applied to the United States after the Cold War. A multipolar system has several major powers of comparable strength. Many analysts argue that the world is shifting toward multipolarity, with the rise of China, the resurgence of Russia, and the growing weight of states such as India.

Hegemony

Hegemony refers to the dominance of one state over others, whether regionally or globally. A hegemon is a state powerful enough to shape the rules and institutions of the international system. Debates about the rise and decline of hegemonic powers are central to the study of long-term shifts in global politics.

Diplomacy and International Institutions

Diplomacy is the practice of conducting negotiations and managing relationships between states. International institutions, such as the United Nations, are organizations through which states cooperate, set rules, and resolve disputes. These concepts together describe how order and cooperation are pursued in an international system that lacks a single overarching government, a condition known as anarchy.
