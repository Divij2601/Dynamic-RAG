BRICS: ORGANIZATION AND GEOPOLITICAL PROFILE

Overview

BRICS is an intergovernmental organization and economic grouping of major emerging economies that positions itself as a counterweight to Western-dominated institutions. The name is an acronym originally formed from the initials of its founding members. The group holds annual summits and coordinates on economic, financial, and political matters, advocating for a more multipolar world order and reform of global governance institutions.

Origins and Naming

The term BRIC was coined in 2001 by the economist Jim O'Neill of Goldman Sachs to describe four large, fast-growing emerging economies: Brazil, Russia, India, and China. These countries began holding formal summits in 2009. In 2010, South Africa was invited to join, and the group adopted the name BRICS, with the S representing South Africa. For more than a decade the group consisted of these five members.

Expansion

BRICS underwent a major expansion in the mid-2020s. Several new members joined, including Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, and Indonesia. Indonesia became a full member in January 2025, the first Southeast Asian nation to join. By 2026 the group had eleven full members: Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, and Indonesia. In addition, BRICS established a category of partner countries to accommodate the large number of states seeking closer ties, with around ten partner countries including Nigeria. Dozens of additional countries have expressed interest in joining. In 2026, Russian officials indicated that the group would not rush further expansion in order to consolidate its recent enlargement.

Institutions

The most significant institution created by BRICS is the New Development Bank, established in 2015 and headquartered in Shanghai, China. The bank finances infrastructure and sustainable development projects in member states and other developing countries, offering an alternative to Western-led institutions such as the World Bank. BRICS members have also discussed mechanisms to reduce dependence on the U.S. dollar in international trade, a process sometimes referred to as de-dollarization, though progress has been gradual.

Goals and Significance

BRICS represents a substantial share of the world's population, economic output, and territory. Its members share a broad desire to increase the influence of the Global South and emerging economies in global affairs and to reduce the dominance of the United States and Western institutions. However, the group is internally diverse, encompassing democracies and authoritarian states, energy exporters and importers, and countries with competing interests, such as India and China, which have an unresolved border dispute. This diversity can limit the group's cohesion and ability to act collectively.

Contemporary Role

In the 2020s, BRICS has become a prominent symbol of the shift toward a multipolar world. Its expansion has increased its weight in global affairs, particularly in debates over the structure of the international financial system, trade, and the representation of developing countries in global governance. While it is unlikely to function as a unified bloc comparable to Western alliances, it serves as an important forum for coordination among major non-Western powers.
