THE REPUBLIC OF INDIA: COUNTRY AND GEOPOLITICAL PROFILE

Overview

The Republic of India is a country in South Asia and, since around 2023, the most populous country in the world, with a population exceeding 1.4 billion people. It is the world's largest democracy and one of the fastest-growing major economies. Its capital is New Delhi, and its largest city is Mumbai, the financial center. As of 2026, the prime minister is Narendra Modi, who has held office since 2014 and consistently ranks among the most popular elected leaders in the world.

Government and Political System

India is a federal parliamentary democratic republic. The president is the head of state in a largely ceremonial role, while the prime minister is the head of government and holds real executive power. The Parliament of India is bicameral, consisting of the Lok Sabha, the lower house, and the Rajya Sabha, the upper house. India's constitution, adopted in 1950, is the longest written national constitution in the world.

The dominant political force in recent years has been the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), led by Narendra Modi, which espouses a Hindu nationalist platform. The Indian National Congress is the principal opposition party. India holds general elections of enormous scale, with hundreds of millions of voters participating.

Economy

India has a rapidly growing, services-oriented economy and is among the largest economies in the world by both nominal gross domestic product and purchasing power parity. It is a global leader in information technology services, pharmaceuticals, and software, with major technology hubs in Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Pune. India has a large and youthful population, which many see as a demographic dividend, though the country faces challenges in job creation, infrastructure, and inequality. The government has pursued initiatives such as Make in India to expand manufacturing and Digital India to expand technology access.

Foreign Policy and Military

India pursues a foreign policy of strategic autonomy, historically rooted in the principle of non-alignment. It maintains a large military and is a nuclear-armed state, having conducted nuclear tests in 1974 and 1998. India balances relationships among major powers: it participates in the Quadrilateral Security Dialogue (the Quad) alongside the United States, Japan, and Australia, while also maintaining longstanding defense ties with Russia and membership in the BRICS group.

India has a tense and militarized relationship with neighboring Pakistan, with whom it has fought several wars, largely over the disputed region of Kashmir. It also has an unresolved border dispute with China, which led to deadly clashes in the Himalayas in 2020. In the mid-2020s, an exchange of fire between India and Pakistan was described as their worst in decades.

Historical Background

The Indian subcontinent is home to one of the world's oldest civilizations, the Indus Valley Civilization. Over millennia it saw a succession of empires and the development of major world religions including Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, and Sikhism. From the eighteenth century, much of the subcontinent came under British colonial rule, administered through the British Raj. Led by figures such as Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru, the Indian independence movement secured independence on August 15, 1947, accompanied by the traumatic partition of the subcontinent into India and Pakistan, which displaced millions and caused widespread violence.

Contemporary Role

In the 2020s, India is widely regarded as a rising power and a pivotal swing state in global geopolitics. Its large economy, strategic location, democratic system, and independent foreign policy give it growing influence. India seeks a permanent seat on the United Nations Security Council and positions itself as a leading voice of the Global South.
