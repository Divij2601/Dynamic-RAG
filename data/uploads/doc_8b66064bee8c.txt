THE RUSSIAN FEDERATION: COUNTRY AND GEOPOLITICAL PROFILE

Overview

The Russian Federation is the largest country in the world by land area, spanning eleven time zones across Eastern Europe and northern Asia. It has a population of roughly 144 million people. Its capital and largest city is Moscow. Russia is a nuclear-armed great power and a permanent member of the United Nations Security Council. As of 2026, the president is Vladimir Putin, who has dominated Russian politics since 1999.

Government and Political System

Russia is formally a federal semi-presidential republic, but in practice it functions as a highly centralized state with power concentrated in the presidency. Vladimir Putin has served as either president or prime minister continuously since 1999. Constitutional changes approved in 2020 reset presidential term limits, potentially allowing Putin to remain in office until 2036. Political opposition, independent media, and civil society have been heavily suppressed.

Economy

Russia's economy is heavily dependent on the export of natural resources, particularly oil and natural gas, of which it is one of the world's largest producers. Energy exports are both a major source of revenue and a tool of geopolitical leverage, especially over Europe. Since its full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022, Russia has been subject to extensive Western economic sanctions, which have reoriented its trade toward China, India, and other non-Western partners. Despite sanctions, the Russian economy has proven more resilient than many analysts expected, partly due to high energy prices and a wartime industrial footing.

Foreign Policy and Military

Russia possesses the largest nuclear arsenal in the world, roughly equal to that of the United States. Its conventional military has been heavily engaged in the war in Ukraine since February 2022, the largest armed conflict in Europe since World War II. Russia seeks to reassert influence over the territory of the former Soviet Union and to weaken the NATO alliance and Western unity.

Russia is a leading member of the BRICS group and has deepened its strategic partnership with China, often described as a relationship without limits. It maintains influence in the Middle East, Africa, and Central Asia, and has used both military force and private military contractors to project power abroad.

Historical Background

Russia emerged from the medieval state of Kievan Rus and grew into the Tsardom of Russia and then the Russian Empire, one of the largest empires in history. The Russian Revolution of 1917 overthrew the monarchy and brought the Bolsheviks to power, leading to the creation of the Soviet Union in 1922. The Soviet Union was a superpower that led the Eastern bloc during the Cold War and was the principal rival of the United States. The Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, and the Russian Federation emerged as its successor state. The 1990s were a period of economic turmoil and declining influence, which Putin's rule reversed through reassertion of state power and a more confrontational posture toward the West.

The War in Ukraine

Russia annexed Crimea from Ukraine in 2014 and launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. By the mid-2020s the war had become a grinding conflict of attrition, with extensive casualties on both sides, intense fighting in eastern Ukraine, and repeated Russian strikes on Ukrainian cities and energy infrastructure. The war has reshaped European security, prompted NATO enlargement to include Finland and Sweden, and deepened Russia's isolation from the West while strengthening its ties to China.

Contemporary Role

In the 2020s, Russia positions itself as a leader of a multipolar world order opposed to Western dominance. Despite a smaller economy than many of its rivals, its nuclear arsenal, energy resources, permanent Security Council seat, and willingness to use military force make it a central actor in global geopolitics.
