THE PEOPLE'S REPUBLIC OF CHINA: COUNTRY AND GEOPOLITICAL PROFILE

Overview

The People's Republic of China is a one-party socialist state in East Asia and the most populous country in the world for most of modern history, with a population of roughly 1.4 billion people. It possesses the world's second-largest economy by nominal gross domestic product and the largest by purchasing power parity. Its capital is Beijing, and its largest city and financial center is Shanghai. As of 2026, the paramount leader is Xi Jinping, who serves as General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party, President of the People's Republic, and Chairman of the Central Military Commission.

Government and Political System

China is governed as a unitary one-party state under the Chinese Communist Party (CCP), which has held power continuously since the founding of the People's Republic in 1949. The National People's Congress is the highest organ of state power in form, but real political authority is concentrated in the Communist Party and its top body, the Politburo Standing Committee. The General Secretary of the Party is the most powerful position in the country.

Xi Jinping, who came to power in 2012, has centralized authority to a degree not seen since Mao Zedong. In 2018, constitutional term limits on the presidency were removed, allowing him to remain in office indefinitely.

Economy

China's economy was transformed by market reforms beginning in 1978 under Deng Xiaoping, which opened the country to foreign investment and private enterprise while retaining Communist Party control. Over the following decades, China became the world's manufacturing hub, often called the factory of the world. It is the largest exporter of goods globally and a leader in manufacturing, electronics, steel, and increasingly in electric vehicles, solar technology, and artificial intelligence.

The Belt and Road Initiative, launched in 2013, is a vast infrastructure and investment program spanning Asia, Africa, Europe, and Latin America, intended to expand China's economic and geopolitical influence. China faces structural challenges including high debt, a property sector crisis, and an aging population.

Foreign Policy and Military

China maintains the world's largest standing military by personnel, the People's Liberation Army, and has rapidly modernized its naval and missile forces. It is a permanent member of the United Nations Security Council with veto power and a nuclear-armed state. China claims sovereignty over Taiwan, a self-governing democracy, and asserts expansive territorial claims in the South China Sea, both of which are major sources of regional tension.

China's relationship with the United States is the defining great-power rivalry of the era, encompassing trade, technology, military posture, and competing visions of global order. China is a founding and leading member of the BRICS group and promotes a multipolar world order less dominated by the United States.

Historical Background

Chinese civilization is among the oldest continuous civilizations in the world, with a recorded history spanning several thousand years and a succession of imperial dynasties. The last imperial dynasty, the Qing, fell in 1911. After decades of instability, civil war, and Japanese invasion, the Communist Party under Mao Zedong won the Chinese Civil War and established the People's Republic on October 1, 1949. Mao's rule included the Great Leap Forward and the Cultural Revolution, periods of severe upheaval. The reform era after 1978 set China on the path to becoming a global power.

Contemporary Role

In the 2020s, China stands as the principal challenger to American global primacy and the anchor of an emerging multipolar order. Its economic weight, technological ambition, and military modernization make it central to nearly every major global issue, from climate policy and trade to security in the Indo-Pacific.
