WORLD WAR I: HISTORICAL OVERVIEW

Overview

World War I, also known as the First World War or the Great War, was a global conflict that lasted from 1914 to 1918. It was one of the deadliest conflicts in history, resulting in an estimated nine to ten million military deaths and millions of civilian deaths. The war was fought primarily in Europe but drew in powers and colonies from around the world. It reshaped the political map of Europe and the Middle East, toppled four empires, and set the stage for many of the conflicts of the twentieth century.

Causes

The causes of World War I were complex and long-developing. Historians point to a combination of factors: a system of entangling alliances that divided Europe into two armed camps; intense militarism and an arms race, particularly the naval rivalry between Britain and Germany; aggressive nationalism; and competition for colonies and influence, known as imperialism. Europe was divided between the Triple Alliance of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy, and the Triple Entente of France, Russia, and Britain.

The immediate trigger was the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the throne of Austria-Hungary, in Sarajevo on June 28, 1914, by a Bosnian Serb nationalist named Gavrilo Princip. Austria-Hungary issued an ultimatum to Serbia, and the resulting crisis activated the alliance system, drawing the major powers into war within weeks.

The Major Powers

The conflict was fought between two main coalitions. The Allied Powers, also called the Entente, included France, the British Empire, Russia, Italy, which switched sides in 1915, and later the United States. The Central Powers included Germany, Austria-Hungary, the Ottoman Empire, and Bulgaria.

The Course of the War

On the Western Front, the German advance through Belgium and into France was halted at the First Battle of the Marne in 1914, leading to a long stalemate characterized by trench warfare. Battles such as Verdun and the Somme in 1916 produced enormous casualties for little territorial gain. New weapons, including machine guns, poison gas, tanks, and aircraft, made the war exceptionally lethal.

On the Eastern Front, fighting between Russia and the Central Powers was more mobile but also costly. Russia withdrew from the war following the Russian Revolution of 1917, signing the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk with Germany in 1918.

The United States entered the war in 1917, prompted by German unrestricted submarine warfare and the Zimmermann Telegram. The arrival of American troops and resources tipped the balance against the exhausted Central Powers.

The End of the War

The Central Powers collapsed in 1918. An armistice ending the fighting took effect on the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month, November 11, 1918, a date now commemorated as Armistice Day or Remembrance Day.

The Treaty of Versailles

The peace settlement was negotiated at the Paris Peace Conference in 1919. The Treaty of Versailles, signed in 1919, imposed harsh terms on Germany, including territorial losses, military restrictions, and a requirement to accept responsibility for the war and pay reparations. The treaty's punitive terms are widely seen as having contributed to economic hardship and resentment in Germany that the Nazi Party later exploited.

Consequences

World War I led to the collapse of the German, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, and Ottoman empires. New nations emerged in Central and Eastern Europe and the Middle East. The League of Nations was created in an attempt to prevent future wars. The war's unresolved tensions, economic disruption, and political instability contributed directly to the outbreak of World War II two decades later.
