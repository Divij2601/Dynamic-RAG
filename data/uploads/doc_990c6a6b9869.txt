THE EUROPEAN UNION: ORGANIZATION AND GEOPOLITICAL PROFILE

Overview

The European Union is a political and economic union of member states located primarily in Europe. As of the mid-2020s it comprises twenty-seven member states following the withdrawal of the United Kingdom. The Union represents one of the largest economic blocs in the world and is a unique entity that combines features of an international organization and a federation, with member states pooling sovereignty in specified areas. Its principal institutions are based in Brussels, Belgium, with additional seats in Luxembourg and Strasbourg, France.

Institutions and Governance

The European Union operates through several key institutions. The European Commission is the executive body that proposes legislation and manages day-to-day affairs. The European Parliament, directly elected by citizens of member states, shares legislative authority. The Council of the European Union represents the governments of member states, and the European Council, composed of national heads of state or government, sets overall political direction. The Court of Justice of the European Union ensures uniform interpretation of EU law, and the European Central Bank manages monetary policy for countries using the euro.

The Single Market and the Euro

A central achievement of the European Union is the single market, which allows the free movement of goods, services, capital, and people among member states. The Schengen Area abolishes passport controls at many internal borders. Nineteen member states, known collectively as the eurozone, use the common currency, the euro, which is the second most important reserve currency in the world after the U.S. dollar.

Foreign Policy and Defense

The European Union conducts a common foreign and security policy, though defense remains largely the responsibility of individual member states, many of which are also members of NATO. The Union has used its economic weight as a geopolitical tool, including through sanctions, trade agreements, and regulatory standards that influence global markets, a phenomenon sometimes called the Brussels effect. The Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022 prompted unprecedented coordination among member states on sanctions, energy policy, and military aid.

Historical Background

The origins of the European Union lie in the aftermath of World War II, when European leaders sought to bind former enemies together economically to prevent future conflict. The European Coal and Steel Community, established in 1951, was the first step. The Treaty of Rome in 1957 created the European Economic Community. Successive treaties deepened integration, culminating in the Maastricht Treaty of 1992, which formally established the European Union and laid the groundwork for the single currency. The Union expanded significantly in 2004 with the accession of several Central and Eastern European states that had previously been part of the Soviet bloc.

Brexit

In a 2016 referendum, the United Kingdom voted to leave the European Union, and it formally withdrew in 2020 in a process known as Brexit. This was the first time a member state had left the Union and represented a significant challenge to the project of European integration.

Contemporary Role

In the 2020s, the European Union is a global economic power and a normative actor that promotes democracy, human rights, environmental standards, and the rule of law. It faces challenges including internal political divisions, debates over further enlargement, migration, energy security, and the need to develop a more autonomous defense capability in a more dangerous geopolitical environment.
