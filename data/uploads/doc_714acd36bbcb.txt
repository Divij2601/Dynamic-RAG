GLOBAL AFFAIRS IN 2025 AND 2026: STATE OF THE WORLD

Overview

The mid-2020s have been defined by overlapping conflicts, intensifying great-power competition, and a continued shift toward a multipolar international order. This document summarizes the major geopolitical developments and ongoing conflicts as of 2025 and 2026.

Major World Leaders

As of 2026, the principal world leaders include Donald Trump as President of the United States, having returned to office for a second term; Xi Jinping as the paramount leader of China; Vladimir Putin as President of Russia; and Narendra Modi as Prime Minister of India, who remained among the most popular elected leaders in the world. Charles III serves as the head of state of the United Kingdom and other Commonwealth realms.

The War in Ukraine

The Russia-Ukraine War, which began with Russia's full-scale invasion in February 2022, continued into the mid-2020s as the largest armed conflict in Europe since World War II. By this period, casualties were severe on both sides, with estimates of hundreds of thousands of soldiers killed. Russian forces intensified offensives in the eastern Donetsk region and expanded long-range strikes on Ukrainian cities and energy infrastructure. The war reshaped European security and prompted Finland and Sweden to join NATO.

The Middle East

The war in Gaza, which escalated sharply after October 2023, continued to dominate Middle Eastern affairs. After renewed Israeli operations, a ceasefire was brokered in October 2025, though it left much of Gaza's territory under Israeli control and remained fragile, with continued casualties reported afterward.

A major escalation occurred in June 2025, when Israel and Iran fought a twelve-day war in which the United States joined Israeli operations and struck Iranian underground nuclear facilities. The conflict caused significant casualties, including hundreds of Iranian deaths. Later in 2025, nationwide protests erupted in Iran over high inflation and economic hardship and were met with a severe government crackdown.

Africa

Sudan's civil war, fought between the national army and the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces, deepened into one of the world's gravest humanitarian crises. In October 2025, the Rapid Support Forces captured El Fasher, the army's last major stronghold in western Sudan, deepening the country's de facto partition. The war was estimated to have killed around 400,000 people and displaced roughly eleven million.

Asia and Other Flashpoints

Myanmar's civil war, following the military coup of 2021, became one of the most fragmented conflicts in the world, involving a vast number of armed groups. Border clashes broke out between Thailand and Cambodia and between Afghanistan and Pakistan. An exchange of fire between India and Pakistan was described as the worst in decades, underscoring the persistent danger along their disputed frontier. Tensions over Taiwan and the South China Sea continued to shape security dynamics in the Indo-Pacific.

The Shift Toward Multipolarity

A defining theme of the period was the continued shift away from a unipolar order dominated by the United States toward a more multipolar world. The expansion of the BRICS group, which grew to eleven full members by 2026 with the addition of countries including Indonesia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, Egypt, and Ethiopia, symbolized the growing assertiveness of the Global South and emerging economies. Debates over trade, tariffs, technology competition, and the reform of global institutions reflected the changing distribution of power.

Outlook for 2026

Analysts identified the leading concerns for 2026 as the wars in Ukraine and Gaza, tensions between Israel and Iran, the nuclear program of North Korea, the risk of cyberattacks, and the potential for new or expanded conflicts in the Middle East and elsewhere. The combination of active wars, great-power rivalry, and institutional strain marked the period as one of significant global instability and transition.
